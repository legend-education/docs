# What does Mathematical Practice 3 (MP3) mean?

<div class="answer-box">
  `MP3` says students should build math arguments and pick apart other people's. They state a claim, lay out their reasoning, and use definitions or prior results to support each step. They listen to a classmate, judge whether the argument actually holds, ask clarifying questions, and explain where they agree or disagree. `MP3` is the practice that turns math class into a conversation rather than a worksheet.
</div>

## Official text

Mathematically proficient students understand and use stated assumptions, definitions, and previously established results in constructing arguments. They make conjectures and build a logical progression of statements to explore the truth of their conjectures. They are able to analyze situations by breaking them into cases, and can recognize and use counterexamples. They justify their conclusions, communicate them to others, and respond to the arguments of others. They reason inductively about data, making plausible arguments that take into account the context from which the data arose. Mathematically proficient students are also able to compare the effectiveness of two plausible arguments, distinguish correct logic or reasoning from that which is flawed, and, if there is a flaw in an argument, explain what it is. Elementary students can construct arguments using concrete referents such as objects, drawings, diagrams, and actions. Such arguments can make sense and be correct, even though they are not generalized or made formal until later grades. Later, students learn to determine domains to which an argument applies.

## What students do

- State a claim ("Two odd numbers always add to an even number.").
- Back the claim with reasoning, not just an example.
- Use definitions and previously established results in the argument.
- Listen to a peer's argument, restate it, and judge whether it holds.
- Find a counterexample when a claim is false.

| Grade band | What `MP3` looks like |
| --- | --- |
| K-2 | Showing with cubes or drawings why an addition strategy works, then explaining a classmate's strategy. |
| 3-5 | Justifying why two fractions are equivalent using a picture or by reasoning about the size of the pieces. |
| 6-8 | Writing a brief proof or chain of reasoning, with each step tied to a definition or prior result. |
| 9-12 | Constructing a formal argument, using counterexamples, and identifying flaws in a peer's proof. |

## Classroom application

`MP3` is built through routines that make student thinking public. The most useful are number talks, "convince me," and "agree, disagree, or unsure." Each of these forces students to make a claim and defend it.

Useful sentence stems for `MP3`:

- "I claim that \_\_\_ because \_\_\_."
- "I agree with \_\_\_'s claim because \_\_\_."
- "I disagree because \_\_\_."
- "Can you say more about \_\_\_?"
- "Your argument works for this case. Does it always work?"

Rubric language for `MP3`:

- **Approaching:** Student states an answer but offers only an example as justification.
- **Meets:** Student states a claim, gives reasoning that goes beyond a single example, and uses definitions or prior results.
- **Exceeds:** Student responds to a counterexample by refining the claim or finding the flaw.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Is one example a viable argument?">
    Not on its own. A single example shows that a claim is sometimes true. A viable argument shows that the claim is always true (or, for an existence claim, shows the example as proof). The fix for younger students is to ask, "Does this work for all numbers, or only this one?" That prompt pushes the reasoning from a single case toward a general argument.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="How is critique different from criticism?">
    Critique is the careful judgment of an argument. It can be positive ("Your reasoning works because \_\_\_") or it can name a gap ("This step needs a definition. Which one are you using?"). Criticism, in everyday language, is often a judgment of the person. `MP3` is built when students learn to critique arguments without it landing as a critique of the classmate. Norms like "we critique math, not people" help students cross that bridge.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
