# What does Mathematical Practice 1 (MP1) mean?

<div class="answer-box">
  `MP1` says students should make sense of problems and stick with them. They read the problem carefully, decide what it is asking, plan an approach, and try it. When the first attempt does not work, they back up, look for another path, and try again. Across the K-12 progression, `MP1` is the practice that names productive struggle as part of doing math, not as a sign of failure.
</div>

## Official text

Mathematically proficient students start by explaining to themselves the meaning of a problem and looking for entry points to its solution. They analyze givens, constraints, relationships, and goals. They make conjectures about the form and meaning of the solution and plan a solution pathway rather than simply jumping into a solution attempt. They consider analogous problems and try special cases and simpler forms of the original problem in order to gain insight into its solution. They monitor and evaluate their progress and change course if necessary. They check their answers to problems using a different method, and they continually ask themselves, "Does this make sense?"

## What students do

- Read the problem twice before picking up a pencil.
- Restate the problem in their own words and identify what is being asked.
- Plan a path, then start. They do not just grab numbers and operate.
- Try a simpler case (smaller numbers, a drawing) when stuck.
- Check the answer against the original question, not just the arithmetic.

| Grade band | What `MP1` looks like |
| --- | --- |
| K-2 | Acting the problem out with cubes or counters, retelling the story, and asking, "Does my answer make sense?" |
| 3-5 | Drawing a tape diagram or array, picking an operation that matches, and trying a smaller version of the problem when stuck. |
| 6-8 | Identifying givens, constraints, and goals before writing equations. Trying a special case to test a strategy. |
| 9-12 | Choosing among several solution paths, evaluating which is most efficient, and checking with a second method. |

## Classroom application

`MP1` is the practice teachers see most often, because it is the one that fails first. When a student looks at a word problem and freezes, what is missing is usually `MP1` rather than the underlying content.

A short routine helps build the habit: **read the problem twice with no pencil, restate in your own words, identify what is being asked, then plan**. Many teachers script this on an anchor chart and reference it during launches.

Rubric language for `MP1`:

- **Approaching:** Student starts solving without restating the problem or planning. Stops when stuck.
- **Meets:** Student restates the problem, plans an approach, and tries again when the first attempt does not work.
- **Exceeds:** Student checks the answer against the original question with a second method.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How is MP1 different from problem solving in general?">
    Problem solving is the broader skill. `MP1` is specifically about the metacognitive habits that go with it: restating, planning, monitoring, and checking. A student who solves a problem on the first try by guessing has done problem solving but has not necessarily demonstrated `MP1`. A student who tries a strategy, sees it fail, backs up, and tries another has demonstrated `MP1` even if the final answer is wrong.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Do I have to teach perseverance, or do some students just have it?">
    You have to teach it. Perseverance in math is mostly the result of two things students can be taught: a habit of restating the problem before solving, and the belief that being stuck is a normal step, not a verdict. Naming productive struggle out loud ("We're in the stuck part. That's where the math happens.") and resisting the urge to rescue students too quickly are the two highest-leverage teacher moves.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
