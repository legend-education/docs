# What does Mathematical Practice 7 (MP7) mean?

<div class="answer-box">
  `MP7` says students should notice the structure of math: how an expression is put together, how a number can be decomposed, how a geometric figure can be broken into parts. They see 7 x 8 as 5 x 8 + 2 x 8. They see a quadratic as a difference of squares. Structure is what lets students solve a new problem by treating it as a familiar one in disguise.

## Official text

Mathematically proficient students look closely to discern a pattern or structure. Young students, for example, might notice that three and seven more is the same amount as seven and three more, or they may sort a collection of shapes according to how many sides the shapes have. Later, students will see 7 x 8 equals the well remembered 7 x 5 + 7 x 3, in preparation for learning about the distributive property. In the expression x^2 + 9x + 14, older students can see the 14 as 2 x 7 and the 9 as 2 + 7. They recognize the significance of an existing line in a geometric figure and can use the strategy of drawing an auxiliary line for solving problems. They also can step back for an overview and shift perspective. They can see complicated things, such as some algebraic expressions, as single objects or as being composed of several objects. For example, they can see 5 - 3(x - y)^2 as 5 minus a positive number times a square and use that to realize that its value cannot be more than 5 for any real numbers x and y.

## What students do

- See a number as a sum or product (5 + 2, 7 x 1) and choose the form that helps.
- Split a shape into known shapes (a rectangle and a triangle) to find an area.
- Recognize a familiar form inside a new problem (a difference of squares inside a polynomial).
- Use the properties of operations (commutative, associative, distributive) on purpose, not by accident.
- Step back and see an expression as a single object.

| Grade band | What `MP7` looks like |
| --- | --- |
| K-2 | Seeing 8 as 5 + 3 to add or subtract. Sorting shapes by number of sides. |
| 3-5 | Using the distributive property to break a multiplication problem into easier pieces. |
| 6-8 | Recognizing a linear relationship in a table or a graph. Decomposing a shape to find area or volume. |
| 9-12 | Seeing structure inside an algebraic expression (factor, complete the square, recognize a chunk). |

## Classroom application

`MP7` is built through routines that ask students to notice. Number talks are the clearest example. When a teacher asks, "How did you see 17 + 8?" and students answer "17 + 3 = 20, then + 5 = 25," they are using `MP7`.

A question that works at every grade is, "Can you see this another way?" That single question moves students from a single procedure to noticing structure.

Rubric language for `MP7`:

- **Approaching:** Student treats each problem as a fresh procedure with no use of structure.
- **Meets:** Student decomposes numbers, expressions, or figures to solve more efficiently.
- **Exceeds:** Student names the structure they used and explains why it helped.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How is MP7 different from MP8?">
    `MP7` is about seeing structure inside a single problem (this 7 x 8 can be written as 7 x 5 + 7 x 3). `MP8` is about seeing structure across many problems (whenever I do 1/2 + 1/4, the answer is 3/4, so the pattern must be ___). The two practices reinforce each other and often show up in the same lesson, but it helps to keep them separate when planning. `MP7` looks inside one expression. `MP8` looks across many.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What does MP7 look like in early elementary?">
    It looks like seeing the relationships between numbers. A kindergartner who sees that 8 is made of 5 and 3 is using `MP7`. A first grader who knows that 8 + 5 must equal 13 because 8 + 2 = 10 and 10 + 3 = 13 is using `MP7`. The skill is small at this age but builds the foundation for later algebraic thinking.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
