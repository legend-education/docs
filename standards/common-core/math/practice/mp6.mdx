# What does Mathematical Practice 6 (MP6) mean?

<div class="answer-box">
  `MP6` says students should be careful in the way they talk about and write math. They use definitions accurately, state units, label diagrams, and write equations that say exactly what they mean. Precision is not the same as right answers. A student can get the right answer with sloppy notation; `MP6` is the practice that turns work into something another mathematician can read.
</div>

## Official text

Mathematically proficient students try to communicate precisely to others. They try to use clear definitions in discussion with others and in their own reasoning. They state the meaning of the symbols they choose, including using the equal sign consistently and appropriately. They are careful about specifying units of measure, and labeling axes to clarify the correspondence with quantities in a problem. They calculate accurately and efficiently, express numerical answers with a degree of precision appropriate for the problem context. In the elementary grades, students give carefully formulated explanations to each other. By the time they reach high school they have learned to examine claims and make explicit use of definitions.

## What students do

- Use math vocabulary the way the field uses it ("expression," "equation," "factor," "term").
- State units with every answer that needs them.
- Use the equal sign to mean "is the same as," not "and then."
- Label diagrams, axes, and tables so a reader can follow the work.
- Round to a level of precision that fits the problem (whole minutes, not 11.7 minutes).

| Grade band | What `MP6` looks like |
| --- | --- |
| K-2 | Naming a shape correctly ("triangle," not "pizza slice") and using "more than" and "less than" accurately. |
| 3-5 | Writing equations that record each step (3 x 4 = 12, 12 + 2 = 14) instead of stringing them together (3 x 4 = 12 + 2 = 14). |
| 6-8 | Distinguishing expression and equation, attending to units when working with rates and ratios. |
| 9-12 | Using mathematical definitions exactly, including specifying domain and range, and choosing significant figures that fit the precision of the data. |

## Classroom application

`MP6` is the practice that lives in the small moves. The teacher's pen on student work is most useful when it makes precision visible: circling a missing unit, asking what the equal sign means in a string of equations, or asking why an axis is unlabeled.

A few high-leverage routines for `MP6`:

- Insist on units in every answer with a real-world context.
- Use "always, sometimes, never" prompts to force precise definitions.
- Stop a discussion to ask, "What do we mean by ___?" when a word is being used loosely.

Rubric language for `MP6`:

- **Approaching:** Student arrives at correct answers but uses notation, units, or vocabulary inconsistently.
- **Meets:** Student uses definitions, notation, and units accurately, with answers expressed at an appropriate precision.
- **Exceeds:** Student catches imprecision in others' work and explains the fix.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Is MP6 just about neat handwriting?">
    No. Neat handwriting helps, but `MP6` is about the math itself: definitions, units, the equal sign, and the level of precision that fits the problem. A student with messy handwriting who uses every term correctly is meeting `MP6`. A student with beautiful handwriting who chains together a string of `=` signs is not.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How precise is precise enough?">
    Precision is set by the problem, not by the page. An answer about how many buses are needed should be rounded up to a whole number, because half a bus is not useful. An answer about a chemistry concentration may need three significant figures. `MP6` is met when students choose precision that fits the situation rather than copying every digit from the calculator.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
