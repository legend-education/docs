# What does Mathematical Practice 4 (MP4) mean?

<div class="answer-box">
  `MP4` says students should use math to describe and reason about the real world. They take a real situation (a school dance budget, the height of a thrown ball, the spread of a rumor), decide what matters, and write the math that captures it. They check whether their model fits the situation, accept that simplifications are part of modeling, and revise when their model misses something important.

## Official text

Mathematically proficient students can apply the mathematics they know to solve problems arising in everyday life, society, and the workplace. In early grades, this might be as simple as writing an addition equation to describe a situation. In middle grades, a student might apply proportional reasoning to plan a school event or analyze a problem in the community. By high school, a student might use geometry to solve a design problem or use a function to describe how one quantity of interest depends on another. Mathematically proficient students who can apply what they know are comfortable making assumptions and approximations to simplify a complicated situation, realizing that these may need revision later. They are able to identify important quantities in a practical situation and map their relationships using such tools as diagrams, two-way tables, graphs, flowcharts, and formulas. They can analyze those relationships mathematically to draw conclusions. They routinely interpret their mathematical results in the context of the situation and reflect on whether the results make sense, possibly improving the model if it has not served its purpose.

## What students do

- Read a real situation and identify the quantities that matter.
- Choose a representation (equation, graph, diagram, table) that captures the relationships.
- Make assumptions explicit ("I am assuming the price stays the same all year.").
- Use the model to answer the question.
- Check the answer against the situation and revise the model if needed.

| Grade band | What `MP4` looks like |
| --- | --- |
| K-2 | Writing a number sentence that matches a story: "There were 6 ducks. 2 flew away. 6 - 2 = 4." |
| 3-5 | Choosing whether a situation calls for a tape diagram, a bar graph, or an equation. |
| 6-8 | Setting up a proportional or linear model for a context, naming the meaning of slope and intercept. |
| 9-12 | Picking a function family that fits a phenomenon and revising the model when residuals show a pattern. |

## Classroom application

`MP4` is the practice most associated with modeling tasks and performance assessments. It is also the practice most likely to feel slow in a classroom that runs on procedural fluency, because modeling intentionally trades speed for fit-to-reality.

A short modeling cycle students can run:

1. Read the situation and name the question.
2. List the quantities that matter and the assumptions you are making.
3. Choose a representation and build the model.
4. Use the model to answer the question.
5. Check the answer. Revise the model if it does not fit.

Rubric language for `MP4`:

- **Approaching:** Student computes without identifying what the numbers represent or whether the result fits the situation.
- **Meets:** Student builds a model that captures the key quantities and uses it to answer the question.
- **Exceeds:** Student names assumptions, checks the model against the situation, and revises when needed.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Is every word problem a modeling task?">
    No. Many word problems are dressed-up exercises with a single correct setup. A modeling task gives students room to choose what to count, what to ignore, and which representation fits. The two are useful at different times. Routine word problems build fluency. True modeling tasks build `MP4` and usually take a full class period or longer.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How is MP4 different from MP2?">
    `MP2` is about moving between symbols and situations within one problem. `MP4` is about building the model in the first place. A student who is given an equation and asked to interpret it is working on `MP2`. A student who is given a real situation and asked to build the equation is working on `MP4`.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
