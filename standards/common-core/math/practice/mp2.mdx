# What does Mathematical Practice 2 (MP2) mean?

<div class="answer-box">
  `MP2` says students should move comfortably between two ways of thinking. They take a real situation and represent it with numbers and symbols (decontextualize), and they take an equation and explain what it means back in the situation (contextualize). The number sentence and the word problem are two views of the same thing. Strong math thinkers can move from one to the other and check that the two views agree.
</div>

## Official text

Mathematically proficient students make sense of quantities and their relationships in problem situations. They bring two complementary abilities to bear on problems involving quantitative relationships: the ability to decontextualize, to abstract a given situation and represent it symbolically and manipulate the representing symbols as if they have a life of their own, without necessarily attending to their referents, and the ability to contextualize, to pause as needed during the manipulation process in order to probe into the referents for the symbols involved. Quantitative reasoning entails habits of creating a coherent representation of the problem at hand; considering the units involved; attending to the meaning of quantities, not just how to compute them; and knowing and using different properties of operations and objects.

## What students do

- Write an equation that captures the relationships in a word problem.
- Explain what each variable or number stands for in the situation.
- Pause inside a calculation and ask, "What would this number mean back in the story?"
- Use units to check that an answer makes sense (10 cars, not 10 minutes).
- Treat a symbol like `n` as a stand-in for a quantity, not a magic letter.

| Grade band | What `MP2` looks like |
| --- | --- |
| K-2 | Telling a story that matches a number sentence (5 \+ 3 = 8 because three more cats came). |
| 3-5 | Writing an equation with a variable for the unknown, then explaining what the variable represents. |
| 6-8 | Setting up a proportion or equation from a context and naming what each ratio or term means. |
| 9-12 | Choosing variables, units, and scales when modeling, and checking that the symbolic answer makes sense in the situation. |

## Classroom application

`MP2` shows up every time a word problem appears. The most common student error is not arithmetic. It is jumping straight to numbers without naming what they mean.

A simple routine: before solving, students label each quantity ("4 is the cost per pound. 12 is the number of pounds."). After solving, they read the answer back in a sentence using the units from the problem.

Rubric language for `MP2`:

- **Approaching:** Student manipulates numbers without explaining what they represent in the situation.
- **Meets:** Student translates the problem into symbols and back into words, with consistent units.
- **Exceeds:** Student uses the structure of the situation to predict how the answer should behave, then confirms it.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="What is the difference between abstract and quantitative reasoning?">
    Quantitative reasoning sticks with the situation. It cares about pounds, dollars, and people. Abstract reasoning treats the symbols as objects that can be manipulated by the rules of arithmetic and algebra. Strong math thinkers can do both and can move between the two views during one problem. `MP2` is met when a student can decontextualize the problem, work in the symbol world, then contextualize the answer back into the situation.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Why does MP2 keep showing up on assessment rubrics?">
    Most state and curriculum-built rubrics include a column for explaining the meaning of an answer, not just computing it. That column is `MP2` in plain clothing. The standardized test items that ask "What does the 0.5 represent in the context of the problem?" are direct measures of `MP2`.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
