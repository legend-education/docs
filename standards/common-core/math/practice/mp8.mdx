# What does Mathematical Practice 8 (MP8) mean?

<div class="answer-box">
  `MP8` says students should notice when they are doing the same thing over and over and turn that pattern into a rule. After solving a handful of problems, they pause and ask, "What is repeating here? Can I write a shortcut?" The rules students discover this way include "any number times zero is zero," "to divide by a fraction, multiply by its reciprocal," and "the slope is the same between any two points on a line." `MP8` is how informal patterns become general statements.

## Official text

Mathematically proficient students notice if calculations are repeated, and look both for general methods and for shortcuts. Upper elementary students might notice when dividing 25 by 11 that they are repeating the same calculations over and over again, and conclude they have a repeating decimal. By paying attention to the calculation of slope as they repeatedly check whether points are on the line through (1, 2) with slope 3, middle school students might abstract the equation (y - 2)/(x - 1) = 3. Noticing the regularity in the way terms cancel when expanding (x - 1)(x \+ 1), (x - 1)(x^2 \+ x \+ 1), and (x - 1)(x^3 \+ x^2 \+ x \+ 1) might lead them to the general formula for the sum of a geometric series. As they work to solve a problem, mathematically proficient students maintain oversight of the process, while attending to the details. They continually evaluate the reasonableness of their intermediate results.

## What students do

- Work through several similar problems and notice what stays the same.
- State the pattern in words first, then in symbols.
- Test the pattern on a new case to see whether it holds.
- Use the pattern as a shortcut, with the understanding that it can be checked against the long way.

| Grade band | What `MP8` looks like |
| --- | --- |
| K-2 | Noticing that 3 \+ 4 and 4 \+ 3 give the same answer, and using that to skip a recalculation. |
| 3-5 | Noticing that multiplying any number by 10 puts a zero on the end (and explaining why in terms of place value). |
| 6-8 | Noticing that the slope between any two points on a line is the same, and writing that as an equation. |
| 9-12 | Noticing that certain expansions follow a predictable pattern and writing a general formula. |

## Classroom application

`MP8` is the practice that powers good rule-noticing. Avoid handing students a rule and asking them to apply it. Instead, give them four or five worked examples and ask, "What do you notice?" The rule emerges from the data the students themselves built.

A useful template: after a set of problems, ask "What was the same in every problem? What was different? What rule could we write that captures the same part?"

Rubric language for `MP8`:

- **Approaching:** Student treats each problem in a set as a brand-new calculation, even when the pattern is clear.
- **Meets:** Student notices the pattern across problems and uses it as a shortcut.
- **Exceeds:** Student writes a general rule, tests it on a new case, and explains why it must hold.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Should I let students discover a rule, or just tell them?">
    Both have a place. For rules that students will own and use across grades (the distributive property, the slope formula, the order of operations), discovery through `MP8` makes the rule stick. For shortcuts that are useful but not foundational, telling and then having students verify is fine. The rule of thumb is that anything important enough to live on an anchor chart is worth discovering.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="How is MP8 different from MP7?">
    `MP7` is about structure inside a single problem. `MP8` is about regularity across many problems. A student who sees 7 x 8 as 7 x 5 \+ 7 x 3 is using `MP7`. A student who solves several problems and notices "the second number always ends up doubled" is using `MP8`. The two often work together. `MP7` is one piece of the noticing; `MP8` is the part that generalizes across many pieces.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

Students notice when calculations or steps repeat across problems, name the pattern in words, write it in symbols, and use it as a general method. `MP8` powers the discovery of rules such as the slope formula, the distributive property, and patterns in polynomial expansion.
