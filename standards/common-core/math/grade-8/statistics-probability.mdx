# What are the Common Core Statistics & Probability standards for Grade 8?

<div class="answer-box">
  Statistics and Probability in grade 8 (`8.SP`) is one cluster: investigating patterns of association in bivariate data. Students construct and interpret scatter plots (`8.SP.A.1`), fit a straight line informally to scatter plots that suggest a linear association (`8.SP.A.2`), use the equation of a linear model to solve problems and interpret slope and intercept in context (`8.SP.A.3`), and display and interpret bivariate categorical data in a two-way table (`8.SP.A.4`).
</div>

## Classroom application

`8.SP.A` is short but rich. Students take two measurements on each subject (height and arm span, hours of sleep and test score, ice cream sales and temperature) and plot the pairs as points on a scatter plot. The visual pattern in the cloud of points is the data's signature. A positive linear association means the cloud rises from lower-left to upper-right. A negative linear association rises from upper-left to lower-right. Clustering, outliers, and nonlinear shapes are described in plain words.

`8.SP.A.2` introduces line-of-best-fit informally. Students do not compute a regression line; they draw a line that "fits" the cloud by eye, then check that the points are roughly evenly distributed on both sides. The criterion is closeness of points to the line and balance above and below. A useful classroom move is to have multiple students draw their own line on the same scatter plot, then compare; the differences highlight that "fitting a line" is a judgment, not a unique answer.

`8.SP.A.3` connects to `8.F.B.4`. Students find the slope and intercept of their fitted line, then interpret each in context. If the line for hours-studied versus test-score has slope 4, an additional hour of study is associated with 4 more points on the test. The slope's units are "points per hour." Interpreting slope and intercept in real units is the heart of statistical reasoning at this grade.

`8.SP.A.4` is the categorical-data parallel. Two-way tables organize counts by two categorical variables (curfew yes/no by chores yes/no). Relative frequencies (proportions within a row or column) reveal whether the variables are associated. If 80 percent of curfew-yes students also have chores, but only 30 percent of curfew-no students have chores, the two variables are associated.
