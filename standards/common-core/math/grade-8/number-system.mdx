# What are the Common Core The Number System standards for Grade 8?

<div class="answer-box">
  The Number System in grade 8 (`8.NS`) is a small but important domain. Students learn that numbers not expressible as a fraction are irrational (`8.NS.A.1`), that every number has a decimal expansion (terminating, repeating, or non-repeating non-terminating), and that they can locate irrational numbers like sqrt(2) and pi approximately on a number line by truncating their decimal expansion (`8.NS.A.2`). This is the first formal encounter with irrational numbers.

## What it covers

- Know that there are numbers that are not rational, and approximate them by rational numbers (`8.NS.A`).

## Classroom application

The work in `8.NS` is short but conceptually heavy. Students have spent K-7 working only with rational numbers. This year, they meet numbers that cannot be written as a fraction. The high-leverage starting move is a long-division demonstration: 1/3 produces 0.333... forever; 1/7 produces 0.142857142857... with a six-digit repeat; 1/8 produces 0.125 and stops. Every fraction's decimal expansion either terminates or eventually repeats. That is the rational-number signature.

`8.NS.A.1` also asks students to convert a repeating decimal to a fraction. The standard algebraic move is: let x = 0.7777..., then 10x = 7.7777..., so 9x = 7, giving x = 7/9. Practicing this with several examples makes the repeating-equals-rational direction concrete.

For `8.NS.A.2`, the placement of sqrt(2) on a number line is the canonical task. By squaring nearby numbers, students show 1.4 squared is 1.96 (too small) and 1.5 squared is 2.25 (too big), so sqrt(2) is between 1.4 and 1.5. Continuing gives 1.41 squared is 1.9881 and 1.42 squared is 2.0164, so sqrt(2) is between 1.41 and 1.42. The truncation process makes the irrational number tangible without computing forever.

A useful display is a number line showing sqrt(2), sqrt(3), pi, and a few rational benchmarks (1, 1.5, 2, 3). Students estimate each irrational number's position by squaring to compare. The work connects directly to the Pythagorean theorem in `8.G.B`, where sqrt(2) appears as the hypotenuse of a unit-length right triangle.
