# What are the Common Core Math standards for Grade 8?

<div class="answer-box">
  Grade 8 is the bridge year to high school math. The Functions domain (`8.F`) is brand new and introduces the formal idea of a function and the equation `y = mx + b`. Expressions and Equations (`8.EE`) covers linear equations in one and two variables, including systems. The Pythagorean theorem enters in Geometry (`8.G`), along with congruence and similarity through transformations. The Number System (`8.NS`) extends to irrational numbers. Statistics adds bivariate data and the line of best fit.

## What it covers

- The Number System (`8.NS`): rational and irrational numbers and the approximation of irrationals on a number line.
- Expressions and Equations (`8.EE`): integer exponents, square and cube roots, scientific notation, linear equations in one and two variables, and systems of linear equations.
- Functions (`8.F`): the definition of a function, comparing functions in different representations, modeling with linear functions, and describing qualitative graphs.
- Geometry (`8.G`): congruence and similarity through transformations, angle relationships, the Pythagorean theorem, and volume of cones, cylinders, and spheres.
- Statistics and Probability (`8.SP`): scatter plots, the line of best fit, and two-way frequency tables.

## What changes this year

Grade 8 is when the function concept arrives in formal language. A function (`8.F.A.1`) is a rule that assigns exactly one output to each input. Students see functions in graphs, tables, equations, and verbal descriptions, and compare them across representations (`8.F.A.2`). The equation `y = mx + b` enters here as the linear function with slope `m` and y-intercept `b` (`8.F.A.3`, `8.F.B.4`).

Expressions and Equations builds on grade 7 to cover linear equations in two variables and systems of two linear equations (`8.EE.C.8`). Students solve simultaneous equations algebraically and by graphing, and they relate the algebraic solution to the intersection of the two lines.

Geometry shifts from measurement to transformations. Students develop congruence through a sequence of translations, reflections, and rotations (`8.G.A.2`) and similarity through transformations including dilations (`8.G.A.4`). The Pythagorean theorem enters (`8.G.B.6`, `8.G.B.7`, `8.G.B.8`), with proof, application, and use on the coordinate plane.

The Number System extends to irrational numbers (`8.NS.A.1`). Students approximate irrationals on a number line and use rational approximations in real situations.

Statistics adds bivariate data. Students construct scatter plots, identify patterns of association, and use the line of best fit to model linear relationships (`8.SP.A.1`, `8.SP.A.2`, `8.SP.A.3`).

## Classroom application

The major work of grade 8 is linear functions and equations (`8.F`, `8.EE`) and the Pythagorean theorem (`8.G.B`). Schedule the year so these three areas carry the bulk of instructional time. Geometry transformations support the similarity work that students will use again in high school.

For linear functions, teach `y = mx + b` as the equation of a line and as the rule for a function in one breath. A graph, a table, a context, and the equation are four views of the same relationship. The teacher move that builds the most flexibility is asking students to start from any one of the four and generate the other three.

For the Pythagorean theorem, the proof by rearrangement is worth a full lesson. Students cut a square into pieces and rearrange them to show that the area of the square on the hypotenuse equals the sum of the areas of the squares on the two legs. The proof builds `MP3` and `MP7` and makes the theorem feel inevitable rather than memorized.

Transformations work in `8.G.A` is best taught with patty paper or geometry software. Students physically move shapes, then describe the moves with coordinates and rules. The shift from "I moved it" to "I translated it by the vector (3, -2)" is the language work that supports formal geometry in high school.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Is grade 8 the same as Algebra I?">
    No, though they overlap. Grade 8 covers a focused subset of algebra: linear functions, linear equations, and systems. A full Algebra I course covers all of grade 8 plus quadratic functions, exponential functions, factoring, and more advanced equation solving. Some districts compress grade 8 and Algebra I into a single accelerated middle school year. The standards do not require this and call out the trade-offs of acceleration. Done well, the standard sequence is grade 8 in middle school followed by Algebra I in grade 9.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Why is the Pythagorean theorem in grade 8 and not earlier?">
    The theorem depends on knowing about square roots (`8.EE.A.2`) and irrational numbers (`8.NS.A.1`), both of which arrive in grade 8. Earlier grades work with the side lengths of right triangles but do not have the number system needed to handle the irrational results that come from many right triangles (the diagonal of a 1-by-1 square is square root of 2). The grade 8 placement is intentional and aligns with the algebra students need for the formula to make sense.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
