# What are the Common Core Geometry standards for Grade 8?

<div class="answer-box">
  Geometry in grade 8 (`8.G`) introduces transformations and the Pythagorean theorem. Students verify experimentally the properties of rotations, reflections, and translations (`8.G.A.1`), define congruence and similarity through sequences of transformations (`8.G.A.2`, `8.G.A.4`), explain a proof of the Pythagorean theorem and apply it in problems (`8.G.B`), and find volumes of cones, cylinders, and spheres (`8.G.C`).
</div>

## What it covers

- Understand congruence and similarity using physical models, transparencies, or geometry software (`8.G.A`).
- Understand and apply the Pythagorean theorem (`8.G.B`).
- Solve real-world and mathematical problems involving volume of cylinders, cones, and spheres (`8.G.C`).

## Classroom application

`8.G.A` is the transformations cluster. The introduction is hands-on: tracing paper, geometry software like GeoGebra or Desmos, or coordinate grids. Students take a figure and a transformation, perform the transformation, and observe what is preserved. Lengths and angles stay the same under rotations, reflections, and translations. Parallel lines stay parallel. The figure stays congruent to the original.

`8.G.A.2` reframes congruence: two figures are congruent if and only if there is a sequence of rigid motions mapping one to the other. The classroom task is to give students two congruent figures on a coordinate grid and ask them to describe a sequence (translate, then reflect, then rotate) that exhibits the congruence. There is often more than one valid sequence.

`8.G.A.3` adds coordinates. A reflection over the x-axis maps (x, y) to (x, -y). A 90-degree rotation about the origin maps (x, y) to (-y, x). A dilation by scale factor k about the origin maps (x, y) to (kx, ky). The coordinate description makes transformations precise and testable.

`8.G.A.4` extends to similarity: a sequence that includes a dilation maps any figure to one similar to it. Together with `8.G.A.5`, this gives the angle-angle criterion: if two triangles have two pairs of congruent angles, they are similar.

`8.G.B.6` asks for a proof of the Pythagorean theorem. The standard does not require any one proof; teachers should pick an accessible one. The classic "squares on the sides of a right triangle" demonstration, with rearrangement of four congruent right triangles inside a larger square, is concrete and convincing for grade-8 students.

`8.G.B.7` is the application. Given a right triangle with legs 3 and 4, the hypotenuse is sqrt(3 squared + 4 squared) = sqrt(25) = 5. Three-dimensional applications include finding the diagonal of a rectangular box. `8.G.B.8` is the coordinate-distance application: the distance from (1, 2) to (4, 6) is sqrt((4-1) squared + (6-2) squared) = sqrt(9 + 16) = sqrt(25) = 5.

`8.G.C.9` adds three new volume formulas to the prism formula students already know. A cone has volume (1/3)(pi)(r squared)(h). A cylinder has volume (pi)(r squared)(h). A sphere has volume (4/3)(pi)(r cubed). The 1/3 in the cone formula and the 4/3 in the sphere formula are worth pausing on; the calculus that justifies them comes later, but the formulas should not feel arbitrary.
