# What are the Common Core Math standards for Grade 5?

<div class="answer-box">
  Grade 5 is the year students reach fluency with operations on fractions and decimals. They add and subtract fractions with unlike denominators (`5.NF.A.1`), multiply and divide fractions in limited cases (`5.NF.B`), and operate on decimals to the hundredths (`5.NBT.B.7`). They reach the standard algorithm for multi-digit multiplication (`5.NBT.B.5`). Volume of a right rectangular prism enters as a new geometric idea (`5.MD.C`). This is the last elementary grade before the ratio and proportion work of grade 6.
</div>

## What it covers

- Operations and Algebraic Thinking (`5.OA`): writing and interpreting numerical expressions, and analyzing patterns and relationships.
- Number and Operations in Base Ten (`5.NBT`): place value with decimals, the standard algorithm for multi-digit multiplication, division with two-digit divisors, and decimal operations.
- Number and Operations: Fractions (`5.NF`): addition and subtraction of fractions with unlike denominators, multiplication of fractions, and division of unit fractions by whole numbers and the reverse.
- Measurement and Data (`5.MD`): unit conversion, line plots with fractions, and volume of right rectangular prisms.
- Geometry (`5.G`): the coordinate plane and classifying two-dimensional figures by attributes.

## What changes this year

Grade 5 is the capstone of elementary arithmetic. Students reach the standard algorithm for multi-digit multiplication (`5.NBT.B.5`) and divide whole numbers by two-digit divisors (`5.NBT.B.6`). Decimals get full treatment: place value to the thousandths (`5.NBT.A.3`), reading and writing decimals, and the four operations on decimals to the hundredths (`5.NBT.B.7`).

Fraction operations come together. Students add and subtract fractions with unlike denominators by finding a common denominator (`5.NF.A.1`). They multiply a fraction by a fraction using the meaning of multiplication and area models (`5.NF.B.4`). They interpret division of a unit fraction by a whole number and a whole number by a unit fraction (`5.NF.B.7`). Note that full division of a fraction by a fraction waits until grade 6 (`6.NS.A.1`).

The coordinate plane enters as a new tool (`5.G.A.1`, `5.G.A.2`). Students plot points in the first quadrant and use them to represent real-world relationships, which sets up the function thinking of grade 6 and beyond.

Volume of a right rectangular prism (`5.MD.C.3`, `5.MD.C.4`, `5.MD.C.5`) extends the area thinking from grade 3 into three dimensions. Students count unit cubes, then connect that count to the multiplication of three side lengths.

## Classroom application

The two big rocks of the grade 5 year are decimal arithmetic (`5.NBT.B.7`) and fraction operations (`5.NF`). Most state tests draw heavily from both. Schedule the year so that fractions get four to six weeks rather than a single short unit; this is the last year fractions are the focus before they become the building blocks of ratio and proportional thinking in grade 6.

For decimal arithmetic, place value charts, base-ten blocks, and money are the most useful representations. A student who can write 2.36 as 2 + 0.3 + 0.06 has the place value understanding the algorithms depend on. Adding 2.36 and 1.7 by lining up the decimal points (and only the decimal points) is a `MP6` move worth teaching explicitly.

For fraction multiplication, the area model carries the lesson. A 2/3 by 4/5 rectangle inside a unit square has 8 of the 15 small parts shaded. The model makes 2/3 x 4/5 = 8/15 visible. Without the model, fraction multiplication often becomes a memorized rule with no meaning.

Volume work in `5.MD.C` is best taught with unit cubes. Students build a prism, count the cubes, then connect the count to length x width x height. Connecting count to formula is `MP8` (express regularity in repeated reasoning).

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Do students divide fractions in grade 5?">
    Only in limited cases. `5.NF.B.7` covers division of a unit fraction by a whole number (1/3 divided by 4) and a whole number by a unit fraction (3 divided by 1/4). General fraction-by-fraction division is a grade 6 standard (`6.NS.A.1`). The reason for the limit is that the grade 5 cases can be understood as repeated subtraction or as the meaning of division, while the general case usually requires the invert-and-multiply rule that builds on grade 6 reasoning.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Why is the coordinate plane only the first quadrant in grade 5?">
    `5.G.A.1` and `5.G.A.2` are explicit that grade 5 uses only the first quadrant. Negative numbers and all four quadrants enter in grade 6 (`6.NS.C.6`, `6.NS.C.8`). The grade 5 work sets up the structure of the coordinate plane (axes, origin, ordered pairs) using only positive numbers so students can focus on the structure before the sign work.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
