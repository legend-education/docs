# What are the Common Core Measurement & Data standards for Grade 5?

<div class="answer-box">
  Measurement and Data in grade 5 (`5.MD`) is anchored by volume. Students convert between units within a system (`5.MD.A.1`), make line plots from fractional measurements and apply fraction operations to the data (`5.MD.B.2`), and introduce volume as the count of unit cubes in a right rectangular prism (`5.MD.C.3` to `5.MD.C.5`). Volume connects to the multiplication of three side lengths and to the additivity that students used for area in grade 3.

## What it covers

- Convert like measurement units within a given measurement system (`5.MD.A`).
- Represent and interpret data (`5.MD.B`).
- Geometric measurement: understand concepts of volume and relate volume to multiplication and to addition (`5.MD.C`).

  <Accordion title="5.MD.C.3 sub-points: volume as a count of unit cubes">
    - **5.MD.C.3.a:** A cube with side length 1 unit, called a "unit cube," is said to have "one cubic unit" of volume, and can be used to measure volume.
    - **5.MD.C.3.b:** A solid figure which can be packed without gaps or overlaps using n unit cubes is said to have a volume of n cubic units.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="5.MD.C.5 sub-points: volume, multiplication, and addition">
    - **5.MD.C.5.a:** Find the volume of a right rectangular prism with whole-number side lengths by packing it with unit cubes, and show that the volume is the same as would be found by multiplying the edge lengths, equivalently by multiplying the height by the area of the base. Represent threefold whole-number products as volumes, e.g., to represent the associative property of multiplication.
    - **5.MD.C.5.b:** Apply the formulas V = l x w x h and V = b x h for rectangular prisms to find volumes of right rectangular prisms with whole-number edge lengths in the context of solving real world and mathematical problems.
    - **5.MD.C.5.c:** Recognize volume as additive. Find volumes of solid figures composed of two non-overlapping right rectangular prisms by adding the volumes of the non-overlapping parts, applying this technique to solve real world problems.

## Classroom application

The volume work in `5.MD.C` should follow the same teach-it-twice pattern as grade 3 area. First students pack a prism with unit cubes and count, then they connect the count to length x width x height, then they apply the formula. Students who jump straight to the formula often fail to see that volume is a count of cubes, which makes irregular-shape volume problems (`5.MD.C.5.c`) harder than they need to be.

The connection between volume and area (volume = area of base x height) is one of the most useful insights in grade 5 geometry. It generalizes to any prism (a triangular prism's volume is the area of the triangular base times the height) and previews high school work with volume of more complex solids.

`5.MD.A.1` builds on grade 4 conversions but adds the metric system's nice connection to decimals. 5 cm = 0.05 m is a place value fact: cm and m are 100 apart, so the digit shifts two places. This connects directly to `5.NBT.A.2` (powers of 10) and to the decimal arithmetic of `5.NBT.B.7`.

The line plot work in `5.MD.B.2` lets fraction operations show up in a measurement context. A line plot of liquid amounts (1/4, 1/2, 1/4, 3/4, 1/2) becomes a problem about adding the fractions, then dividing by the number of measurements. The example in the standard (redistribute the liquid equally) is the canonical task type.
