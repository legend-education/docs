# What are the Common Core Number & Operations in Base Ten standards for Grade 5?

<div class="answer-box">
  Number and Operations in Base Ten in grade 5 (`5.NBT`) is the capstone of elementary base-ten arithmetic. Students see that a digit represents 10 times what it would in the place to its right and 1/10 of what it would in the place to its left (`5.NBT.A.1`), read and compare decimals to thousandths (`5.NBT.A.3`), and round decimals to any place (`5.NBT.A.4`). They reach the standard algorithm for multi-digit multiplication (`5.NBT.B.5`), divide with two-digit divisors (`5.NBT.B.6`), and add, subtract, multiply, and divide decimals to hundredths (`5.NBT.B.7`).

## What it covers

- Understand the place value system (`5.NBT.A`).
- Perform operations with multi-digit whole numbers and with decimals to hundredths (`5.NBT.B`).

## Classroom application

`5.NBT.A.1` extends grade 4's "ten times" relationship to "ten times" and "one-tenth." The new direction is the more important one for grade 5, because it sets up decimals as a continuation of the place value system to the right of the ones place. Place value charts that include hundredths and thousandths make this concrete.

Decimal arithmetic (`5.NBT.B.7`) is the heaviest content of the year. Each operation rests on a different big idea. Addition and subtraction work by lining up place values (the standard is explicit that the decimal point is what you line up). Multiplication works by counting the total decimal places in the factors. Division by a decimal is harder and is usually taught by scaling both the dividend and divisor by the same power of 10. The standard does not require the standard algorithm for decimal operations (that is `6.NS.B.3`); place value strategies are sufficient.

For `5.NBT.B.5`, the standard algorithm for multi-digit multiplication arrives this year. Connect it to the grade 4 area model that students already know. Each row of the standard algorithm is one of the partial products from the area model.

`5.NBT.B.6` extends division to two-digit divisors. Partial-quotient strategies work well here. A problem like 4032 / 24 becomes "How many 24s fit?" with students testing 100 x 24 = 2400, then more 24s in the remaining 1632.
