# What are the Common Core Geometry standards for Grade 5?

<div class="answer-box">
  Geometry in grade 5 (`5.G`) introduces the coordinate plane and shape hierarchy. Students use perpendicular axes to define a coordinate system (`5.G.A.1`), graph points in the first quadrant to represent real-world relationships (`5.G.A.2`), and reason about shape hierarchy (`5.G.B.3`, `5.G.B.4`), recognizing that attributes of a category belong to all its subcategories.

## What it covers

- Graph points on the coordinate plane to solve real-world and mathematical problems (`5.G.A`).
- Classify two-dimensional figures into categories based on their properties (`5.G.B`).

## Classroom application

`5.G.A.1` introduces the coordinate plane carefully. The standard explicitly notes that the first number tells how far to travel along the x-axis and the second how far along the y-axis. The convention is set here so that grade 6 work in all four quadrants and grade 8 graphing of linear functions can build on a sturdy base. Grade 5 stays in the first quadrant only.

For `5.G.A.2`, the typical task is a real situation that maps to ordered pairs. Time and distance, hours worked and money earned, day of the week and number of pages read. Plotting the pairs shows a relationship between the two quantities. The standard does not name linear or proportional relationships, but the work foreshadows both.

The shape hierarchy in `5.G.B.3` and `5.G.B.4` is the year's most demanding logical work. A square is a rectangle (because it has four right angles and opposite sides parallel and equal). A rectangle is a parallelogram. A parallelogram is a quadrilateral. The hierarchy goes from specific to general, and an attribute that belongs to a category belongs to all of its subcategories. This is the foundation of the deductive reasoning students will use in high school geometry.

A Venn diagram or nested-categories diagram makes the hierarchy visible. Quadrilaterals contain parallelograms; parallelograms contain rectangles and rhombuses; squares sit in the intersection of rectangles and rhombuses.
