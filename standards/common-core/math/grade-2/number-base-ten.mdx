# What are the Common Core Number & Operations in Base Ten standards for Grade 2?

<div class="answer-box">
  Number and Operations in Base Ten in grade 2 (`2.NBT`) is the major work of the year. Students see three-digit numbers as hundreds, tens, and ones (`2.NBT.A.1`), count and skip-count to 1000, read and write numbers to 1000 in three forms (`2.NBT.A.3`), and compare them using `<`, `>`, and `=`. They reach fluency with addition and subtraction within 100 (`2.NBT.B.5`), add and subtract within 1000 using place value strategies (`2.NBT.B.7`), and explain why the strategies work (`2.NBT.B.9`).
</div>

## What it covers

- Understand place value (`2.NBT.A`).
- Use place value understanding and properties of operations to add and subtract (`2.NBT.B`).

## Classroom application

The expanded form work in `2.NBT.A.3` is where place value gets sturdy. Students see 358 as 300 + 50 + 8, then build that number with base-ten blocks (3 flats, 5 rods, 8 cubes). The two representations together make place value a tool rather than a label.

For `2.NBT.B.5`, place value strategies should come before the standard algorithm. A useful sequence: open number line, then place value drawings (3 hundreds and 5 tens and 8 ones), then a written method that records what was actually done. The standard algorithm shows up later in grade 4 (`4.NBT.B.4`).

The "explain why" piece of `2.NBT.B.9` is the one that often gets cut for time. Do not cut it. A student who can explain why 47 - 28 worked the way they did is one who can adapt to 470 - 280 or 4.7 - 2.8 later. The explanations are short (a sentence or two) and can live alongside the work rather than in a separate writing block.

`2.NBT.B.8` is small but worth a daily routine. The class chants "10 more, 10 less, 100 more, 100 less" around a target number. Across a year, that routine builds the place value patterns that grade 3 mental math depends on.
