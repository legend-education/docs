# What are the Common Core Math standards for Grade 2?

<div class="answer-box">
  Grade 2 is the year place value becomes the spine of arithmetic. Students read, write, and compare numbers to 1000 in terms of hundreds, tens, and ones (`2.NBT.A.1`). They fluently add and subtract within 100 (`2.NBT.B.5`) and reach mastery of basic addition and subtraction facts within 20 (`2.OA.B.2`). They also measure length in standard units, tell time to the nearest 5 minutes, work with money, and start counting equal groups, which sets up grade 3 multiplication.

## What it covers

- Operations and Algebraic Thinking (`2.OA`): one-step and two-step word problems within 100, fact fluency within 20, and the start of equal-groups thinking.
- Number and Operations in Base Ten (`2.NBT`): place value to 1000, comparing numbers, adding and subtracting within 1000, and skip counting.
- Measurement and Data (`2.MD`): length in standard units, time to the nearest 5 minutes, money, and line plots.
- Geometry (`2.G`): identifying shapes by attributes, partitioning into halves, thirds, and fourths, and finding the area as a count of unit squares.

## What changes this year

Grade 2 is the year three-digit numbers start to behave. Students see 358 as 3 hundreds, 5 tens, and 8 ones (`2.NBT.A.1`), read and write numbers to 1000 in three forms (`2.NBT.A.3`), and compare them using place value reasoning (`2.NBT.A.4`).

Addition and subtraction fluency takes a major step. Students reach fluency within 100 using a written strategy (`2.NBT.B.5`), add and subtract within 1000 using place value strategies (`2.NBT.B.7`), and know all sums of two one-digit numbers from memory by the end of the year (`2.OA.B.2`).

Two-step word problems appear for the first time (`2.OA.A.1`). The problems now involve two operations or two parts, which forces students to plan rather than computing immediately.

Equal-groups thinking arrives at the end of the year through `2.OA.C.3` (odd and even), `2.OA.C.4` (rectangular arrays), and `2.G.A.2` (partitioning rectangles into rows and columns of same-size squares). Those three standards plant the seeds for grade 3 multiplication.

## Classroom application

A grade 2 math block usually has a daily fact fluency routine (rolling dice, fact triangles, or Math Talks) aimed at `2.OA.B.2`. A daily problem-based lesson handles the major work of place value and addition and subtraction. A small-group time covers measurement and geometry.

The most useful tool at this grade is the open number line. A student solving 73 - 28 on an open number line records jumps of 10, 5, and 2 and lands on 45. That work makes the place value reasoning in `2.NBT.B.7` visible.

Two-step problems (`2.OA.A.1`) need a routine. Students underline the question, identify the two parts, label what they will find first, then solve. Without the routine, two-step problems quickly become guess-an-operation.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Do students need to use the standard algorithm in grade 2?">
    No. The standard algorithm for addition and subtraction is a grade 4 standard (`4.NBT.B.4`). In grade 2, students use "strategies based on place value, properties of operations, and the relationship between addition and subtraction" (`2.NBT.B.5`, `2.NBT.B.7`). The open number line, decomposing into hundreds-tens-ones, and compensation are all on-grade strategies. Introducing the standard algorithm in grade 2 often crowds out the place value thinking the algorithm depends on.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Why are arrays in grade 2 if multiplication is grade 3?">
    `2.OA.C.4` asks students to use addition to find the total number of objects in a rectangular array. The work is repeated addition, not multiplication. That repeated addition becomes the bridge to grade 3 multiplication (`3.OA.A.1`), which treats the same array as a multiplication situation. Grade 2 students should not write multiplication equations yet, but they should be comfortable with arrays and equal groups.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
