# What are the Common Core Geometry standards for Grade 2?

<div class="answer-box">
  Geometry in grade 2 (`2.G`) sharpens shape vocabulary, introduces rectangle partitioning into rows and columns of same-size squares (`2.G.A.2`, the seed of area and arrays), and partitions circles and rectangles into halves, thirds, and fourths (`2.G.A.3`). The third in `2.G.A.3` is new this year, and "three equal shares" gives "thirds" the same status as "halves" and "fourths."

## Classroom application

`2.G.A.1` builds the early polygon vocabulary. The classroom move that pays off is "by the attribute, not the look." A pentagon is not a house shape; it is a shape with five straight sides and five angles. Use irregular pentagons alongside regular ones so that students recognize the count of sides as the defining feature.

`2.G.A.2` is the most consequential geometry standard at this grade. The standard asks students to partition a rectangle into rows and columns of same-size squares and count them. That is an array in disguise. Students who can build a 4-by-3 partition and count 12 squares are doing the work that becomes `3.OA.A.1` and `3.MD.C.5` next year. Connect this work to `2.OA.C.4` (arrays of objects) to make the bridge explicit.

The "equal shares can look different" note in `2.G.A.3` is small and powerful. A rectangle cut horizontally into fourths and a rectangle cut diagonally into fourths can both be true fourths even though the pieces look different. Build that idea with folded paper. Many older students still struggle with this and rely on the prototype "fourths look like four square pieces."

`2.G.A.3` is the last grade before fraction notation arrives in grade 3. Push the language: "three thirds make a whole," "two halves are the same as one whole." Students who fluently use this language arrive at `3.NF` ready to attach the symbols.
