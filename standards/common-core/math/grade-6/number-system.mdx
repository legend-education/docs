# What are the Common Core Number System standards for Grade 6?

<div class="answer-box">
  The Number System in grade 6 (`6.NS`) extends arithmetic in two directions. Students divide fractions by fractions (`6.NS.A.1`), reach fluency with multi-digit decimal arithmetic using the standard algorithm (`6.NS.B.3`), and find greatest common factors and least common multiples (`6.NS.B.4`). The number line stretches to negative numbers (`6.NS.C`), and the coordinate plane extends to all four quadrants (`6.NS.C.8`).

## What it covers

- Apply and extend previous understandings of multiplication and division to divide fractions by fractions (`6.NS.A`).
- Compute fluently with multi-digit numbers and find common factors and multiples (`6.NS.B`).
- Apply and extend previous understandings of numbers to the system of rational numbers (`6.NS.C`).

    - **6.NS.C.6.a:** Recognize opposite signs of numbers as indicating locations on opposite sides of 0 on the number line; recognize that the opposite of the opposite of a number is the number itself, e.g., -(-3) = 3, and that 0 is its own opposite.

    - **6.NS.C.6.c:** Find and position integers and other rational numbers on a horizontal or vertical number line diagram; find and position pairs of integers and other rational numbers on a coordinate plane.

  <Accordion title="6.NS.C.7 sub-points: ordering and absolute value">
    - **6.NS.C.7.a:** Interpret statements of inequality as statements about the relative position of two numbers on a number line diagram. For example, interpret -3 > -7 as a statement that -3 is located to the right of -7 on a number line oriented from left to right.
    - **6.NS.C.7.b:** Write, interpret, and explain statements of order for rational numbers in real-world contexts. For example, write -3 deg C > -7 deg C to express the fact that -3 deg C is warmer than -7 deg C.
    - **6.NS.C.7.c:** Understand the absolute value of a rational number as its distance from 0 on the number line; interpret absolute value as magnitude for a positive or negative quantity in a real-world situation. For example, for an account balance of -30 dollars, write |-30| = 30 to describe the size of the debt in dollars.
    - **6.NS.C.7.d:** Distinguish comparisons of absolute value from statements about order. For example, recognize that an account balance less than -30 dollars represents a debt greater than 30 dollars.

## Classroom application

`6.NS.A.1` is the standard most associated with the "invert and multiply" rule. Teach the rule alongside the meaning: (2/3) / (3/4) asks "how many three-fourths fit in two-thirds?" Use a fraction strip or area model first, then connect to the procedure. The standard explicitly asks for a visual model and a story context, so the meaning is part of the work, not optional.

For `6.NS.C`, the number line is the home of negative numbers. Begin with contexts students can picture (temperature, elevation, debt) and represent them on a horizontal or vertical number line. The "opposite of the opposite" idea in `6.NS.C.6.a` is small but worth a lesson. -(-3) = 3 because the opposite of -3 is 3, and 0 is its own opposite.

Absolute value (`6.NS.C.7.c`) is the distance from zero. The standard explicitly distinguishes comparisons of absolute value from order. -7 is less than -3 (because it sits to the left on the number line), but |-7| is greater than |-3| (because it is farther from zero). The classroom move is to keep the two ideas separate from the start.

`6.NS.C.8` extends the coordinate plane to all four quadrants. Reflections across an axis flip the sign of one coordinate. This connects to the transformations of grade 8 (`8.G.A.3`).

The standard algorithm for division (`6.NS.B.2`) and for decimal operations (`6.NS.B.3`) arrives at this grade. Most students saw the long-division procedure earlier; grade 6 is when it is the named target. Tie the algorithm back to the place value strategies students have used since grade 4.
