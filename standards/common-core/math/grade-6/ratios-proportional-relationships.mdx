# What are the Common Core Ratios & Proportional Relationships standards for Grade 6?

<div class="answer-box">
  Ratios and Proportional Relationships in grade 6 (`6.RP`) is brand new and is the year's biggest conceptual move. Students learn the language of ratios (`6.RP.A.1`), understand a unit rate as the value of `a/b` for the ratio `a:b` (`6.RP.A.2`), and use ratio and rate reasoning to solve real-world problems including unit pricing, constant speed, percent, and measurement conversions (`6.RP.A.3`). Percent is treated as a rate per 100.
</div>

## What it covers

- Understand ratio concepts and use ratio reasoning to solve problems (`6.RP.A`).

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="6.RP.A.3 sub-points: applications of ratio reasoning">
    - **6.RP.A.3.a:** Make tables of equivalent ratios relating quantities with whole-number measurements, find missing values in the tables, and plot the pairs of values on the coordinate plane. Use tables to compare ratios.
    - **6.RP.A.3.b:** Solve unit rate problems including those involving unit pricing and constant speed. For example, if it took 7 hours to mow 4 lawns, then at that rate, how many lawns could be mowed in 35 hours? At what rate were lawns being mowed?
    - **6.RP.A.3.c:** Find a percent of a quantity as a rate per 100 (e.g., 30% of a quantity means 30/100 times the quantity); solve problems involving finding the whole, given a part and the percent.
    - **6.RP.A.3.d:** Use ratio reasoning to convert measurement units; manipulate and transform units appropriately when multiplying or dividing quantities.

## Classroom application

The two highest-leverage representations for ratios are the ratio table and the double number line. A ratio table for 3:4 lists pairs (3, 4), (6, 8), (9, 12), and so on. A double number line places the same pairs on two parallel scales, with the ratio fixed at corresponding marks. Both let students see equivalent ratios as the same multiplicative relationship.

The unit rate (`6.RP.A.2`) is the keystone. For the ratio 3:4, the unit rate is 3/4 (or, going the other direction, 4/3). The unit rate is a single number that captures the ratio. Once students can find a unit rate, every ratio problem becomes a multiplication problem: total cost = (unit price) x (units), total distance = (speed) x (time), and so on.

Percent as a rate per 100 (`6.RP.A.3.c`) is the framing that unifies percent problems. 30% of 80 is 30/100 times 80, which is 24. Treating percent as a rate (rather than a special new operation) lets students use the same ratio reasoning they use for unit pricing.

`6.RP` is the major work of grade 6 and deserves the longest unit of the year (six to eight weeks). The work transfers directly to `7.RP` (proportional relationships) and underlies all of the proportional reasoning students will do in middle school and beyond.
