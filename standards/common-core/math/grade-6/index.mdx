# What are the Common Core Math standards for Grade 6?

<div class="answer-box">
  Grade 6 is the year arithmetic turns into early algebra. Three new domains arrive at once: Ratios and Proportional Relationships (`6.RP`), The Number System (`6.NS`, including negative numbers and absolute value), and Expressions and Equations (`6.EE`, including variables, one-step equations, and inequalities). Students also divide fractions by fractions (`6.NS.A.1`), find areas of triangles and quadrilaterals by decomposing, and start formal work with statistical questions and data distributions.

## What it covers

- Ratios and Proportional Relationships (`6.RP`): the language of ratios and unit rates, percent as a rate per 100, and problems involving rate.
- The Number System (`6.NS`): division of fractions by fractions, multi-digit decimal arithmetic with the standard algorithms, and the introduction of negative numbers and absolute value.
- Expressions and Equations (`6.EE`): exponents, evaluating expressions with variables, equivalent expressions, and one-step equations and inequalities.
- Geometry (`6.G`): area of triangles and quadrilaterals by decomposition, volume of prisms with fractional edge lengths, polygons on the coordinate plane, and surface area using nets.
- Statistics and Probability (`6.SP`): the idea of a statistical question, describing distributions, and summarizing data with measures of center and variability.

## What changes this year

Grade 6 is a major year. Three of the five domains are entirely new. Ratios and Proportional Relationships (`6.RP`) introduces the language of ratios and the concept of a unit rate, with percent treated as a rate per 100 (`6.RP.A.3.c`). This work is the bridge from grade 5 multiplicative thinking to grade 7 proportional reasoning.

The Number System (`6.NS`) extends arithmetic in two directions. First, division of fractions by fractions becomes general (`6.NS.A.1`). Second, the number line stretches to include negative numbers (`6.NS.C`), with absolute value, opposites, and ordering on the full number line.

Expressions and Equations (`6.EE`) begins the algebra strand. Students use variables to write expressions and equations, evaluate expressions, identify equivalent expressions, and solve one-step equations and inequalities. The standards make a clear distinction between expressions, equations, and inequalities.

Statistics and Probability (`6.SP`) introduces the idea that a statistical question is one that anticipates variability in the answers. Students describe distributions by shape, center, and spread, and learn the difference between mean and median.

## Classroom application

The major work of grade 6 is `6.RP`, `6.NS`, and `6.EE`. Plan the year so these three domains get the bulk of instructional time. Geometry and statistics are supporting work, often woven into review weeks or focused units.

For ratios and rates, the double number line and the ratio table are the most useful representations. A 3-to-5 ratio displayed on a double number line lets students scale up (6 to 10, 9 to 15) and find equivalent ratios visually. The ratio table organizes the same information vertically and supports comparison.

For negative numbers, the number line is the high-leverage tool. A student who can locate -3 on the number line, find its opposite, and read its absolute value as the distance from zero has the foundation for grade 7 integer operations.

For one-step equations, the move teachers should make is naming the operation that undoes the one in the equation. The equation 2x = 12 is solved by dividing both sides by 2. The equation x + 5 = 12 is solved by subtracting 5 from both sides. The language of "doing the same thing to both sides" sets up grade 7 multi-step equation work.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="What is the difference between a ratio and a fraction?">
    A fraction is a single number with a numerator and denominator. A ratio is a multiplicative comparison between two quantities. The ratio 3 to 5 can be written 3:5 or as the fraction 3/5, and they look the same on paper, but they mean different things. The fraction 3/5 says "three of five parts of one whole." The ratio 3 to 5 says "for every 3 of one thing, there are 5 of another." Keeping this distinction clear in grade 6 prevents confusion when students hit proportional reasoning in grade 7.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How do I teach negative numbers without confusing students?">
    Start with contexts students can picture: temperature, elevation, and money owed. Use the number line as the home of negative numbers from the first day, and resist the urge to teach rules ("a negative times a negative is a positive") until grade 7. In grade 6, students are learning what negative numbers are and how to order and compare them (`6.NS.C.7`). The operations come next year. Premature rule teaching crowds out the place value and number line thinking the rules depend on.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
