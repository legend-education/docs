# What are the Common Core Expressions & Equations standards for Grade 7?

<div class="answer-box">
  Expressions and Equations in grade 7 (`7.EE`) extends the algebra strand from grade 6. Students add, subtract, factor, and expand linear expressions with rational coefficients (`7.EE.A.1`), see how rewriting an expression can reveal information (`7.EE.A.2`), solve multi-step real-life problems with positive and negative rational numbers (`7.EE.B.3`), and solve equations of the form `px + q = r` and `p(x + q) = r` and matching inequalities (`7.EE.B.4`).
</div>

## What it covers

- Use properties of operations to generate equivalent expressions (`7.EE.A`).
- Solve real-life and mathematical problems using numerical and algebraic expressions and equations (`7.EE.B`).

## Classroom application

`7.EE.A.1` extends the distributive property work from grade 6 to expressions with rational coefficients. 3(2x - 1.5) = 6x - 4.5. Factoring is the reverse: 6x - 4.5 = 3(2x - 1.5) or 1.5(4x - 3). Both directions deserve practice, with several different factorings highlighted to support `MP7` (look for structure).

`7.EE.A.2` is the strategic-rewrite standard. The same expression in two forms can show two things about a situation. `a + 0.05a` shows "the original plus the increase." `1.05a` shows "the new amount as a multiplier of the original." Both are useful in percent change problems. The classroom routine is to give an expression and ask, "What does each form tell you?"

For `7.EE.B.4`, equations should always be paired with arithmetic and algebraic solutions. A perimeter problem like "The perimeter is 54 and the length is 6. What is the width?" can be solved by reasoning (54 - 12 = 42, 42 / 2 = 21) or by algebra (2L + 2W = 54, 12 + 2W = 54, 2W = 42, W = 21). Both methods are valid. Comparing the two builds `MP1` (make sense of problems) and `MP3` (critique reasoning).

The inequality work in `7.EE.B.4.b` is the harder part. Solving `3x + 50 >= 100` gives `x >= 50/3 = 16.67`. In a context where the variable is a count of sales, the answer is `x >= 17`. The interpretation in context is part of the standard, and rubrics that include it tend to catch the common error of ignoring the situation.
