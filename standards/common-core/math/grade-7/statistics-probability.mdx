# What are the Common Core Statistics & Probability standards for Grade 7?

<div class="answer-box">
  Statistics and Probability in grade 7 (`7.SP`) adds sampling (`7.SP.A`), comparison of two populations (`7.SP.B`), and probability as a formal topic for the first time (`7.SP.C`). Students learn that a random sample lets them make inferences about a population (`7.SP.A.2`), compare two distributions with measures of center and variability (`7.SP.B.4`), and find probabilities of simple and compound events using lists, tables, tree diagrams, and simulation.

## What it covers

- Use random sampling to draw inferences about a population (`7.SP.A`).
- Draw informal comparative inferences about two populations (`7.SP.B`).
- Investigate chance processes and develop, use, and evaluate probability models (`7.SP.C`).

## Classroom application

`7.SP.A` introduces the idea of inference. A sample of words from a book lets students estimate the mean word length in the book. Multiple samples reveal the variability in the estimate. The classroom move is to have each student or pair generate a sample, then assemble the class's estimates into a distribution. The shape and spread of the class's estimates is itself a useful object.

For `7.SP.B`, comparison of two populations is done with random samples and measures of center and variability. Box plots are the highest-leverage display: two box plots side by side reveal differences in median, interquartile range, and overall spread at a glance.

Probability enters in `7.SP.C.5`. The 0-to-1 scale gives a useful visual prompt: ask students where on a 0-to-1 number line they would place the probability of various events. "Drawing a red ball from a bag with 3 red and 7 blue" is at 0.3. "It will rain in October" is somewhere closer to 0.5 or higher. The placements support discussion of likely versus unlikely.

The theoretical-versus-experimental distinction in `7.SP.C.6` and `7.SP.C.7` is the standard's central conceptual move. A theoretical model says rolling a 3 has probability 1/6. An experiment of 600 rolls gives a relative frequency near 1/6, but probably not exactly. Comparing experimental and theoretical probabilities, and explaining discrepancies, is what statistical thinking at this grade looks like.

Compound events in `7.SP.C.8` are the most procedurally demanding. Students use organized lists, tables, and tree diagrams to count outcomes. Simulation (rolling dice, flipping coins, using a random number generator) is the high-leverage tool for complex compound events where the count is hard.
