# What are the Common Core Geometry standards for Grade 7?

<div class="answer-box">
  Geometry in grade 7 (`7.G`) extends to scale drawings (`7.G.A.1`), constructions of triangles from given conditions (`7.G.A.2`), and cross-sections of three-dimensional figures (`7.G.A.3`). The circumference and area formulas for circles enter for the first time (`7.G.B.4`). Students use angle relationships (supplementary, complementary, vertical, adjacent) to find unknown angles (`7.G.B.5`), and they solve area, surface area, and volume problems with two- and three-dimensional figures (`7.G.B.6`).
</div>

## What it covers

- Draw, construct, and describe geometrical figures and describe the relationships between them (`7.G.A`).
- Solve real-life and mathematical problems involving angle measure, area, surface area, and volume (`7.G.B`).

## Classroom application

Scale drawings in `7.G.A.1` are a direct application of `7.RP`. The scale factor is a unit rate. A scale of 1 inch to 4 feet means every inch on the drawing represents 4 feet in real life. Doubling the scale factor doubles the linear dimensions and squares the areas, which is a useful `MP7` insight (look for structure).

The triangle-construction work in `7.G.A.2` is one of the more surprising standards. Three side lengths usually determine a unique triangle (SSS). Two sides and the included angle do too (SAS). But three angles do not (similarity, not congruence), and SSA can give zero, one, or two triangles. The standard wants students to notice these patterns through exploration, not memorize them.

Cross-sections in `7.G.A.3` connect to grade 5 and grade 6 volume work. A horizontal slice of a right rectangular prism gives a rectangle. A vertical slice through a rectangular pyramid gives a triangle. Using 3D models or software (such as a dynamic geometry tool) makes the slicing visible.

For `7.G.B.4`, the relationship `C = 2 pi r` and `A = pi r^2` arrives at this grade. The informal derivation called for in the standard usually goes: a circle is approximated by many narrow triangles, each with height `r`, and the total base of all the triangles is the circumference `2 pi r`. The total area is then `(1/2)(2 pi r)(r) = pi r^2`. The derivation supports `MP3` and `MP7`.

The angle work in `7.G.B.5` is the algebraic side of geometry. Two supplementary angles sum to 180. Two complementary angles sum to 90. Vertical angles are equal. Each fact gives an equation that, paired with `7.EE.B.4`, lets students solve for an unknown angle.
