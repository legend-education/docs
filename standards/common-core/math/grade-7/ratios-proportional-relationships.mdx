# What are the Common Core Ratios & Proportional Relationships standards for Grade 7?

<div class="answer-box">
  Ratios and Proportional Relationships in grade 7 (`7.RP`) is the year ratio thinking becomes proportional thinking. Students compute unit rates from ratios of fractions (`7.RP.A.1`), recognize a proportional relationship across tables, graphs, equations, and contexts, and identify the constant of proportionality (`7.RP.A.2`). They solve multi-step ratio and percent problems including tax, tip, markup, markdown, simple interest, percent error, and percent increase and decrease (`7.RP.A.3`).

## What it covers

- Analyze proportional relationships and use them to solve real-world and mathematical problems (`7.RP.A`).

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="7.RP.A.2 sub-points: representing proportional relationships">
    - **7.RP.A.2.a:** Decide whether two quantities are in a proportional relationship, e.g., by testing for equivalent ratios in a table or graphing on a coordinate plane and observing whether the graph is a straight line through the origin.
    - **7.RP.A.2.b:** Identify the constant of proportionality (unit rate) in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions of proportional relationships.
    - **7.RP.A.2.c:** Represent proportional relationships by equations. For example, if total cost t is proportional to the number n of items purchased at a constant price p, the relationship between the total cost and the number of items can be expressed as t = pn.
    - **7.RP.A.2.d:** Explain what a point (x, y) on the graph of a proportional relationship means in terms of the situation, with special attention to the points (0, 0) and (1, r) where r is the unit rate.

## Classroom application

The four representations of `7.RP.A.2` (table, graph, equation, verbal description) should be taught as four views of the same thing. Use a daily routine: given the relationship in one form, produce the other three. The connections are the standard's main target.

The graph of a proportional relationship is a straight line through the origin. The slope of that line is the constant of proportionality, which is also the unit rate, which is also the coefficient in `y = kx`. Naming these four things as the same number across four contexts is the central insight of `7.RP.A.2.b`.

For `7.RP.A.3`, percent problems are not new operations. They are proportional relationships in disguise. A 15% tip on a $40 meal: students set up the proportion `15/100 = t/40` or compute `0.15 x 40` directly. Both work. The classroom move is to keep percent as a unit rate (`6.RP.A.3.c`), now applied to a wider set of contexts.

Percent change problems (markup, markdown, percent increase and decrease) need careful attention to the "of what" question. A 20% increase on $50 is 20% of 50, which is 10; the new total is $60. The standard explicitly includes percent error, which is calculated as |measured - actual| / actual x 100%. Treating each context with a tape diagram or double number line keeps the proportional reasoning visible.

`7.RP` is the major work of grade 7 and deserves the longest unit (six to eight weeks). The work transfers directly to grade 8 linear functions (`8.F`) and to high school work with linear and exponential models.
