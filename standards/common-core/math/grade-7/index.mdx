# What are the Common Core Math standards for Grade 7?

<div class="answer-box">
  Grade 7 is the year of proportional relationships. Students recognize and represent proportions in tables, graphs, equations (`y = kx`), and contexts, and solve multi-step ratio and percent problems. The Number System extends to all four operations on rational numbers (including negatives). Expressions and Equations grow to multi-step equations and inequalities with rational coefficients. Statistics adds sampling and informal comparison of populations. Probability shows up here for the first time as a formal topic.

## What it covers

- Ratios and Proportional Relationships (`7.RP`): proportional relationships in multiple representations, constant of proportionality, and multi-step ratio and percent problems.
- The Number System (`7.NS`): the four operations on rational numbers, including integers, and converting between fractions and decimals.
- Expressions and Equations (`7.EE`): adding and subtracting linear expressions, rewriting expressions, and solving multi-step equations and inequalities with rational coefficients.
- Geometry (`7.G`): scale drawings, constructions, slicing solids, circles, angle relationships, area, and volume.
- Statistics and Probability (`7.SP`): random sampling, comparing populations, probability of simple events, and probability of compound events.

## What changes this year

Grade 7 is when ratio thinking from grade 6 becomes proportional thinking. Students recognize when two quantities are in a proportional relationship, identify the constant of proportionality (`7.RP.A.2.b`), and represent the relationship with the equation `y = kx` (`7.RP.A.2.c`). Percent moves from "a rate per 100" to multi-step problems with tax, tip, markup, markdown, simple interest, percent error, and percent increase or decrease (`7.RP.A.3`).

Operations on rational numbers come together. Students add, subtract, multiply, and divide signed rational numbers and apply these operations to solve real-world problems (`7.NS.A.1`, `7.NS.A.2`, `7.NS.A.3`). This is the year the rule "a negative times a negative is a positive" makes sense, because it is built from the meanings of multiplication and division on the number line.

Equations grow from one-step (grade 6) to multi-step (`7.EE.B.4`). Students solve equations of the form `px + q = r` and `p(x + q) = r` and graph inequalities of the form `px + q > r` or `px + q < r`. This sets up the function and linear equation work of grade 8.

Probability appears for the first time. Students learn that probability is a number between 0 and 1 (`7.SP.C.5`), use experimental and theoretical probability, and find probabilities of compound events using lists, tables, and tree diagrams.

## Classroom application

The major work of grade 7 is `7.RP`, `7.NS`, and `7.EE`. Schedule the year so these three domains carry the bulk of instructional time, with geometry, statistics, and probability woven in.

For proportional relationships, the four representations should be taught together: table, graph, equation, and verbal description. A useful daily move is to give students a relationship in one representation and ask them to produce the other three. The connection between "a straight line through the origin" on a graph and the equation `y = kx` (where k is the slope and the constant of proportionality) is the keystone of `7.RP`.

For signed-number operations, the chip model (positive and negative counters) and the number line both work. The number line is faster for showing addition and subtraction (movements in opposite directions). The chip model is clearer for showing why -3 + 3 = 0 (zero pairs). Use both and let students choose.

Equations work in grade 7 should always include arithmetic and algebraic methods side by side. A student who solves a tip problem by reasoning (15% of 40 is 6) and a student who solves it by writing 0.15 x 40 = t and computing should both get full credit. Both `MP1` and `MP4` reward this kind of solution flexibility.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Why does the constant of proportionality matter so much?">
    The constant of proportionality is the unit rate of a proportional relationship, the slope of its graph, and the coefficient in its equation. Recognizing it across all four representations is the work of `7.RP.A.2`. Without that recognition, students see proportional relationships as a list of unrelated problems. With it, they see one idea in four costumes, which transfers directly to grade 8 linear functions and high school slope work.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Is grade 7 the right time for full integer operations?">
    Yes. Grade 6 introduced negative numbers as positions on the number line. Grade 7 is the year students operate on them. The progression matters: students need a year of locating, ordering, and comparing negative numbers before they multiply and divide them. Teachers who try to compress signed-number operations into grade 6 usually find students arrive in grade 8 still applying rules they do not understand.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
