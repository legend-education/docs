# What are the Common Core Geometry standards for Grade 1?

<div class="answer-box">
  Geometry in grade 1 (`1.G`) sharpens the difference between attributes that define a shape and attributes that do not (`1.G.A.1`), builds composite shapes from simpler ones (`1.G.A.2`), and introduces equal shares as halves and fourths of circles and rectangles (`1.G.A.3`). The fraction work here is verbal, not numerical. Halves and fourths are introduced as words and as equal parts, with fraction notation coming later.
</div>

## What it covers

- Reason with shapes and their attributes (`1.G.A`).

## Classroom application

`1.G.A.1` is built through the "always, sometimes, never" routine. "A triangle always has three sides." (always). "A triangle is always pointing up." (sometimes, depending on orientation). "A triangle is always red." (never). The routine surfaces the distinction the standard is asking for.

For `1.G.A.2`, pattern blocks and tangrams are the main vehicles. Two triangles make a square. A trapezoid plus a triangle makes a parallelogram. Three triangles make a trapezoid. Six triangles make a hexagon. The composition work builds spatial reasoning and previews the area decomposition of grade 3 and grade 6.

The fraction work in `1.G.A.3` does not use fraction notation. Students do not write 1/2 yet (that is a grade 3 standard). The work is verbal and visual: a circle split into two equal parts has two halves; each half is half of the whole. The key insight at the end of the standard is that more equal parts means smaller parts. That insight is the seed of `3.NF.A.2` (locating fractions on the number line).

Watch for the equal-shares trap. Students often split a circle into two parts that look different (a big piece and a small piece) and call them halves. The standard requires equal shares. Use folded paper, tracing paper, and explicit conversation ("Are they the same size?") to keep the equal-shares idea front and center.
