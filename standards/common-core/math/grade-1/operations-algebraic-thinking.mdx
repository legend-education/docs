# What are the Common Core Operations & Algebraic Thinking standards for Grade 1?

<div class="answer-box">
  Operations and Algebraic Thinking is the spine of grade 1 math. Students solve word problems within 20 across five problem types (add to, take from, put together, take apart, compare) with the unknown in any position (`1.OA.A.1`). They use the commutative and associative properties as strategies (`1.OA.B.3`), reach fluency with addition and subtraction within 10 using strategies like counting on and making ten (`1.OA.C.6`), and learn the meaning of the equal sign (`1.OA.D.7`).

## What it covers

- Represent and solve problems involving addition and subtraction (`1.OA.A`).
- Understand and apply properties of operations and the relationship between addition and subtraction (`1.OA.B`).
- Add and subtract within 20 (`1.OA.C`).
- Work with addition and subtraction equations (`1.OA.D`).

## Classroom application

`1.OA.A.1` is the year-long standard. The 11 problem types within "add to, take from, put together, take apart, and compare" cover every grade 1 word problem. Plan instruction so each type gets multiple days, not a single lesson. A common sequence is add-to and take-from with result unknown, then change unknown, then start unknown; then put-together and take-apart; then compare. The hardest type at this age is compare with difference unknown ("How many more does she have?") because the language hides what to do.

For `1.OA.C.6`, the highest-leverage strategy is making ten. A double ten-frame makes the strategy visible: 8 + 6 becomes 10 + 4 by moving 2 counters from the 6 to fill the 8. Once making ten lands, addition within 20 stops being memorization. By spring, students should know addition facts within 10 from memory and use strategies for facts within 20.

The equal sign work in `1.OA.D.7` is small but consequential. A student who reads `=` as "and then" will mis-solve 8 + ___ = 13 by adding 8 and 13. Treat `1.OA.D.7` as its own short unit with true and false equations, equations with the unknown in different positions, and a daily routine ("Find the number that makes this equation true").
