# What are the Common Core Math standards for Grade 1?

<div class="answer-box">
  Grade 1 is the year addition and subtraction become the spine of math. Students solve word problems within 20 (`1.OA.A.1`), use strategies like counting on and making ten (`1.OA.C.6`), and learn the meaning of the equal sign as a balance. They also stretch counting and place value to 120 (`1.NBT.A.1`), measure with non-standard units, tell time to the hour and half hour, and start putting shapes together to make new shapes.

## What it covers

- Operations and Algebraic Thinking (`1.OA`): addition and subtraction within 20, strategies, properties, and the meaning of the equal sign.
- Number and Operations in Base Ten (`1.NBT`): counting to 120, understanding two-digit numbers as tens and ones, and adding within 100.
- Measurement and Data (`1.MD`): non-standard length measurement, telling time, and representing data with picture and bar graphs.
- Geometry (`1.G`): defining attributes of shapes, composing shapes, and partitioning into halves and fourths.

## What changes this year

Grade 1 is the year addition and subtraction grow up. Word problems get more types: add to, take from, put together, take apart, and compare (`1.OA.A.1`, `1.OA.A.2`). Students learn that the same problem can be solved with several strategies: counting on, making ten, doubles, and using the relationship between addition and subtraction (`1.OA.C.6`). Fluency moves from "within 5" to "within 10," with full fluency within 20 expected in grade 2.

Place value expands from "ten and some more" to two-digit numbers as a number of tens and a number of ones (`1.NBT.B.2`). This sets up every grade of place value that follows. Students also compare two-digit numbers using the symbols `<`, `>`, and `=` (`1.NBT.B.3`).

The equal sign is treated explicitly for the first time (`1.OA.D.7`). The standard makes it clear that `=` means "the same as," and that 8 = 5 + 3 is just as true as 5 + 3 = 8.

## Classroom application

A typical grade 1 math hour has three parts: a number routine (number talk, counting collection, or quick image), a problem-based lesson, and small-group rotations. The number routine builds fluency toward `1.OA.C.6`. The problem-based lesson builds `1.OA.A.1` and `1.OA.A.2`. Small groups touch measurement, data, and geometry.

The making-ten strategy (`1.OA.C.6`) is the most important strategy at this grade. Students see 8 + 6 as 8 + 2 + 4 = 10 + 4 = 14. Once making ten lands, addition within 20 stops being memorization and starts being reasoning. Ten-frames, double ten-frames, and rekenreks are the most useful tools.

A grade 1 word-problem rubric tracks four moves: the student names the situation (add to, take from, etc.), picks a strategy, executes it accurately, and writes an equation that records the work.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="What is the difference between adding within 10 and within 20?">
    Within 10 means both addends and the sum stay at 10 or below, so a student can find the answer by counting on fingers or using a known pair. Within 20 means the sum can go up to 20, so the student often needs a stronger strategy than counting on: making ten, using a known double, or using the relationship between addition and subtraction. `1.OA.C.6` asks for fluency with both, but with strategies rather than memorized facts.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Why teach the equal sign as a separate standard?">
    Because students often read `=` as "and then" rather than "the same as." A student who thinks `=` means "and then" will solve 8 + ___ = 13 by adding 8 and 13. Treating `1.OA.D.7` as its own lesson, with true and false equations and equations with the unknown in different positions, fixes this misconception before it becomes a wall in grade 2 and grade 3.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
