# What are the Common Core Measurement & Data standards for Grade 1?

<div class="answer-box">
  Measurement and Data in grade 1 (`1.MD`) takes three steps past kindergarten. Students compare three objects by length and use a third object to compare two indirectly (`1.MD.A.1`), measure length by laying out same-size length units end to end (`1.MD.A.2`), tell and write time to the hour and half hour (`1.MD.B.3`), and organize, represent, and ask questions about data with up to three categories (`1.MD.C.4`).

## What it covers

- Measure lengths indirectly and by iterating length units (`1.MD.A`).
- Tell and write time (`1.MD.B`).
- Represent and interpret data (`1.MD.C`).

## Classroom application

The "no gaps or overlaps" piece of `1.MD.A.2` is the precision target (`MP6`). Watch for students who lay paper clips with spaces between them or with clips overlapping. The fix is a single anchor chart that names what counts as a good measurement: same-size unit, no gaps, no overlaps, count the units.

`1.MD.A.1` is built through ordering routines. Three pencils on a desk get ordered shortest to longest. Two objects in different parts of the room get compared using a string as the third object. The standard names indirect comparison explicitly because students at this age cannot yet rely on a ruler.

Telling time (`1.MD.B.3`) is one of the harder grade 1 standards. Hour and half hour both depend on knowing that the hour hand moves between numbers and that the minute hand at 6 means 30 minutes. Daily practice with the classroom clock is the highest-leverage move; less helpful is a single time unit at the end of the year.

`1.MD.C.4` connects measurement and data to addition and subtraction. A bar graph of favorite colors with three categories gives the chance to ask "How many in red and blue together?" (`1.OA.A.1`, put together) and "How many more in green than red?" (compare with difference unknown). The graph is the vehicle; the arithmetic is the lift.
