# What are the Common Core Number & Operations in Base Ten standards for Grade 1?

<div class="answer-box">
  Number and Operations in Base Ten in grade 1 (`1.NBT`) builds two-digit place value. Students count to 120 starting at any number (`1.NBT.A.1`), see a two-digit number as a number of tens and a number of ones (`1.NBT.B.2`), and compare two-digit numbers using `<`, `>`, and `=` (`1.NBT.B.3`). They add within 100 using place value strategies (`1.NBT.C.4`), find 10 more or 10 less mentally (`1.NBT.C.5`), and subtract multiples of 10 (`1.NBT.C.6`).
</div>

## What it covers

- Extend the counting sequence (`1.NBT.A`).
- Understand place value (`1.NBT.B`).
- Use place value understanding and properties of operations to add and subtract (`1.NBT.C`).

## Classroom application

The counting work in `1.NBT.A.1` does not get a separate unit. It lives in the daily count-around-the-circle and choral counting routines. Start the year counting to 20, push past 50 by winter, and hit 120 by spring. Counting on from any number is the harder skill; spend extra time starting from non-decade numbers like 47, 89, or 113.

The shift to seeing 47 as "4 tens and 7 ones" (`1.NBT.B.2`) is the foundational move of the year. Linking cubes and base-ten blocks make this concrete: students bundle 10 single cubes into a "ten stick" and then count the sticks and the singles. By spring, the bundling should be automatic.

For `1.NBT.C.4`, place value strategies should come before the standard algorithm (which is a grade 4 standard). A useful chain: 38 + 25 becomes 30 + 20 (= 50) plus 8 + 5 (= 13), which gives 63. Many students find the open number line easier: jump from 38 by 20 to 58, then by 5 to 63. Both record the place value reasoning.

Mental "10 more, 10 less" (`1.NBT.C.5`) is short and important. A daily routine where the teacher calls a number and the class responds with "10 more" or "10 less" builds the place value pattern that powers the rest of the work.
