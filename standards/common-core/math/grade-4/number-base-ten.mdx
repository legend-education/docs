# What are the Common Core Number & Operations in Base Ten standards for Grade 4?

<div class="answer-box">
  Number and Operations in Base Ten in grade 4 (`4.NBT`) is one of the two major rocks of the year. Students extend place value to numbers up to one million, recognize that a digit represents ten times what it would represent in the place to its right (`4.NBT.A.1`), and round to any place. They reach the standard algorithm for addition and subtraction (`4.NBT.B.4`), multiply up to a four-digit number by a one-digit number and two two-digit numbers (`4.NBT.B.5`), and divide four-digit numbers by one-digit divisors (`4.NBT.B.6`).
</div>

## What it covers

- Generalize place value understanding for multi-digit whole numbers (`4.NBT.A`).
- Use place value understanding and properties of operations to perform multi-digit arithmetic (`4.NBT.B`).

## Classroom application

`4.NBT.A.1` is the keystone of grade 4 place value. The "ten times" relationship works in both directions: a 7 in the hundreds place is 10 times the 7 in the tens place, and the 7 in the tens place is 1/10 of the 7 in the hundreds place. The reciprocal relationship sets up decimal work this year and next.

The standard algorithm for addition and subtraction (`4.NBT.B.4`) arrives in grade 4 for the first time. The standard does not say students must use only the algorithm. Other place value strategies remain valid, especially when the algorithm would be slow (computing 1000 - 998 by counting up).

For `4.NBT.B.5`, the area model is the work. A two-digit by two-digit problem like 23 x 47 becomes a 2-by-2 grid: (20 + 3) x (40 + 7) gives 800, 140, 120, and 21. The model exposes the distributive property and connects to the partial-product algorithm and eventually to the standard algorithm (a grade 5 standard, `5.NBT.B.5`).

`4.NBT.B.6` introduces multi-digit division. The rectangular array model is the high-leverage tool: 144 / 8 becomes "what is the side length of a rectangle with area 144 and side length 8?" Students build the side length by partial quotients: 8 x 10 = 80 (so far 10, area used 80, area remaining 64), 8 x 8 = 64 (8 more, total 18). The partial-quotient strategy builds understanding without the algorithm-flavored confusion of the long-division procedure.
