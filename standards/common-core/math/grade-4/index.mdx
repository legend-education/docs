# What are the Common Core Math standards for Grade 4?

<div class="answer-box">
  Grade 4 is when multi-digit multiplication and fraction equivalence come together as the major work. Students multiply up to a four-digit number by a one-digit number, multiply two two-digit numbers (`4.NBT.B.5`), and divide four-digit dividends by one-digit divisors (`4.NBT.B.6`). They build fraction equivalence by multiplying numerator and denominator by the same number (`4.NF.A.1`), add fractions with like denominators, and multiply a fraction by a whole number. Decimals appear for the first time (`4.NF.C.6`).
</div>

## What it covers

- Operations and Algebraic Thinking (`4.OA`): multiplicative comparison, multi-step word problems, factors and multiples, and patterns.
- Number and Operations in Base Ten (`4.NBT`): place value to one million, the standard algorithm for addition and subtraction, multi-digit multiplication, and multi-digit division.
- Number and Operations: Fractions (`4.NF`): equivalence, comparison, addition and subtraction of like fractions, multiplication of a fraction by a whole number, and decimal notation for fractions with denominators 10 and 100.
- Measurement and Data (`4.MD`): unit conversion within a system, area and perimeter, line plots with fractions, and angles measured in degrees.
- Geometry (`4.G`): lines and angles, classifying two-dimensional figures, and symmetry.

## What changes this year

Grade 4 is a major step in arithmetic. Students reach the standard algorithm for addition and subtraction (`4.NBT.B.4`) and begin multi-digit multiplication using place value strategies and the properties of operations (`4.NBT.B.5`). Multi-digit division (`4.NBT.B.6`) begins with a one-digit divisor.

Fractions move from "numbers on the number line" to "numbers you can operate on." Students build equivalence by multiplying numerator and denominator by the same number (`4.NF.A.1`), compare fractions with different numerators and denominators using benchmarks (`4.NF.A.2`), and add and subtract fractions with like denominators (`4.NF.B.3`). They multiply a fraction by a whole number using the meaning of multiplication (`4.NF.B.4`).

Decimals show up for the first time (`4.NF.C.5`, `4.NF.C.6`, `4.NF.C.7`). Students see decimals as fractions with denominators of 10 or 100 and write them in decimal notation. This sets up grade 5 decimal operations.

Multi-step problems become routine (`4.OA.A.3`). Students learn to assess the reasonableness of answers and use letters for unknown quantities. This is also where mental computation and estimation start appearing inside rubrics.

## Classroom application

The two pillars of the grade 4 year are multi-digit multiplication (`4.NBT.B.5`) and fraction equivalence and addition (`4.NF.A.1`, `4.NF.B.3`). Most state tests draw the heaviest from these standards.

The area model is the highest-leverage representation for multi-digit multiplication. A 23 x 47 problem becomes a rectangle split into (20 + 3) x (40 + 7), which gives four partial products: 800, 140, 120, and 21. The area model exposes the place value reasoning behind the standard algorithm and connects directly to the distributive property.

For fractions, a tape diagram and a number line work together. Equivalence is the relationship between two ways of partitioning the same length. Comparison uses common benchmarks (0, 1/2, 1) and reasoning about how many of each piece fit.

Multi-step problems benefit from a labeled tape diagram. Students draw the whole, label what they know and what they need to find, and write equations alongside. The diagram makes each step visible and supports `MP1` (make sense of problems).

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Is the area model the standard algorithm for multiplication?">
    No. The area model is one of the place value strategies named in `4.NBT.B.5`. The standard algorithm for multi-digit multiplication is a grade 5 standard (`5.NBT.B.5`). In grade 4, students should be able to use and explain the area model and other place value strategies. The standard algorithm can be introduced in grade 4 but should not replace the strategy work that explains why it works.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How are mixed numbers treated in grade 4?">
    Mixed numbers appear in `4.NF.B.3.c` and `4.NF.B.4.c`. Students add and subtract mixed numbers with like denominators by replacing each mixed number with an equivalent fraction or by adding the wholes and fractions separately. Conversion between mixed numbers and improper fractions is a tool, not a target. The focus is on understanding the size of the quantity and using equivalence flexibly.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
