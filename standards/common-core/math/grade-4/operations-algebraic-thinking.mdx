# What are the Common Core Operations & Algebraic Thinking standards for Grade 4?

<div class="answer-box">
  Operations and Algebraic Thinking in grade 4 (`4.OA`) introduces multiplicative comparison. Students interpret 35 = 5 x 7 as "35 is 5 times as many as 7" (`4.OA.A.1`) and solve multiplicative comparison word problems (`4.OA.A.2`). They solve multi-step word problems with all four operations and assess reasonableness (`4.OA.A.3`). Factors, multiples, and prime versus composite enter (`4.OA.B.4`), and the year ends with generating and analyzing number patterns (`4.OA.C.5`).
</div>

## What it covers

- Use the four operations with whole numbers to solve problems (`4.OA.A`).
- Gain familiarity with factors and multiples (`4.OA.B`).
- Generate and analyze patterns (`4.OA.C`).

## Classroom application

Multiplicative comparison (`4.OA.A.1` and `4.OA.A.2`) is the new conceptual move of the year. The standard explicitly asks students to distinguish multiplicative from additive comparison. "Maya has 3 more books than Sam" is additive. "Maya has 3 times as many books as Sam" is multiplicative. Tape diagrams make the difference visible: additive comparison stacks equal-length strips; multiplicative comparison repeats one strip.

Multi-step problems (`4.OA.A.3`) need a routine. Underline the question, identify each step in order, label what you will find first, and write an equation with a letter for the unknown. The standard names estimation explicitly, so an estimate-first move is part of the work, not an extra.

Factors and multiples (`4.OA.B.4`) are best taught through arrays. The factor pairs of 24 are the dimensions of the rectangles you can build with 24 unit squares: 1 x 24, 2 x 12, 3 x 8, 4 x 6. A number like 7 has only 1 x 7, which makes it prime. This connection to area runs from grade 3 forward.

The pattern work in `4.OA.C.5` is small but worth doing. The standard explicitly asks students to notice features of a pattern that were not explicit in the rule. That is `MP8` (look for and express regularity in repeated reasoning) in disguise.
