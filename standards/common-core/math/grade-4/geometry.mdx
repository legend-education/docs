# What are the Common Core Geometry standards for Grade 4?

<div class="answer-box">
  Geometry in grade 4 (`4.G`) introduces formal vocabulary for points, lines, line segments, rays, angles, and perpendicular and parallel lines (`4.G.A.1`). Students classify two-dimensional figures by the presence or absence of parallel or perpendicular sides and angles of specified size (`4.G.A.2`), including right triangles, and recognize lines of symmetry (`4.G.A.3`).

## What it covers

- Draw and identify lines and angles, and classify shapes by properties of their lines and angles (`4.G.A`).

## Classroom application

`4.G.A.1` is the vocabulary year. The distinction between a line (extends forever in both directions), a line segment (has two endpoints), and a ray (has one endpoint and extends forever in the other direction) is small but consequential. Use a daily routine of "draw and label" so students get many chances to use the words in sketches.

Parallel and perpendicular lines (`4.G.A.1`, `4.G.A.2`) are best taught with geoboards, dot paper, or graph paper. Students draw a square and label the parallel and perpendicular sides. Then they draw a rhombus and notice that its sides can still be parallel without being perpendicular. The shape and its label do not always match grade school stereotypes; that mismatch is the point of the standard.

Right triangles are a new category at this grade. The standard explicitly calls them out. A right triangle has one 90-degree angle, no more. Showing the right angle with a small square in the corner (the standard notation) helps students mark it consistently.

Symmetry (`4.G.A.3`) is best built through paper folding. A folded square has up to four lines of symmetry (vertical, horizontal, and two diagonals). A folded rectangle has two. Students discover the symmetry by folding rather than memorizing the rules.
