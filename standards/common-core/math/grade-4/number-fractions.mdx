# What are the Common Core Number & Operations: Fractions standards for Grade 4?

<div class="answer-box">
  Fractions in grade 4 (`4.NF`) build equivalence and operate on fractions for the first time. Students explain why `a/b = (n x a)/(n x b)` (`4.NF.A.1`), compare fractions with different denominators (`4.NF.A.2`), add and subtract fractions and mixed numbers with like denominators (`4.NF.B.3`), and multiply a fraction by a whole number (`4.NF.B.4`). Decimals enter as fractions with denominators 10 and 100 (`4.NF.C.5` to `4.NF.C.7`). Grade 4 limits denominators to 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, 12, and 100.
</div>

## What it covers

- Extend understanding of fraction equivalence and ordering (`4.NF.A`).
- Build fractions from unit fractions (`4.NF.B`).
- Understand decimal notation for fractions, and compare decimal fractions (`4.NF.C`).

    - **4.NF.B.3.a:** Understand addition and subtraction of fractions as joining and separating parts referring to the same whole.
    - **4.NF.B.3.b:** Decompose a fraction into a sum of fractions with the same denominator in more than one way, recording each decomposition by an equation. Justify decompositions, e.g., by using a visual fraction model.
    - **4.NF.B.3.c:** Add and subtract mixed numbers with like denominators, e.g., by replacing each mixed number with an equivalent fraction, or by using properties of operations and the relationship between addition and subtraction.
    - **4.NF.B.3.d:** Solve word problems involving addition and subtraction of fractions referring to the same whole and having like denominators, e.g., by using visual fraction models and equations to represent the problem.

  <Accordion title="4.NF.B.4 sub-points: multiplying a fraction by a whole number">
    - **4.NF.B.4.a:** Understand a fraction a/b as a multiple of 1/b. For example, use a visual fraction model to represent 5/4 as the product 5 x (1/4), recording the conclusion by the equation 5/4 = 5 x (1/4).
    - **4.NF.B.4.b:** Understand a multiple of a/b as a multiple of 1/b, and use this understanding to multiply a fraction by a whole number. For example, use a visual fraction model to express 3 x (2/5) as 6 x (1/5), recognizing this product as 6/5. In general, n x (a/b) = (n x a)/b.

## Classroom application

`4.NF.A.1` is the keystone of grade 4 fractions. The visual model that makes the standard concrete is a fraction strip or area model that is partitioned more finely. A 1/2 piece, partitioned into 4 equal parts, gives 4/8. The number of parts and the size of each part both change, but the total length is the same. This is the conceptual basis of the procedural rule "multiply top and bottom by the same number."

For `4.NF.A.2`, benchmark fractions are the high-leverage strategy. 3/8 is less than 1/2 (because 4/8 is 1/2). 5/9 is greater than 1/2 (because 4.5/9 is 1/2). Comparing 3/8 and 5/9 is then easy without finding common denominators. The benchmark strategy supports `MP3` (construct viable arguments) because students explain the reasoning rather than running a procedure.

Multiplying a fraction by a whole number (`4.NF.B.4`) is where the meaning of multiplication matters most. 3 x 2/5 is three groups of 2/5, which is 6/5. The standard wants students to see fraction multiplication as repeated addition of unit fractions, not as a memorized rule. The grade 5 work in `5.NF.B` builds on this meaning.

Decimals enter in `4.NF.C.5` to `4.NF.C.7`. Students see 0.7 as 7/10 and 0.62 as 62/100 before they see them as numbers on the number line. The connection between fraction and decimal notation, established here, runs through every subsequent grade.

Fraction work in grade 4 deserves four to six weeks of dedicated time. Most state tests draw heavily from `4.NF`, especially equivalence and comparison.
