# What are the Common Core Measurement & Data standards for Grade 4?

<div class="answer-box">
  Measurement and Data in grade 4 (`4.MD`) introduces unit conversion within a single system (`4.MD.A.1`), applies the four operations to measurement word problems (`4.MD.A.2`), applies area and perimeter formulas for rectangles (`4.MD.A.3`), makes line plots with fractional measurements (`4.MD.B.4`), and introduces angle measurement in degrees with a protractor (`4.MD.C.5` to `4.MD.C.7`).

## What it covers

- Solve problems involving measurement and conversion of measurements (`4.MD.A`).
- Represent and interpret data (`4.MD.B`).
- Geometric measurement: understand concepts of angle and measure angles (`4.MD.C`).

  <Accordion title="4.MD.C.5 sub-points: concepts of angle measurement">
    - **4.MD.C.5.a:** An angle is measured with reference to a circle with its center at the common endpoint of the rays, by considering the fraction of the circular arc between the points where the two rays intersect the circle. An angle that turns through 1/360 of a circle is called a "one-degree angle," and can be used to measure angles.
    - **4.MD.C.5.b:** An angle that turns through n one-degree angles is said to have an angle measure of n degrees.

## Classroom application

The conversion work in `4.MD.A.1` builds on the place value reasoning of `4.NBT.A.1`. Converting 3 meters to centimeters is 3 x 100 = 300 cm, which works because 1 meter is 100 centimeters. Students who have a sense of the relative sizes of units (a meter stick is about an adult's stride; a kilogram is about a textbook) convert more confidently than those who memorize the conversion factors.

For `4.MD.A.3`, the area and perimeter formulas should feel familiar from grade 3 (`3.MD.C.7`, `3.MD.D.8`). The grade 4 move is using the formulas as equations, including with an unknown factor: "The room has area 192 sq ft and length 12 ft. What is the width?" That problem is `192 = 12 x w`, which connects measurement to algebraic thinking.

Angles enter in `4.MD.C`. The definition in `4.MD.C.5.a` is worth slowing down on. A degree is 1/360 of a full circle. That fraction connects angle measurement to the fraction work in `4.NF`. Pattern blocks help here: a regular hexagon has interior angles of 120 degrees, which is 1/3 of 360.

The "angle measure is additive" piece of `4.MD.C.7` is the engine of every angle problem at this grade. If a 90-degree angle is split into 30 and `x`, then `x` must be 60 because 30 + 60 = 90. Diagrams with decomposed angles, and equations like 30 + x = 90, are the work.
