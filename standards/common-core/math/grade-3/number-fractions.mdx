# What are the Common Core Number & Operations: Fractions standards for Grade 3?

<div class="answer-box">
  Fractions in grade 3 (`3.NF`) is one of the two big rocks of the year. Students see a fraction `a/b` as `a` copies of the unit fraction `1/b` (`3.NF.A.1`), locate fractions on the number line as specific points and lengths (`3.NF.A.2`), and explain equivalence and compare fractions by reasoning about their size (`3.NF.A.3`). Grade 3 limits denominators to 2, 3, 4, 6, and 8.
</div>

## What it covers

- Develop understanding of fractions as numbers (`3.NF.A`).

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="3.NF.A.2 sub-points: fractions on the number line">
    - **3.NF.A.2.a:** Represent a fraction 1/b on a number line diagram by defining the interval from 0 to 1 as the whole and partitioning it into b equal parts. Recognize that each part has size 1/b and that the endpoint of the part based at 0 locates the number 1/b on the number line.
    - **3.NF.A.2.b:** Represent a fraction a/b on a number line diagram by marking off a lengths 1/b from 0. Recognize that the resulting interval has size a/b and that its endpoint locates the number a/b on the number line.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="3.NF.A.3 sub-points: equivalence and comparison">
    - **3.NF.A.3.a:** Understand two fractions as equivalent (equal) if they are the same size, or the same point on a number line.
    - **3.NF.A.3.b:** Recognize and generate simple equivalent fractions, e.g., 1/2 = 2/4, 4/6 = 2/3. Explain why the fractions are equivalent, e.g., by using a visual fraction model.
    - **3.NF.A.3.c:** Express whole numbers as fractions, and recognize fractions that are equivalent to whole numbers. Examples: Express 3 in the form 3 = 3/1; recognize that 6/1 = 6; locate 4/4 and 1 at the same point of a number line diagram.
    - **3.NF.A.3.d:** Compare two fractions with the same numerator or the same denominator by reasoning about their size. Recognize that comparisons are valid only when the two fractions refer to the same whole. Record the results of comparisons with the symbols `>`, `=`, or `<`, and justify the conclusions, e.g., by using a visual fraction model.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Classroom application

The number line is the central representation for `3.NF`. Before students draw a fraction model, they should be able to label 0, 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, and 4/4 on a number line from 0 to 1. The standard explicitly defines a fraction as the endpoint of a length from 0, not as a piece of a circle or a rectangle.

The unit fraction is the small word that does the heavy lifting. A unit fraction is `1/b` for any whole number `b`. Once students see `3/4` as three of `1/4`, comparison and equivalence become reasoning about how many `1/4`s and how big each `1/4` is. This framing makes the whole domain hang together.

For `3.NF.A.3.d`, the two cases to teach are same-numerator and same-denominator comparisons. Same-denominator: 3/4 and 5/4 share `1/4` pieces, so the one with more pieces is larger. Same-numerator: 1/4 and 1/8 share the count of 1, but the pieces are different sizes, so 1/4 (the larger piece) is larger. Both rules sit on the meaning of the unit fraction.

Whole numbers as fractions (`3.NF.A.3.c`) is small but worth a day. 3 = 3/1 and 4/4 = 1 are the two key facts. They prevent the common grade 4 error of treating whole numbers as "non-fractions" that cannot live on the same number line.

Fractions usually get four to six weeks of dedicated time in grade 3, not a single short unit. The work transfers directly to grade 4 equivalence (`4.NF.A.1`) and grade 5 fraction operations (`5.NF`).
