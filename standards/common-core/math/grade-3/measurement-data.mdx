# What are the Common Core Measurement & Data standards for Grade 3?

<div class="answer-box">
  Measurement and Data in grade 3 (`3.MD`) adds three major ideas. Students tell time to the minute and measure time intervals (`3.MD.A.1`), introduce mass and liquid volume in grams, kilograms, and liters (`3.MD.A.2`), and read scaled bar and picture graphs (`3.MD.B.3`). Area enters formally as the count of unit squares (`3.MD.C.5` through `3.MD.C.7`), connecting back to multiplication. Perimeter appears for the first time (`3.MD.D.8`).
</div>

## What it covers

- Solve problems involving measurement and estimation of intervals of time, liquid volumes, and masses of objects (`3.MD.A`).
- Represent and interpret data (`3.MD.B`).
- Geometric measurement: understand concepts of area and relate area to multiplication and to addition (`3.MD.C`).
- Geometric measurement: recognize perimeter (`3.MD.D`).

  <Accordion title="3.MD.C.5 sub-points: area as a count of unit squares">
    - **3.MD.C.5.a:** A square with side length 1 unit, called "a unit square," is said to have "one square unit" of area, and can be used to measure area.
    - **3.MD.C.5.b:** A plane figure which can be covered without gaps or overlaps by n unit squares is said to have an area of n square units.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="3.MD.C.7 sub-points: area, multiplication, and addition">
    - **3.MD.C.7.a:** Find the area of a rectangle with whole-number side lengths by tiling it, and show that the area is the same as would be found by multiplying the side lengths.
    - **3.MD.C.7.b:** Multiply side lengths to find areas of rectangles with whole-number side lengths in the context of solving real world and mathematical problems, and represent whole-number products as rectangular areas in mathematical reasoning.
    - **3.MD.C.7.c:** Use tiling to show in a concrete case that the area of a rectangle with whole-number side lengths a and (b + c) is the sum of a x b and a x c. Use area models to represent the distributive property in mathematical reasoning.
    - **3.MD.C.7.d:** Recognize area as additive. Find areas of rectilinear figures by decomposing them into non-overlapping rectangles and adding the areas of the non-overlapping parts, applying this technique to solve real world problems.

## Classroom application

The area work in `3.MD.C` is among the most important measurement work in elementary math. The standard explicitly asks students to tile first (count unit squares), then connect that count to multiplication (`3.MD.C.7.a`, `3.MD.C.7.b`), then use area models to represent the distributive property (`3.MD.C.7.c`). The sequence matters. Students who jump straight to "length times width" miss the array connection that makes area a multiplication idea.

The "same perimeter, different area" / "same area, different perimeter" piece of `3.MD.D.8` is a small but powerful conceptual move. A 1-by-6 rectangle and a 2-by-3 rectangle both have a perimeter of 14, but their areas are different. Treating this as an explore-and-notice routine builds `MP7` (look for and make use of structure).

For `3.MD.A.1`, time intervals are best solved on an open number line. A problem like "Recess started at 10:42 and lasted 25 minutes. When did it end?" becomes a series of jumps: 10:42 to 11:00 (18 minutes), 11:00 to 11:07 (7 minutes), totaling 25 minutes. The number line connects elapsed time to the same number line work in `2.MD.B.6`.

The fractional-inch line plot in `3.MD.B.4` is small but worth including. It connects measurement to the new fraction work in `3.NF` and previews the line plot work in grades 4 and 5.
