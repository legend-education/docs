# What are the Common Core Math standards for Grade 3?

<div class="answer-box">
  Grade 3 is the biggest jump in elementary math. Multiplication and division within 100 (`3.OA.C.7`) become the major work of the year, paired with the introduction of fractions as numbers on the number line (`3.NF.A.1`, `3.NF.A.2`). Students also start formal area as the count of unit squares, learn the perimeter of polygons, and reach fluent addition and subtraction within 1000. This is the year state math testing typically begins.

## What it covers

- Operations and Algebraic Thinking (`3.OA`): the meaning of multiplication and division, word problems, fact fluency within 100, and two-step problems.
- Number and Operations in Base Ten (`3.NBT`): place value to 1000, fluent addition and subtraction within 1000, and multiplying one-digit numbers by multiples of 10.
- Number and Operations: Fractions (`3.NF`): fractions as numbers on the number line, equivalence, and comparing fractions with the same numerator or denominator.
- Measurement and Data (`3.MD`): time to the minute, mass and volume, area as the count of unit squares, perimeter, and bar and picture graphs.
- Geometry (`3.G`): classifying quadrilaterals and partitioning shapes into equal areas.

## What changes this year

Grade 3 is the year the elementary math story turns. Multiplication and division enter as new operations with their own meaning (`3.OA.A.1`, `3.OA.A.2`), their own word problem types (`3.OA.A.3`), and a fact fluency target by the end of the year (`3.OA.C.7`). All of this happens within 100.

Fractions arrive as numbers (`3.NF.A.1`). Before grade 3, fractions were parts of a whole. Now `1/3` is a number that lives between 0 and 1 on the number line, with a specific location (`3.NF.A.2`). This shift is the foundation for grades 4 and 5 fraction work.

Area arrives as a new measurement idea (`3.MD.C.5`, `3.MD.C.6`, `3.MD.C.7`). Students count unit squares, then connect that count to the multiplication of side lengths. Perimeter (`3.MD.D.8`) is also new at this grade.

Grade 3 is also when state math testing usually begins. Most state tests focus heavily on `3.OA` (multiplication and division word problems) and `3.NF` (fractions on the number line). Teaching both well is the highest-leverage move of the year.

## Classroom application

A typical grade 3 math block dedicates the majority of the year's time to `3.OA` and `3.NF`. The pattern that works at most schools is a fluency block focused on multiplication and division facts, a problem-based lesson that builds the meanings of those operations, and a fraction unit that runs for several weeks rather than a few days.

The most useful representation for grade 3 multiplication is the array. An array of 6 rows by 4 columns lets students see 6 x 4 as repeated addition (six 4s), as area (24 unit squares), and as the multiplication of side lengths. Arrays also support `MP7` (look for and make use of structure), since they expose the distributive property: 6 x 7 = 6 x 5 + 6 x 2.

For fractions, the number line is the highest-leverage tool. A 0-to-1 line partitioned into thirds shows that 1/3 is a specific number, that 2/3 is twice as far from 0 as 1/3, and that 3/3 = 1. The grade 3 fraction work that runs on the number line transfers directly to grade 4 equivalence and grade 5 operations.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="What does 'fluency' mean in 3.OA.C.7?">
    `3.OA.C.7` asks students to fluently multiply and divide within 100 and to know from memory all products of two one-digit numbers by the end of grade 3. Fluency means accurate, efficient, and flexible: the student gets the right answer, gets it quickly, and can use a strategy when the fact has not yet stuck. "Know from memory" is the harder target. Most students get there through repeated meaningful use, not through flash cards alone.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Should I teach fractions before multiplication, or after?">
    Most curricula start with multiplication and division, then move to fractions in the winter or early spring. The reason is that fractions sit on top of multiplication thinking: equal partitioning of a whole into thirds is a multiplication idea. Teaching `3.OA` first gives students the equal-groups thinking they need to make sense of `3.NF`. By April, the two often weave together, with area work in `3.MD` connecting back to both.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
