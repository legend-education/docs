# What are the Common Core Number & Operations in Base Ten standards for Grade 3?

<div class="answer-box">
  Number and Operations in Base Ten in grade 3 (`3.NBT`) is a small but important domain. Students round to the nearest 10 or 100 using place value reasoning (`3.NBT.A.1`), reach fluency with addition and subtraction within 1000 (`3.NBT.A.2`), and multiply one-digit numbers by multiples of 10 from 10 to 90 (`3.NBT.A.3`, like 7 x 80). The work supports the multiplication and division of `3.OA` and sets up multi-digit multiplication in grade 4.

## What it covers

- Use place value understanding and properties of operations to perform multi-digit arithmetic (`3.NBT.A`).

## Classroom application

The rounding work in `3.NBT.A.1` is best built on the open number line. Mark the number to be rounded, mark the two nearest multiples of 10 (or 100), and ask which is closer. The midpoint convention is part of the standard but does not need a separate lesson; if a student lands exactly halfway, the convention is to round up. The point of rounding is estimation, which feeds the reasonableness check in `3.OA.D.8`.

Fluency within 1000 (`3.NBT.A.2`) builds on grade 2 strategies but with larger numbers. Open number lines, place value drawings, and partial-sum methods all work. The standard algorithm is still a grade 4 standard; grade 3 should make place value visible in any written method.

`3.NBT.A.3` is small but powerful. Multiplying 7 x 80 should not be a memorized rule about adding zeros. Students see 7 x 80 as 7 x 8 tens, which is 56 tens, which is 560. That reasoning relies on the work students did in `3.OA` and prepares them for grade 4 multi-digit multiplication.

`3.NBT` is a supporting domain, not the major work of grade 3. Most teachers thread these three standards through the year rather than teaching them as a single unit: rounding sits with estimation practice, fluency lives in number talks, and multiplying by multiples of 10 shows up inside the `3.OA` unit.
