# What are the Common Core Geometry standards for Grade 3?

<div class="answer-box">
  Geometry in grade 3 (`3.G`) sharpens shape classification and connects geometry to fractions. Students see shared attributes across categories (`3.G.A.1`, like "rhombuses, rectangles, and others are quadrilaterals"), and they partition shapes into parts with equal areas, naming each part as a unit fraction of the whole (`3.G.A.2`).

## What it covers

- Reason with shapes and their attributes (`3.G.A`).

## Classroom application

`3.G.A.1` introduces hierarchy in shape classification. Students learn that "quadrilateral" is a category big enough to include rhombuses, rectangles, and squares (and many irregular four-sided shapes). The classroom move that builds the most insight is a sorting routine: a set of mixed quadrilaterals gets sorted into named groups, and then the named groups get pulled back into the larger "quadrilateral" pile.

The standard also asks students to draw quadrilaterals that do not belong to any of the named subcategories. This works against the prototype problem (a quadrilateral is a square or a rectangle). A trapezoid or a kite is a quadrilateral with no special name in grade 3. Drawing them on purpose builds the category idea.

`3.G.A.2` is small but connects two of the big grade 3 ideas. A rectangle partitioned into 8 equal-area pieces gives 8 pieces, each `1/8` of the whole. The standard explicitly uses the unit fraction language from `3.NF.A.1`. This is the year fractions and geometry start to talk to each other; that conversation continues in grade 4's equivalence work and grade 5's area-model fraction multiplication.

The grade 3 hierarchy work is also the foundation for `5.G.B.4` (classifying figures in a hierarchy based on properties), so the careful language at this grade pays off later.
