# What are the Common Core math standards for high school?

<div class="answer-box">
  Common Core math for high school is not organized by grade. Instead, all high school math sits in 6 conceptual categories: Number and Quantity, Algebra, Functions, Modeling, Geometry, and Statistics and Probability. Schools build their own course sequences on top of the categories. A traditional pathway uses Algebra I, Geometry, and Algebra II. An integrated pathway weaves all categories across Mathematics I, II, and III. A small number of standards marked with `(+)` are advanced and intended for students taking precalculus.
</div>

## How high school math is organized

Common Core math through grade 8 follows the grade level. High school does not. Instead, the standards group every high school standard into one of 6 conceptual categories. The categories describe what students should know and be able to do by the end of high school. They do not prescribe which year each idea is taught.

Schools build a course sequence on top of the categories. Two common pathways:

| Pathway | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Traditional | Algebra I | Geometry | Algebra II |
| Integrated | Mathematics I | Mathematics II | Mathematics III |

Both pathways cover all 6 categories by the end of the third year. The Common Core document itself does not require one pathway over the other.

## How to read a high school math code

The prefix tells you the category, the domain, and the standard.

| Prefix | Conceptual category |
| --- | --- |
| `HSN` | Number and Quantity |
| `HSA` | Algebra |
| `HSF` | Functions |
| `HSG` | Geometry |
| `HSS` | Statistics and Probability |

Modeling does not have its own prefix. Modeling standards appear inside the other categories, marked with a star (★). Modeling also functions as its own category in the standards document. See the [Modeling page](/standards/common-core/math/high-school/modeling) for more.

### Worked example

```text
HSA.REI.B.4
└─┬─┘ └┬┘ │ │
  │    │  │ └── standard number within the cluster
  │    │  └──── cluster letter (B)
  │    └─────── domain (Reasoning with Equations and Inequalities)
  └──────────── conceptual category (Algebra)
```

Read this as a standard in the Algebra conceptual category, in the Reasoning with Equations and Inequalities domain. The standard asks students to solve quadratic equations in one variable.

<Tip>
  A `(+)` after a standard marks it as advanced. The plus standards extend the topic for students who will take precalculus or another higher-level course. They are not required for college and career readiness.
</Tip>

## Major topics by course (traditional pathway)

The traditional Algebra I, Geometry, Algebra II pathway groups standards roughly as follows. The exact placement varies by state.

| Course | Major topics from the conceptual categories |
| --- | --- |
| Algebra I | Linear and exponential functions, systems, quadratic functions, polynomial arithmetic, descriptive statistics. |
| Geometry | Congruence and similarity through transformations, proof, trigonometry of right triangles, circles, three-dimensional figures. |
| Algebra II | Polynomial, rational, radical, and trigonometric functions, complex numbers, statistical inference. |

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Why are high school standards not organized by grade?">
    Different states and districts run different high school pathways. A grade 9 student in one state might be in Algebra I; in another state, in Geometry; in a third, in Mathematics I. The conceptual categories let the standards describe the math without prescribing the path. Schools (and their states) decide the order and the courses.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What does the star (★) mean on a standard?">
    The star marks a modeling standard. The intent is that students apply the standard to model a real situation, not just to do procedural work. A standard like `HSA.CED.A.1` (creating equations) is marked with a star because students should create equations that describe real-world situations and use them to solve problems. Modeling is also a conceptual category in its own right and runs across all of high school math.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What are the (+) standards?">
    The plus standards are advanced. They are intended for students who will take precalculus, calculus, or other advanced courses. They include topics like vectors and matrices, complex roots of polynomials, and conic sections beyond the basics. They are part of Common Core but not required for college and career readiness in math.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
