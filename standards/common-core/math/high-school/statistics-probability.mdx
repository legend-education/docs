# What are the Common Core high school Statistics & Probability standards?

<div class="answer-box">
  Statistics and Probability in high school is a conceptual category with four domains: Interpreting Categorical and Quantitative Data (`S-ID`), Making Inferences and Justifying Conclusions (`S-IC`), Conditional Probability and the Rules of Probability (`S-CP`), and Using Probability to Make Decisions (`S-MD`). The work moves from describing data, through inferring conclusions about populations, through formal probability rules, to decision-making under uncertainty.

## What it covers

- Interpreting Categorical and Quantitative Data: summarize, represent, and interpret data on a single count or measurement variable; summarize, represent, and interpret data on two categorical and quantitative variables; interpret linear models (`S-ID`).
- Making Inferences and Justifying Conclusions: understand and evaluate random processes underlying statistical experiments; make inferences and justify conclusions from sample surveys, experiments, and observational studies (`S-IC`).
- Conditional Probability and the Rules of Probability: understand independence and conditional probability and use them to interpret data; use the rules of probability to compute probabilities of compound events in a uniform probability model (`S-CP`).
- Using Probability to Make Decisions: calculate expected values and use them to solve problems; use probability to evaluate outcomes of decisions (`S-MD`).

## Classroom application

`S-ID.A` continues work from middle school on summarizing one-variable data. The new content at high school is the normal distribution (`S-ID.A.4`). Students compute mean and standard deviation, fit a normal model where appropriate, and use the empirical rule (about 68 percent of data within one standard deviation, 95 percent within two, 99.7 percent within three). The standard is explicit that not all data are normal; recognizing skew, multimodality, or heavy tails matters.

`S-ID.B.5` extends two-way categorical work from grade 8 (`8.SP.A.4`). Joint, marginal, and conditional relative frequencies all live in a two-way table. A useful classroom routine is a two-way table of grade level by extracurricular participation. Joint relative frequency is "the fraction of all students who are both ninth-graders and athletes." Marginal is "the fraction of all students who are ninth-graders." Conditional is "the fraction of ninth-graders who are athletes."

`S-ID.B.6` brings function-fitting to data. Students fit linear, quadratic, or exponential models depending on the context, then assess fit by plotting residuals (observed minus predicted). A residual plot that looks like random scatter around zero indicates a good fit. A residual plot with a curved pattern indicates a wrong model family.

`S-ID.C.9` is one of the most important standards in the entire category: correlation is not causation. Two variables that move together may share a cause, may share a confounding variable, or may simply happen to align in this data. Establishing causation requires a randomized experiment, not observation. The standard's classroom payoff is helping students read media reports critically.

`S-IC.A.1` is the conceptual frame for the whole inference cluster. Statistics is described as a process: a sample is taken from a population, a parameter is estimated, and inference is made back to the population. The key insight is that random sampling supports inference, while convenience samples do not.

`S-IC.A.2` introduces simulation as the workhorse for inference at this level. A claim that a coin is fair can be tested by simulating many fair-coin sequences and asking how often a result as extreme as the observed one would occur by chance. This is the conceptual heart of significance testing without the formal-test machinery.

`S-IC.B.3` distinguishes three study designs: sample survey (estimating a parameter), experiment (comparing treatments under random assignment), and observational study (looking at associations without intervention). Only experiments support causal inference; surveys and observational studies support association only. This connects directly back to `S-ID.C.9`.

`S-CP.A.2` and `S-CP.A.3` introduce the formal definitions of independence and conditional probability. Two events are independent if and only if P(A and B) = P(A)P(B). Conditional probability P(A given B) = P(A and B)/P(B). The classroom routine is a two-way table where students compute joint, marginal, and conditional probabilities and check whether independence holds.

`S-CP.A.5` is the cluster's most accessible standard: explain conditional probability in everyday language. The standard's example contrasts P(cancer given smoker) with P(smoker given cancer). These are different quantities. Reasoning about which one is relevant to a medical decision is the kind of work the standard targets.

`S-MD` is mostly plus-standards on random variables, expected value, and probability-based decisions. The decision-theoretic framing (`S-MD.B.5`) is unique to this cluster: a game with high variance but a positive expected value is favorable in the long run, but might be too risky for a single play. Comparing strategies by expected value is the central move.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Which standards are required for every student?">
    All non-plus standards in `S-ID`, `S-IC`, and `S-CP.A` and `S-CP.B.6`, `S-CP.B.7` are required. Plus-standards are `S-CP.B.8`, `S-CP.B.9`, and all of `S-MD`.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Where do these standards live in a typical sequence?">
    `S-ID` typically appears in Algebra 1 (one-variable data, two-way tables, scatter plots). `S-IC` and `S-CP` typically appear in Algebra 2. Plus-standards on expected value and probability-based decisions appear in pre-calculus, statistics electives, or AP Statistics.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Do students need to do formal hypothesis testing?">
    The standards do not require formal-test machinery (z-tests, t-tests, p-values). They require informal inference using simulation (`S-IC.A.2`, `S-IC.B.4`, `S-IC.B.5`). Formal hypothesis testing belongs to AP Statistics or college courses.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How does this relate to MP4 and Modeling?">
    Many `S-ID`, `S-IC`, and `S-MD` standards are star-marked, meaning they are modeling standards. The work of fitting a model to data, interpreting residuals, and using expected value to evaluate decisions is modeling under the high-school category framing. MP4 (model with mathematics) is the underlying practice.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

  The Common Core high school Statistics and Probability conceptual category covers four domains. In Interpreting Categorical and Quantitative Data (`S-ID`), students summarize one- and two-variable data, fit functions to scatter plots, interpret linear models, and distinguish correlation from causation.
