# What are the Common Core high school Algebra standards?

<div class="answer-box">
  Algebra in high school is a conceptual category with four domains: Seeing Structure in Expressions (`A-SSE`), Arithmetic with Polynomials and Rational Expressions (`A-APR`), Creating Equations (`A-CED`), and Reasoning with Equations and Inequalities (`A-REI`). The work is about interpreting expressions in terms of their structure, performing polynomial arithmetic, building equations to model relationships, and solving equations and systems with justification.

## What it covers

- Seeing Structure in Expressions: interpret the structure; write expressions in equivalent forms to solve problems (`A-SSE`).
- Arithmetic with Polynomials and Rational Expressions: perform operations; understand the relationship between zeros and factors; use polynomial identities; rewrite rational expressions (`A-APR`).
- Creating Equations: create equations that describe numbers or relationships (`A-CED`).
- Reasoning with Equations and Inequalities: understand solving as a process of reasoning; solve equations and inequalities in one variable; solve systems; represent and solve graphically (`A-REI`).

  <Accordion title="A-SSE.A.1 sub-points">
    `A-SSE.A.1.a` Interpret parts of an expression, such as terms, factors, and coefficients.

    `A-SSE.A.1.b` Interpret complicated expressions by viewing one or more of their parts as a single entity. For example, interpret P(1 + r) to the n as the product of P and a factor not depending on P.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="A-SSE.B.3 sub-points">
    `A-SSE.B.3.a` Factor a quadratic expression to reveal the zeros of the function it defines.

    `A-SSE.B.3.b` Complete the square in a quadratic expression to reveal the maximum or minimum value of the function it defines.

    `A-SSE.B.3.c` Use the properties of exponents to transform expressions for exponential functions. For example, rewrite (1.15) to the t as ((1.15) to the 1/12) to the (12t) approximately equal to (1.012) to the (12t) to reveal the approximate equivalent monthly interest rate if the annual rate is 15%.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="A-REI.B.4 sub-points: solving quadratics">
    `A-REI.B.4.a` Use the method of completing the square to transform any quadratic equation in x into an equation of the form (x - p) squared = q that has the same solutions. Derive the quadratic formula from this form.

    `A-REI.B.4.b` Solve quadratic equations by inspection (e.g., for x squared = 49), taking square roots, completing the square, the quadratic formula and factoring, as appropriate to the initial form of the equation. Recognize when the quadratic formula gives complex solutions and write them as a plus or minus bi for real numbers a and b.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Classroom application

`A-SSE` is the structural-vision domain. A useful classroom routine is "what do you see?" for an expression like x to the fourth minus 16. Some students see x to the fourth and 16 separately; others see (x squared) squared minus 4 squared, a difference of squares. The standard rewards the second view because it suggests factoring. `A-SSE.B.3` is the operational standard: factor a quadratic to find zeros, complete the square to find a vertex, rewrite an exponential to interpret growth rate.

`A-APR` is the polynomial-arithmetic domain. `A-APR.A.1` makes the closure analogy explicit: polynomials behave under addition, subtraction, and multiplication the way integers do; rational expressions behave the way rational numbers do (`A-APR.D.7+`). The Remainder Theorem (`A-APR.B.2`) is the high-leverage tool for checking whether (x - a) is a factor of p(x): just compute p(a). If zero, it is a factor.

`A-CED` is where modeling lives within Algebra. Students take a verbal description ("a phone plan charges $20 setup plus $5 per month") and produce an equation (C = 20 + 5m). They check feasibility by interpreting the variables, including domain restrictions (m must be a nonnegative whole number).

`A-REI.A.1` asks students to justify each step in solving an equation. This is the algebraic version of geometric proof. Given 2x + 3 = 11, subtracting 3 from both sides is justified by "if equals are subtracted from equals, the results are equal." Dividing both sides by 2 is justified similarly. This explicit reasoning supports MP3 (construct viable arguments).

`A-REI.B.4` covers quadratic solving methods. Four methods (inspection, square roots, completing the square, factoring, quadratic formula) are introduced. Selecting the right one for the form at hand is the strategic skill; the formula always works but is often overkill. Completing the square is the conceptual bridge to deriving the quadratic formula.

`A-REI.C.5` proves that elimination preserves the solution set of a system. This formal result undergirds the elimination method students used in grade 8 (`8.EE.C.8`). `A-REI.D.10` and `A-REI.D.11` give the geometric view: solving f(x) = g(x) means finding where the graphs intersect.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Which standards are required for all students versus advanced students?">
    Standards marked (+) are for students preparing for advanced courses. In Algebra, the plus standards are `A-SSE.B.4`, `A-APR.C.5`, `A-APR.D.7`, `A-REI.C.8`, and `A-REI.C.9`. All other Algebra standards are required for college- and career-ready students.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Where do these standards live in a traditional course sequence?">
    Most of `A-SSE.A`, `A-CED`, `A-REI.B.3`, and `A-REI.C.6` appear in Algebra 1. Quadratics (`A-REI.B.4`), polynomial work (`A-APR.A`, `A-APR.B`), and some of `A-SSE.B.3` appear in Algebra 1 and Algebra 2 depending on the curriculum. Rational expressions (`A-APR.D`) and quadratic systems (`A-REI.C.7`) typically appear in Algebra 2.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How does this category relate to high school Functions?">
    Algebra deals with expressions, equations, and inequalities. Functions deals with the function concept: rule, domain, range, behavior, families. The same algebraic objects (linear, quadratic, exponential) show up in both categories, but the lens is different. A quadratic expression in Algebra becomes a quadratic function in Functions when you assign each input an output.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

<div class="canonical-summary">
  The Common Core high school Algebra conceptual category covers four domains. In Seeing Structure in Expressions (`A-SSE`), students interpret parts of an expression and rewrite expressions in equivalent forms to reveal properties such as zeros, vertices, and growth rates. In Arithmetic with Polynomials and Rational Expressions (`A-APR`), they perform polynomial arithmetic, apply the Remainder Theorem, and rewrite rational expressions. In Creating Equations (`A-CED`), they build equations, inequalities, and systems to model real situations and rearrange formulas. In Reasoning with Equations and Inequalities (`A-REI`), they justify steps in solving, solve linear and quadratic equations and inequalities, solve systems algebraically and graphically, and graph solution sets of inequalities.
