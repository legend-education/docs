# What are the Common Core high school Geometry standards?

<div class="answer-box">
  Geometry in high school is a conceptual category with six domains: Congruence (`G-CO`), Similarity, Right Triangles, and Trigonometry (`G-SRT`), Circles (`G-C`), Expressing Geometric Properties with Equations (`G-GPE`), Geometric Measurement and Dimension (`G-GMD`), and Modeling with Geometry (`G-MG`). The course is built on rigid motions and dilations from grade 8 (`8.G.A`): students prove geometric theorems using transformations rather than the traditional axiomatic approach.
</div>

## What it covers

- Congruence: experiment with transformations in the plane; understand congruence in terms of rigid motions; prove geometric theorems; make geometric constructions (`G-CO`).
- Similarity, Right Triangles, and Trigonometry: understand similarity in terms of similarity transformations; prove theorems involving similarity; define trigonometric ratios and solve problems involving right triangles; apply trigonometry to general triangles (`G-SRT`).
- Circles: understand and apply theorems about circles; find arc lengths and areas of sectors of circles (`G-C`).
- Expressing Geometric Properties with Equations: translate between the geometric description and the equation for a conic section; use coordinates to prove simple geometric theorems algebraically (`G-GPE`).
- Geometric Measurement and Dimension: explain volume formulas and use them to solve problems; visualize relationships between two-dimensional and three-dimensional objects (`G-GMD`).
- Modeling with Geometry: apply geometric concepts in modeling situations (`G-MG`).

## Classroom application

`G-CO.A` introduces the transformations approach. Students start with precise definitions and use them to define rigid motions formally. A rotation is defined by a center and an angle. A reflection is defined by a line. A translation is defined by a vector (or a directed segment). These formal definitions support `G-CO.B`, where congruence is defined as "the existence of a rigid motion sequence that maps one figure to the other."

`G-CO.B.8` is the conceptual capstone of the congruence cluster. The familiar triangle-congruence criteria (ASA, SAS, SSS) are not axioms in Common Core Geometry; they are theorems derived from the rigid-motions definition. The proof of SAS, for instance, starts by placing one of the congruent angles at the origin with one leg along the positive x-axis; rigid motions can always do this. Then the side lengths force the other two vertices to coincide.

`G-CO.C` is the formal proof cluster. Students prove theorems about lines and angles (vertical angles congruent, alternate interior angles when parallel lines are cut by a transversal), about triangles (angle sum 180 degrees, isosceles base angles, midsegment), and about parallelograms (opposite sides congruent, diagonals bisect). Each proof should appeal to either rigid-motions or to previously proven results.

`G-CO.D.12` and `G-CO.D.13` are the construction standards. Compass-and-straightedge constructions copy a segment, copy an angle, bisect a segment, bisect an angle, construct perpendiculars and parallels. These are not just historical artifacts; they ground the rigid-motions theory in concrete tools.

`G-SRT` is the similarity-and-trigonometry domain. `G-SRT.A.1` extends the rigid-motions toolkit to include dilations. `G-SRT.A.2` defines similarity as "rigid motions plus a dilation." `G-SRT.B.4` proves the Pythagorean theorem using similar triangles (different from grade 8's proof). `G-SRT.C.6` is the conceptual foundation of trigonometry: because all right triangles with a given acute angle are similar, the side ratios depend only on the angle, not on the size of the triangle. This is what makes sine, cosine, and tangent well-defined as functions of the angle.

`G-C` is the circles domain. `G-C.A.1` ("all circles are similar") is a one-sentence theorem with a one-sentence proof: given two circles, dilate the first by the ratio of the radii, then translate the center to match. `G-C.A.2` is the angle-and-chord cluster: inscribed angles on the same arc are congruent; inscribed angles on a diameter are right; a radius to a tangent point is perpendicular to the tangent. `G-C.B.5` derives the radian definition.

`G-GPE` is the coordinate-algebraic domain. `G-GPE.A.1` derives the circle equation (x - h) squared + (y - k) squared = r squared from the Pythagorean theorem and the distance formula. `G-GPE.B.4` and `G-GPE.B.5` ask for coordinate proofs: place a figure in the plane, find slopes and distances, conclude that the figure is a rectangle (or not).

`G-GMD` is the measurement-and-dimension domain. `G-GMD.A.1` asks for informal arguments for area and volume formulas, using dissection (cutting and rearranging), Cavalieri's principle (two solids with equal cross-section areas at every height have equal volume), and limiting arguments (a circle's area as a limit of inscribed polygons). `G-GMD.B.4` is the cross-section-and-rotation cluster: a vertical slice through a sphere is a circle; a rectangle rotated about one side traces out a cylinder.

`G-MG` is modeling with geometry. These three star-standards live entirely in real-world applications: estimating tree volume, computing population density, designing typographic grids. They overlap heavily with the Modeling category.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How is this Geometry course different from the one I was taught?">
    The biggest difference is the foundation. Traditional Euclidean geometry takes a list of axioms (any two points determine a line, etc.) and uses them to prove theorems. Common Core Geometry takes rigid motions and dilations as the foundation. Congruence is defined as "there is a rigid motion mapping one figure to the other," and similarity is "rigid motions plus a dilation." Triangle congruence criteria like SAS become theorems instead of axioms. The proofs and the content overlap heavily, but the framing is different.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Where does a typical Geometry course place these standards?">
    Most of `G-CO`, `G-SRT.A`, `G-SRT.B`, `G-SRT.C`, `G-C.A`, and `G-GPE.A.1` plus `G-GPE.B` appear in a one-year Geometry course (between Algebra 1 and Algebra 2 in a traditional sequence). `G-GMD` and `G-MG` appear there too. The plus-standards (`G-SRT.D`, `G-C.A.4`, `G-GPE.A.3`, `G-GMD.A.2`) typically appear in pre-calculus.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Is two-column proof required?">
    The standards do not specify a proof format. Two-column proofs, paragraph proofs, flowchart proofs, and informal arguments are all valid. The standards do require students to construct viable arguments (MP3) and use precise reasoning. Many teachers use paragraph proofs for theorems about lines and angles, and two-column proofs for triangle congruence work.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

<div class="canonical-summary">
  The Common Core high school Geometry conceptual category covers six domains. In Congruence (`G-CO`), students use rigid motions to define congruence, prove theorems about lines, angles, triangles, and parallelograms, and perform compass-and-straightedge constructions. In Similarity, Right Triangles, and Trigonometry (`G-SRT`), they extend transformations to dilations, prove triangle-similarity criteria, and use right-triangle trig in applied problems. In Circles (`G-C`), they prove all circles are similar, work with inscribed and central angles, and derive radian measure. In Expressing Geometric Properties with Equations (`G-GPE`), they derive equations for circles and parabolas and use coordinates to prove theorems. In Geometric Measurement and Dimension (`G-GMD`), they give informal arguments for area and volume formulas and identify cross-sections of 3D objects. In Modeling with Geometry (`G-MG`), they apply geometric methods to real-world objects and design problems. Source: corestandards.org/Math/Content/HSG.
