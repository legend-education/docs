# What are the Common Core high school Number & Quantity standards?

<div class="answer-box">
  Number and Quantity in high school is a conceptual category, not a single course. Its four domains extend numbers: real numbers and rational exponents (`N-RN`), quantities and units (`N-Q`), the complex number system (`N-CN`), and vectors and matrix quantities (`N-VM`). Standards marked with a plus sign (+) are additional material for students preparing for advanced courses such as calculus or statistics; everything else is expected for all college- and career-ready students.

## What it covers

- The Real Number System: extend the properties of exponents to rational exponents; use properties of rational and irrational numbers (`N-RN`).
- Quantities: reason quantitatively and use units to solve problems (`N-Q`).
- The Complex Number System: perform arithmetic with complex numbers; represent them and operations on them; use them in polynomial identities and equations (`N-CN`).
- Vector and Matrix Quantities: represent and model with vector quantities; perform operations on vectors; perform operations on matrices and use matrices in applications (`N-VM`).

## Classroom application

`N-RN.A` extends what students learned in grade 8 about integer exponents (`8.EE.A.1`). The key conceptual move is that fractional exponents are defined to make the exponent rules continue to hold. If 5 to the 1/3 means the cube root of 5, then (5 to the 1/3) cubed = 5 to the 1, which is just 5. That is exactly the property of cube roots. A useful prompt is to ask students to write sqrt(x) using a rational exponent (x to the 1/2) and verify that x to the 1/2 times x to the 1/2 = x.

`N-Q` is the conceptual home of "modeling with units." A useful classroom routine is to assign messy real-world problems and require students to track units through every step (cubic feet, miles per hour squared, dollars per gallon). When the units of the final answer match the units asked for, the work is checked.

`N-CN.A` introduces the imaginary unit i. The motivation is that some quadratics, like x squared + 1 = 0, have no real solutions; defining i so that i squared = -1 gives those quadratics solutions. Arithmetic on complex numbers proceeds by treating i as a variable and replacing i squared with -1 whenever it appears. The standard `N-CN.C.7` is the only required standard in this domain for all students; the rest are plus-standards for advanced courses.

`N-VM` is almost entirely plus-standards. Vectors and matrices appear in calculus, statistics, and physics tracks. The teaching arc for vectors moves from arrows on paper (magnitude and direction), to coordinate components (a vector from (1, 2) to (4, 6) has components (3, 4)), to algebraic operations (adding two vectors, scaling by a constant). Matrices move from data organization (rows and columns) to operations to transformations of the plane.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="What does the plus sign (+) on a standard mean?">
    A plus sign indicates a standard that is additional material for students preparing for advanced math courses (calculus, statistics, discrete math). It is not required for all students to be college- and career-ready. Most plus-standards live in the Number & Quantity (vectors, matrices, complex numbers beyond solving) and Functions (trigonometric and inverse functions) categories. Schools and districts choose which plus-standards to include.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Which Number & Quantity standards are required for every student?">
    The required (non-plus) standards are `N-RN.A.1`, `N-RN.A.2`, `N-RN.B.3`, `N-Q.A.1`, `N-Q.A.2`, `N-Q.A.3`, `N-CN.A.1`, `N-CN.A.2`, and `N-CN.C.7`. Everything else in this category is marked (+) and is for advanced tracks.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Where do these standards typically live in a course sequence?">
    In a traditional sequence, `N-RN` and `N-Q` work appears in Algebra 1. Complex numbers (`N-CN.A` and `N-CN.C.7`) appear in Algebra 2. Plus-standards on complex numbers, vectors, and matrices appear in pre-calculus or beyond. In an integrated sequence, the work is distributed across courses but the same standards are covered.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
