# What are the Common Core Geometry standards for Kindergarten?

<div class="answer-box">
  Geometry in kindergarten (`K.G`) is about naming, describing, and building shapes. Students identify and name squares, circles, triangles, rectangles, hexagons, cubes, cones, cylinders, and spheres regardless of size or orientation (`K.G.A.2`). They use position words like "above," "below," "beside," and "behind" (`K.G.A.1`). They compare shapes by their parts and analyze attributes (`K.G.B.4`), and they build new shapes by combining smaller ones (`K.G.B.6`).
</div>

## What it covers

- Identify and describe shapes (`K.G.A`).
- Analyze, compare, create, and compose shapes (`K.G.B`).

## Classroom application

`K.G.A.2` is more subtle than it sounds. A student who calls every three-sided shape a triangle has learned the prototype but may struggle when a triangle is rotated, very thin, or very long. Use a variety of triangles (right, obtuse, acute, isosceles, scalene, in any orientation) from day one so students learn that a triangle is "three straight sides," not "the picture on the chart."

The flat vs. solid distinction in `K.G.A.3` is easier when students sort real objects. A can is a cylinder. A ball is a sphere. A box is a rectangular prism. Pair the solids with their faces (the top of a can is a circle; the side of a box is a rectangle) to set up the grade 1 work in `1.G.A.2`.

Composing shapes (`K.G.B.6`) is where geometry meets play. Pattern blocks let students put two trapezoids together to make a hexagon, or two squares to make a rectangle. The classroom move that builds the most thinking is asking, "What other shapes can you make from these pieces?" That single question runs the standard.

This domain is small but appears across the year in centers and small groups rather than in a single concentrated unit.
