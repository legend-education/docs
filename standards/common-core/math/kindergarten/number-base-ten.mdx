# What are the Common Core Number & Operations in Base Ten standards for Kindergarten?

<div class="answer-box">
  Number and Operations in Base Ten in kindergarten is small but important. There is one cluster and one standard. Students see the numbers 11 through 19 as made of "ten ones and some more ones" (`K.NBT.A.1`). The work is the seed of place value, the idea that 14 is not just the number after 13 but is also 10 + 4. This insight powers every grade of place value work that follows.
</div>

## What it covers

- Work with numbers 11 to 19 to gain foundations for place value (`K.NBT.A`).

## Standards

## Classroom application

The double ten-frame is the highest-leverage tool for `K.NBT.A.1`. Students build the number 14 as a full ten-frame and a four-frame next to it. The full ten-frame on the left is permanent; the four on the right changes as the number changes. After many days of building, students start saying "ten and four" before they say "fourteen," and the place value insight takes root.

Equations like 14 = 10 + 4 should appear with the work. The standard explicitly mentions recording the composition by a drawing or equation. Writing the equation alongside the drawing builds the habit of pairing representation with notation.

Watch for the "teen number trap": some students see 14 as "one and four" because the digits look like 1 and 4. The fix is repeated experience with the ten-frame so that the 1 is felt as a full ten, not as a one.

This single standard takes weeks, not days. Most kindergarten units treat `K.NBT.A.1` as a recurring thread through the spring rather than a single short unit. By June, students who can compose and decompose teens fluently arrive in grade 1 ready for two-digit place value (`1.NBT.B.2`).
