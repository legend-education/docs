# What are the Common Core Counting & Cardinality standards for Kindergarten?

<div class="answer-box">
  The Counting and Cardinality domain (`K.CC`) is the heart of kindergarten math. Students learn the number names through 100 (`K.CC.A.1`), count up to 20 objects without skipping or recounting (`K.CC.B.5`), and compare small groups by counting or matching (`K.CC.C.6`). The work moves from reciting numbers to using them as tools for measuring quantity and comparing groups.
</div>

## What it covers

- Know number names and the count sequence (`K.CC.A`).
- Count to tell the number of objects (`K.CC.B`).
- Compare numbers (`K.CC.C`).

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="K.CC.B.4 sub-points: connect counting to cardinality">
    - **K.CC.B.4.a:** When counting objects, say the number names in the standard order, pairing each object with one and only one number name and each number name with one and only one object.
    - **K.CC.B.4.b:** Understand that the last number name said tells the number of objects counted. The number of objects is the same regardless of their arrangement or the order in which they were counted.
    - **K.CC.B.4.c:** Understand that each successive number name refers to a quantity that is one larger.
  </Accordion>

## Classroom application

`K.CC` is the most "do it every day" domain in elementary math. Choral counting builds `K.CC.A.1` and `K.CC.A.2`. Counting collections (a bag of beans, buttons, or links that students count and record) builds `K.CC.B.4` and `K.CC.B.5` at the same time. Comparing two groups, sometimes by lining them up one-to-one and sometimes by counting, builds `K.CC.C.6`.

The one-to-one correspondence in `K.CC.B.4.a` is often the first thing to wobble. Watch for students who say number names faster than they touch objects, or touch the same object twice. The fix is slowing down and using a tool (a finger, a counter that gets moved, a dot that gets crossed out) that forces one number to one object.

The cardinality principle (`K.CC.B.4.b`) is the moment "the last number is the answer" clicks. Ask, "How many did you count?" and listen for whether the student gives the last number said or recounts from 1. The student who recounts has not yet generalized cardinality.

Comparing numerals on paper (`K.CC.C.7`) usually comes last in the year. By spring, most kindergartners can look at a 4 and a 7 and say 7 is greater because it comes later in the count.
