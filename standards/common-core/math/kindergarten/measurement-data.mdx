# What are the Common Core Measurement & Data standards for Kindergarten?

<div class="answer-box">
  Measurement and Data in kindergarten (`K.MD`) is the first careful look at attributes that can be measured. Students describe objects by length, weight, and other attributes (`K.MD.A.1`), compare two objects directly to say which has more of an attribute (`K.MD.A.2`), and sort objects into categories and count each category (`K.MD.B.3`). No standard units yet; the goal is the language and the comparison itself.

## What it covers

- Describe and compare measurable attributes (`K.MD.A`).
- Classify objects and count the number of objects in each category (`K.MD.B`).

## Classroom application

`K.MD.A.1` is built through language. A student holds a pencil and says, "It is long and it is light." Two attributes, two pieces of vocabulary. The standard does not ask for measurement in units, only for describing the attribute.

`K.MD.A.2` is built through direct comparison. Two students stand back to back to compare height. Two pencils are placed end to end at the same starting line. The standard explicitly says "directly compare," because students at this age cannot yet measure with consistent units. The grade 1 work in `1.MD.A.1` and `1.MD.A.2` adds indirect comparison and non-standard units.

`K.MD.B.3` connects measurement to counting. A common kindergarten move is to sort the morning's snack into categories (red apples, green apples, oranges), count each pile, and order them by count. That sequence builds `K.MD.B.3` and rehearses `K.CC.B.4` and `K.CC.C.6` in one activity.

This domain is small in standard count but generous in classroom time. Many kindergarten teachers thread `K.MD` through the year by building one measurement or sorting activity into the weekly small-group rotation.
