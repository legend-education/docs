# What are the Common Core Math standards for Kindergarten?

<div class="answer-box">
  Kindergarten is the year counting becomes a system. The major work of the year is counting (`K.CC`) and the first work with addition and subtraction within 10 (`K.OA`). Place value, measurement, and geometry are present but smaller.

## What it covers

- Counting and Cardinality (`K.CC`): the count sequence, counting objects, and comparing numbers.
- Operations and Algebraic Thinking (`K.OA`): representing and solving addition and subtraction within 10, including word problems and decompositions.
- Number and Operations in Base Ten (`K.NBT`): the early idea that 11 to 19 are made of "ten and some more."
- Measurement and Data (`K.MD`): describing and comparing measurable attributes, and sorting and counting categories.
- Geometry (`K.G`): naming shapes, describing positions, and building simple shapes from parts.

## What changes this year

Kindergarten is the year counting stops being a song and starts being a tool. Students learn the number names through 100 (`K.CC.A.1`), then learn to count up to 20 objects in line, rectangular, or scattered arrangements without skipping or recounting (`K.CC.B.5`). The shift is from reciting numbers to using them.

The second big move is the first work with addition and subtraction. By June, students can solve word problems within 10 (`K.OA.A.2`), decompose numbers less than or equal to 10 into pairs (`K.OA.A.3`), and fluently add and subtract within 5 (`K.OA.A.5`). Most of this work happens with concrete objects and fingers, not symbols.

Place value gets a single but important kindergarten standard: 11 through 19 are made of a ten and some ones (`K.NBT.A.1`). That standard plants the seed for every grade of place value that follows.

## Classroom application

Most kindergarten math lessons run in three short blocks: a counting routine, a problem-of-the-day, and a small-group activity. The counting routine (choral counting, count-around-the-circle, or counting collections) builds `K.CC`. The daily problem builds `K.OA`. The small group rotates among measurement, sorting, and geometry tasks.

Counting collections are the highest-leverage routine at this grade. Students get a bag of objects, count them, and record the count with a drawing or a number. Done daily, this routine builds `K.CC.B.4` (count to tell how many) and `K.CC.B.5` (count up to 20 objects) at the same time.

A kindergarten word-problem rubric does not need many criteria. Did the student understand the situation? Did they pick an operation that matches? Did they get an answer they can defend with objects or a drawing? Those three questions track `K.OA.A.1` and `K.OA.A.2`.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Do kindergartners need to write equations, or is counting enough?">
    Both, but equations come last. The standard `K.OA.A.1` lists "objects, fingers, mental images, drawings, sounds, acting out situations, verbal explanations, expressions, or equations" as ways students represent addition and subtraction. The equation is one option, not the goal. Most kindergartners spend the first half of the year on objects and drawings, then move to expressions and equations in the spring.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Why is fluency only within 5?">
    `K.OA.A.5` asks for fluent addition and subtraction within 5 because counting on and number relationships within 5 are foundational. The progression assumes a student who knows 2 + 3 = 5 fluently will be able to use 2 + 4 next year by counting on one more. Fluency within 10 is the grade 1 target (`1.OA.C.6`), and within 20 is grade 2 (`2.OA.B.2`).
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
