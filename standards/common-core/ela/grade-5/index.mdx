# What are the Common Core ELA standards for Grade 5?

<div class="answer-box">
  Grade 5 is the year students start quoting the text directly. `RL.5.1` and `RI.5.1` ask for accurate quotation when explaining what a text says and when drawing inferences. Writing uses logically ordered reasons and multi-source research with notes that summarize or paraphrase. `RI.5.8` asks students to match reasons and evidence to the specific point in the argument they support, the bridge to grade 6 argument analysis.

## What it covers

- Reading Foundational Skills (`RF.5`): morphology-based decoding and grade-level fluency. This is the last grade where `RF` appears.
- Reading Literature (`RL.5`) and Reading Informational Text (`RI.5`): quoting accurately, finding two or more main ideas, analyzing point of view across multiple accounts, and comparing text structures across multiple texts.
- Writing (`W.5`): logically ordered opinion and informative pieces with linking clauses, narrative pacing, multi-source research, and notes that summarize or paraphrase.
- Speaking and Listening (`SL.5`): drawing conclusions from a discussion, summarizing a speaker's points with the evidence behind them, and using multimedia in presentations.
- Language (`L.5`): perfect verb tenses, correlative conjunctions, sentence combining, varieties of English, and logical-relationship transitions (`however`, `moreover`).

## What changes this year

Grade 5 is the year the standards step up from "refer to details" to "quote accurately." `RL.5.1` and `RI.5.1` are the key change, and they match the way state assessments score short and extended responses.

Research grows up too. `W.5.7` and `W.5.8` now require multiple sources and notes that summarize or paraphrase. That is a real step past the single- or two-source projects of grade 4.

Informational reading widens. `RI.5.2` asks students to determine two or more main ideas of a text. `RI.5.8` asks them to match specific evidence to the specific point in the argument it supports.

Grade 5 is also the last year of the `RF` strand. Starting in grade 6, foundational skills are folded into the main reading work, and the middle school band adds literacy standards for content areas.

## Standards

### Reading: Foundational Skills (`RF.5`)

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="RF.5.3 sub-points: phonics and word recognition">
    - **RF.5.3.a:** Use combined knowledge of all letter-sound correspondences, syllabication patterns, and morphology (e.g., roots and affixes) to read accurately unfamiliar multisyllabic words in context and out of context.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="RF.5.4 sub-points: fluency">
    - **RF.5.4.a:** Read on-level text with purpose and understanding.
    - **RF.5.4.b:** Read on-level prose and poetry orally with accuracy, appropriate rate, and expression on successive readings.
    - **RF.5.4.c:** Use context to confirm or self-correct word recognition and understanding, rereading as necessary.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### Writing (`W.5`)

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="W.5.1 sub-points: opinion writing">
    - **W.5.1.a:** Introduce a topic or text clearly, state an opinion, and create an organizational structure in which ideas are logically grouped to support the writer's purpose.
    - **W.5.1.b:** Provide logically ordered reasons that are supported by facts and details.
    - **W.5.1.c:** Link opinion and reasons using words, phrases, and clauses (e.g., `consequently`, `specifically`).
    - **W.5.1.d:** Provide a concluding statement or section related to the opinion presented.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="W.5.2 sub-points: informative or explanatory writing">
    - **W.5.2.a:** Introduce a topic clearly, provide a general observation and focus, and group related information logically; include formatting (e.g., headings), illustrations, and multimedia when useful to aiding comprehension.
    - **W.5.2.b:** Develop the topic with facts, definitions, concrete details, quotations, or other information and examples related to the topic.
    - **W.5.2.c:** Link ideas within and across categories of information using words, phrases, and clauses (e.g., `in contrast`, `especially`).
    - **W.5.2.d:** Use precise language and domain-specific vocabulary to inform about or explain the topic.
    - **W.5.2.e:** Provide a concluding statement or section related to the information or explanation presented.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="W.5.3 sub-points: narrative writing">
    - **W.5.3.a:** Orient the reader by establishing a situation and introducing a narrator and/or characters; organize an event sequence that unfolds naturally.
    - **W.5.3.b:** Use narrative techniques, such as dialogue, description, and pacing, to develop experiences and events or show the responses of characters to situations.
    - **W.5.3.c:** Use a variety of transitional words, phrases, and clauses to manage the sequence of events.
    - **W.5.3.d:** Use concrete words and phrases and sensory details to convey experiences and events precisely.
    - **W.5.3.e:** Provide a conclusion that follows from the narrated experiences or events.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="W.5.9 sub-points: using textual evidence in writing">
    - **W.5.9.a:** Apply grade 5 Reading standards to literature.
    - **W.5.9.b:** Apply grade 5 Reading standards to informational texts.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### Speaking and Listening (`SL.5`)

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="SL.5.1 sub-points: collaborative discussion">
    - **SL.5.1.a:** Come to discussions prepared, having read or studied required material; explicitly draw on that preparation and other information known about the topic to explore ideas under discussion.
    - **SL.5.1.b:** Follow agreed-upon rules for discussions and carry out assigned roles.
    - **SL.5.1.c:** Pose and respond to specific questions by making comments that contribute to the discussion and elaborate on the remarks of others.
    - **SL.5.1.d:** Review the key ideas expressed and draw conclusions in light of information and knowledge gained from the discussions.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### Language (`L.5`)

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="L.5.1 sub-points: grammar and usage">
    - **L.5.1.a:** Explain the function of conjunctions, prepositions, and interjections in general and their function in particular sentences.
    - **L.5.1.b:** Form and use the perfect (e.g., `I had walked`; `I have walked`; `I will have walked`) verb tenses.
    - **L.5.1.c:** Use verb tense to convey various times, sequences, states, and conditions.
    - **L.5.1.d:** Recognize and correct inappropriate shifts in verb tense.
    - **L.5.1.e:** Use correlative conjunctions (e.g., `either/or`, `neither/nor`).
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.5.2 sub-points: capitalization, punctuation, and spelling">
    - **L.5.2.a:** Use punctuation to separate items in a series.
    - **L.5.2.b:** Use a comma to separate an introductory element from the rest of the sentence.
    - **L.5.2.c:** Use a comma to set off the words `yes` and `no` (e.g., `Yes, thank you`), to set off a tag question from the rest of the sentence (e.g., `It's true, isn't it?`), and to indicate direct address (e.g., `Is that you, Steve?`).
    - **L.5.2.d:** Use underlining, quotation marks, or italics to indicate titles of works.
    - **L.5.2.e:** Spell grade-appropriate words correctly, consulting references as needed.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.5.3 sub-points: language for effect">
    - **L.5.3.a:** Expand, combine, and reduce sentences for meaning, reader/listener interest, and style.
    - **L.5.3.b:** Compare and contrast the varieties of English (e.g., dialects, registers) used in stories, dramas, or poems.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.5.4 sub-points: word meaning">
    - **L.5.4.a:** Use context (e.g., cause/effect relationships and comparisons in text) as a clue to the meaning of a word or phrase.
    - **L.5.4.b:** Use common, grade-appropriate Greek and Latin affixes and roots as clues to the meaning of a word (e.g., `photograph`, `photosynthesis`).
    - **L.5.4.c:** Consult reference materials (e.g., dictionaries, glossaries, thesauruses), both print and digital, to find the pronunciation and determine or clarify the precise meaning of key words and phrases.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.5.5 sub-points: word relationships">
    - **L.5.5.a:** Interpret figurative language, including similes and metaphors, in context.
    - **L.5.5.b:** Recognize and explain the meaning of common idioms, adages, and proverbs.
    - **L.5.5.c:** Use the relationship between particular words (e.g., synonyms, antonyms, homographs) to better understand each of the words.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Classroom application

Teach the accurate quote. A typical close-reading lesson asks students to highlight a sentence, copy it into a response with quotation marks (`L.5.2.d`), then explain what it says and what they infer from it (`RL.5.1`, `RI.5.1`). The same workflow drives `W.5.9` responses: read, annotate, quote, explain.

Most state assessments now expect students to work at least one direct quote into a constructed response by grade 5. The rubric usually awards a separate point for accurate quotation.

Research expands to several sources (`W.5.7`), with summarized or paraphrased notes and a source list (`W.5.8`). A useful structure: one focused question per project, three sources, two pieces of evidence per source, and a one-paragraph synthesis. The same workflow shows up next year in the content-area literacy standards (`RH`, `RST`, `WHST`).

For language, the highest-leverage instruction is verb-tense consistency (`L.5.1.d`) and logical-relationship transitions (`L.5.6`). Both move student writing toward grade 6 expectations.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="What does 'quote accurately' mean for a fifth grader?">
    Accurate quotation at grade 5 means copying the exact words from the text and putting them in quotation marks. Students do not yet need parenthetical citation with page numbers, but they are expected to introduce the quote in their own sentence (for example, "The narrator says, '\_\_\_.'") and explain what it shows. This standard is the first step in a progression that continues through grade 8 (more precise quotation) and grades 9 through 12 (strong, thorough evidence with proper citation).
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Why does RF disappear after this grade?">
    The Reading Foundational Skills strand is designed to end with grade 5. By grade 6, students are expected to apply phonics, word analysis, and fluency inside the main `RL` and `RI` work without a separate set of standards. If a grade 6 student is still struggling with multisyllable decoding or oral fluency, the standards point teachers back to the grade 5 versions for intervention rather than introducing a new strand.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
