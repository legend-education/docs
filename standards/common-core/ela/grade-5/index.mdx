# What are the Common Core ELA standards for Grade 5?

<div class="answer-box">
Grade 5 is the year students start quoting the text directly. `RL.5.1` and `RI.5.1` ask for accurate quotation when explaining what a text says and when drawing inferences. Writing uses logically ordered reasons and multi-source research with notes that summarize or paraphrase. `RI.5.8` asks students to match reasons and evidence to the specific point in the argument they support, the bridge to grade 6 argument analysis.
</div>

## What it covers

- Reading Foundational Skills (`RF.5`): morphology-based decoding and grade-level fluency. This is the last grade where `RF` appears.
- Reading Literature (`RL.5`) and Reading Informational Text (`RI.5`): quoting accurately, finding two or more main ideas, analyzing point of view across multiple accounts, and comparing text structures across multiple texts.
- Writing (`W.5`): logically ordered opinion and informative pieces with linking clauses, narrative pacing, multi-source research, and notes that summarize or paraphrase.
- Speaking and Listening (`SL.5`): drawing conclusions from a discussion, summarizing a speaker's points with the evidence behind them, and using multimedia in presentations.
- Language (`L.5`): perfect verb tenses, correlative conjunctions, sentence combining, varieties of English, and logical-relationship transitions (`however`, `moreover`).

## What changes this year

Grade 5 is the year the standards step up from "refer to details" to "quote accurately." `RL.5.1` and `RI.5.1` are the key change, and they match the way state assessments score short and extended responses.

Research grows up too. `W.5.7` and `W.5.8` now require multiple sources and notes that summarize or paraphrase. That is a real step past the single- or two-source projects of grade 4.

Informational reading widens. `RI.5.2` asks students to determine two or more main ideas of a text. `RI.5.8` asks them to match specific evidence to the specific point in the argument it supports.

Grade 5 is also the last year of the `RF` strand. Starting in grade 6, foundational skills are folded into the main reading work, and the middle school band adds literacy standards for content areas.

## Classroom application

Teach the accurate quote. A typical close-reading lesson asks students to highlight a sentence, copy it into a response with quotation marks (`L.5.2.d`), then explain what it says and what they infer from it (`RL.5.1`, `RI.5.1`). The same workflow drives `W.5.9` responses: read, annotate, quote, explain.

Most state assessments now expect students to work at least one direct quote into a constructed response by grade 5. The rubric usually awards a separate point for accurate quotation.

Research expands to several sources (`W.5.7`), with summarized or paraphrased notes and a source list (`W.5.8`). A useful structure: one focused question per project, three sources, two pieces of evidence per source, and a one-paragraph synthesis. The same workflow shows up next year in the content-area literacy standards (`RH`, `RST`, `WHST`).

For language, the highest-leverage instruction is verb-tense consistency (`L.5.1.d`) and logical-relationship transitions (`L.5.6`). Both move student writing toward grade 6 expectations.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="What does 'quote accurately' mean for a fifth grader?">
    Accurate quotation at grade 5 means copying the exact words from the text and putting them in quotation marks. Students do not yet need parenthetical citation with page numbers, but they are expected to introduce the quote in their own sentence (for example, "The narrator says, '___.'") and explain what it shows. This standard is the first step in a progression that continues through grade 8 (more precise quotation) and grades 9 through 12 (strong, thorough evidence with proper citation).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Why does RF disappear after this grade?">
    The Reading Foundational Skills strand is designed to end with grade 5. By grade 6, students are expected to apply phonics, word analysis, and fluency inside the main `RL` and `RI` work without a separate set of standards. If a grade 6 student is still struggling with multisyllable decoding or oral fluency, the standards point teachers back to the grade 5 versions for intervention rather than introducing a new strand.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
