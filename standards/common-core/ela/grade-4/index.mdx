# What are the Common Core ELA standards for Grade 4?

<div class="answer-box">
Grade 4 is the inference year. Students refer to details and examples when explaining what a text says outright and what they can reasonably infer (`RL.4.1`, `RI.4.1`). Writing happens in grouped paragraphs with headings and a clear concluding section. `W.4.9` (draw evidence from texts to support analysis) begins this year and becomes the spine of every grade 4 through grade 12 writing rubric.

## What it covers

- Reading Foundational Skills (`RF.4`): morphology-based decoding of multisyllable words and grade-level fluency.
- Reading Literature (`RL.4`) and Reading Informational Text (`RI.4`): inference with text-based details, theme and main idea with summary, structural elements of poems and drama, and comparing firsthand and secondhand accounts.
- Writing (`W.4`): grouped-paragraph opinion and informative pieces with headings and precise vocabulary; `W.4.9` evidence-from-text writing begins.
- Speaking and Listening (`SL.4`): preparing for discussions, paraphrasing across media, and using formal English when the task calls for it.
- Language (`L.4`): relative pronouns and adverbs, progressive verb tenses, modal auxiliaries, frequently confused words, similes and idioms, and Greek and Latin roots.

## What changes this year

Grade 4 is when reading and writing connect. `W.4.9` is the biggest move: students now draw evidence from literary or informational texts to support analysis, reflection, and research. That is what state assessments call "constructed response" or "extended response," and almost every reading assessment from here through grade 12 is built on it.

On the reading side, `RL.4.1` and `RI.4.1` add inference. Students explain what the text says outright and what they can reasonably figure out from it.

Writing structure tightens. `W.4.2` asks for grouped paragraphs with headings. `W.4.3` asks for sensory detail and concrete words. Language adds the grammar machinery students need to write longer sentences, including relative pronouns (`L.4.1.a`) and complete-sentence editing for fragments and run-ons (`L.4.1.f`).

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="W.4.1 sub-points: opinion writing">
    - **W.4.1.a:** Introduce a topic or text clearly, state an opinion, and create an organizational structure in which related ideas are grouped to support the writer's purpose.
    - **W.4.1.b:** Provide reasons that are supported by facts and details.
    - **W.4.1.c:** Link opinion and reasons using words and phrases (e.g., `for instance`, `in order to`, `in addition`).
    - **W.4.1.d:** Provide a concluding statement or section related to the opinion presented.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="W.4.2 sub-points: informative or explanatory writing">
    - **W.4.2.a:** Introduce a topic clearly and group related information in paragraphs and sections; include formatting (e.g., headings), illustrations, and multimedia when useful to aiding comprehension.
    - **W.4.2.b:** Develop the topic with facts, definitions, concrete details, quotations, or other information and examples related to the topic.
    - **W.4.2.c:** Link ideas within categories of information using words and phrases (e.g., `another`, `for example`, `also`, `because`).
    - **W.4.2.d:** Use precise language and domain-specific vocabulary to inform about or explain the topic.
    - **W.4.2.e:** Provide a concluding statement or section related to the information or explanation presented.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="W.4.3 sub-points: narrative writing">
    - **W.4.3.a:** Orient the reader by establishing a situation and introducing a narrator and/or characters; organize an event sequence that unfolds naturally.
    - **W.4.3.b:** Use dialogue and description to develop experiences and events or show the responses of characters to situations.
    - **W.4.3.c:** Use a variety of transitional words and phrases to manage the sequence of events.
    - **W.4.3.d:** Use concrete words and phrases and sensory details to convey experiences and events precisely.
    - **W.4.3.e:** Provide a conclusion that follows from the narrated experiences or events.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="W.4.9 sub-points: using textual evidence in writing">
    - **W.4.9.a:** Apply grade 4 Reading standards to literature (e.g., "Describe in depth a character, setting, or event in a story or drama, drawing on specific details in the text [e.g., a character's thoughts, words, or actions].").
    - **W.4.9.b:** Apply grade 4 Reading standards to informational texts (e.g., "Explain how an author uses reasons and evidence to support particular points in a text").
  </Accordion>

## Classroom application

Grade 4 readers spend much of the year on the move from "what the text says" to "what the text implies." A typical lesson asks students to read a short passage, answer a literal question (`RL.4.1` or `RI.4.1`), then answer an inference question that needs two or more details put together. State assessments now expect both kinds of responses, often in the same question stem ("What does the text say, and what can you infer?").

The same evidence habit shows up in `RI.4.8`, where students explain how the author's reasons support each point. That work is the bridge to the argument analysis they will do in grade 5.

Writing in grade 4 should look like a real essay. A `W.4.2` informative piece has an introduction, two or three grouped paragraphs (often with headings), and a concluding section, with precise content-area vocabulary throughout (`W.4.2.d`).

The most efficient way to teach `W.4.9` is to plan reading and writing together. Students annotate a passage during reading, then use those annotations as the evidence pool for a short written response the next day. It is the same workflow they will use from grade 5 through high school.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How is W.4.9 different from W.4.8?">
    `W.4.8` is about gathering and organizing information from sources (notes and a source list). `W.4.9` is about using what you have read as evidence inside your writing, applied through the grade 4 Reading standards. A short written response to a literature passage that quotes a character's words to support a claim is a `W.4.9` task. A research report that compiles facts from three websites with a bibliography is a `W.4.8` task. Most grade 4 assignments touch both.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="When do students need to start citing the text directly?">
    Direct quotation is not required until `RL.5.1` and `RI.5.1` next year. At grade 4, the standard is to "refer to details and examples." Paraphrasing, pointing to a specific line, or describing what a character said are all acceptable. Holding the bar at paraphrase in fall and pushing toward direct citation by spring puts students on track for grade 5.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

<div class="canonical-summary">
The Common Core ELA standards for Grade 4 cover six strands: Reading Foundational Skills (`RF.4`), Reading Literature (`RL.4`), Reading Informational Text (`RI.4`), Writing (`W.4`), Speaking and Listening (`SL.4`), and Language (`L.4`). Grade 4 introduces inference (`RL.4.1`, `RI.4.1`), comparison of firsthand and secondhand accounts, and the connection between reading and writing through `W.4.9`, which asks students to draw evidence from texts to support analysis, reflection, and research. Writing uses grouped paragraphs with headings, precise vocabulary, and a clear concluding section. Language adds relative pronouns, progressive verb tenses, modal auxiliaries, and frequently confused words. Source: corestandards.org/ELA-Literacy/RL/4.
