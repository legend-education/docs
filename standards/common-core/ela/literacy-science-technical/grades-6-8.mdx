# What are the Common Core Literacy in Science and Technical Subjects standards for Grades 6-8?

<div class="answer-box">
These standards live inside the ELA document but are written for middle school science and technical-subject teachers. Reading (`RST.6-8`) covers working with scientific text: citing evidence, following a procedure, reading visual representations of data, and distinguishing facts from opinion. Writing (`WHST.6-8`) covers argument and informative writing in science class, plus short research. `WHST` is shared with history and social studies.
</div>

## What it covers

- Reading in science and technical subjects (`RST.6-8`): citing textual evidence, central ideas in a scientific source, following a multistep procedure, scientific and technical vocabulary, text structure in science writing, the author's purpose, reading visual representations of data, distinguishing fact from opinion or speculation, and comparing information across sources.
- Writing in history, science, and technical subjects (`WHST.6-8`): argument and informative writing on discipline-specific content, short research projects, and gathering information with citation. (`WHST` is shared with history and social studies.)
- Scope: the `RST` standards apply to science classes and to technical-subject courses like pre-engineering, computer science, and career and technical education.

## What changes this band

Middle school is where students start reading scientific writing on its own terms. The job changes from "reading about science" in a textbook narrative to working with a primary source: a procedure, a research summary, a data table, or a diagram.

The reading work is concrete. `RST.6-8.3` asks students to follow a multistep procedure, the way a lab manual reads. `RST.6-8.7` asks them to read a chart, graph, or model in print or digital form. `RST.6-8.8` asks them to tell facts from reasoned judgment from speculation, the way a science teacher already separates "the data show" from "this is just a guess."

Writing in science class is its own thing. `WHST.6-8.1` is a scientific argument: claim, reasons, evidence from a lab or a source. `WHST.6-8.2` includes the narration of a scientific procedure or technical process, which is the formal lab report in plain language.

  <Accordion title="WHST.6-8.1 sub-points: discipline-specific argument writing">
    - **WHST.6-8.1.a:** Introduce claim(s) about a topic or issue, acknowledge and distinguish the claim(s) from alternate or opposing claims, and organize the reasons and evidence logically.
    - **WHST.6-8.1.b:** Support claim(s) with logical reasoning and relevant, accurate data and evidence that demonstrate an understanding of the topic or text, using credible sources.
    - **WHST.6-8.1.c:** Use words, phrases, and clauses to create cohesion and clarify the relationships among claim(s), counterclaims, reasons, and evidence.
    - **WHST.6-8.1.d:** Establish and maintain a formal style.
    - **WHST.6-8.1.e:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the argument presented.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="WHST.6-8.2 sub-points: informative or explanatory writing">
    - **WHST.6-8.2.a:** Introduce a topic clearly, previewing what is to follow; organize ideas, concepts, and information into broader categories as appropriate to achieving purpose; include formatting (e.g., headings), graphics (e.g., charts, tables), and multimedia when useful to aiding comprehension.
    - **WHST.6-8.2.b:** Develop the topic with relevant, well-chosen facts, definitions, concrete details, quotations, or other information and examples.
    - **WHST.6-8.2.c:** Use appropriate and varied transitions to create cohesion and clarify the relationships among ideas and concepts.
    - **WHST.6-8.2.d:** Use precise language and domain-specific vocabulary to inform about or explain the topic.
    - **WHST.6-8.2.e:** Establish and maintain a formal style and objective tone.
    - **WHST.6-8.2.f:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the information or explanation presented.

<Note>
`WHST` is the same writing set across history, science, and technical subjects. The discipline shows up in the prompt and the evidence, not in a separate standard.
</Note>

## Classroom application

Pair every reading with a real task. A procedure paragraph (`RST.6-8.3`) goes with carrying out the procedure. A diagram or data table (`RST.6-8.7`) goes with answering a question from it. Reading without a task is where most students lose the thread; the standards are written so the reading lives next to the work.

Use the lab report as a recurring writing genre. The lab report is the natural shape of `WHST.6-8.2` (informative writing with the narration of a procedure). A simple template works: question, procedure, data, claim, evidence, reasoning. Score it on `WHST.6-8.1` (claim and evidence) and `WHST.6-8.2` (procedure narration).

Teach the fact-and-speculation distinction with the texts you already use. Highlight one sentence in a science article. Ask the class whether it's a fact, a reasoned judgment based on the data, or speculation about what might be true. This is `RST.6-8.8` in three minutes and is some of the most useful science reading work in the standards.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How do these standards line up with NGSS?">
    They line up closely. The NGSS practices "Constructing explanations and designing solutions" and "Engaging in argument from evidence" are the same work named in `WHST.6-8.1` and `WHST.6-8.2`. The NGSS practice "Obtaining, evaluating, and communicating information" is essentially `RST.6-8`. Schools that teach NGSS already cover the literacy standards; most just need to make the writing rubric explicit.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What counts as a technical subject?">
    Career and technical education courses (pre-engineering, automotive technology, computer science, allied health, agriculture, and similar courses) all count. The `RST` standards apply whenever students are reading discipline-specific text with procedures, vocabulary, and data. A teacher in any of these subjects can use the same reading and writing routines a science teacher uses.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
