# What are the Common Core Literacy in Science and Technical Subjects standards for Grades 11-12?

<div class="answer-box">
Grades 11-12 are the K-12 endpoint for science literacy. Students cite scientific evidence with attention to gaps and inconsistencies (`RST.11-12.1`), evaluate hypotheses and conclusions in light of competing information (`RST.11-12.8`), and synthesize information from a range of sources into a coherent understanding (`RST.11-12.9`). Writing in science reaches its endpoint with arguments that anticipate audience and integrate sources without overreliance.
</div>

## What it covers

- Reading in science and technical subjects (`RST.11-12`): citing evidence with attention to gaps and inconsistencies; determining central ideas across a complex source; following complex multistep procedures with feedback or correction; advanced vocabulary, symbols, and notation; structural analysis; the author's purpose in a definition or experimental design; integration of multiple sources of information and data; evaluating conclusions against competing information; and synthesizing across sources.
- Writing in history, science, and technical subjects (`WHST.11-12`): argument that anticipates the audience's knowledge, concerns, values, and possible biases; informative writing where each element builds on the last; sustained research from authoritative sources; integrating sources without overreliance.
- Scope: applies to high school science courses (biology, chemistry, earth science, physics, AP sciences) and to technical-subject courses (engineering, computer science, CTE).

## What changes this band

The reading set now includes peer-reviewed research summaries and engineering documentation. Students should be reading abstracts and selections from real research articles, technical manuals, and published procedures, not just textbook explanations.

Reading now includes gaps and inconsistencies. `RST.11-12.1` adds attention to important distinctions the author makes and to gaps or inconsistencies in the account. Science is rarely tidy; the standards now ask students to notice that.

Source synthesis becomes the work. `RST.11-12.9` asks students to synthesize information from a range of sources into a coherent understanding of a process, phenomenon, or concept. This is the bridge from "I read three sources" to "here is what they tell me together about the science."

Writing reaches its endpoint with the audience-aware argument. `WHST.11-12.1` mirrors the grades 11-12 ELA argument standard: students develop both claims and counterclaims fairly, anticipate the audience's values and possible biases, and integrate sources without overreliance on any one of them.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="WHST.11-12.1 sub-points: discipline-specific argument writing">
    - **WHST.11-12.1.a:** Introduce precise, knowledgeable claim(s), establish the significance of the claim(s), distinguish the claim(s) from alternate or opposing claims, and create an organization that logically sequences the claim(s), counterclaims, reasons, and evidence.
    - **WHST.11-12.1.b:** Develop claim(s) and counterclaims fairly and thoroughly, supplying the most relevant data and evidence for each while pointing out the strengths and limitations of both claim(s) and counterclaims in a discipline-appropriate form that anticipates the audience's knowledge level, concerns, values, and possible biases.
    - **WHST.11-12.1.c:** Use words, phrases, and clauses as well as varied syntax to link the major sections of the text, create cohesion, and clarify the relationships between claim(s) and reasons, between reasons and evidence, and between claim(s) and counterclaims.
    - **WHST.11-12.1.d:** Establish and maintain a formal style and objective tone while attending to the norms and conventions of the discipline in which they are writing.
    - **WHST.11-12.1.e:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from or supports the argument presented.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="WHST.11-12.2 sub-points: informative or explanatory writing">
    - **WHST.11-12.2.a:** Introduce a topic and organize complex ideas, concepts, and information so that each new element builds on that which precedes it to create a unified whole; include formatting (e.g., headings), graphics (e.g., figures, tables), and multimedia when useful to aiding comprehension.
    - **WHST.11-12.2.b:** Develop the topic thoroughly by selecting the most significant and relevant facts, extended definitions, concrete details, quotations, or other information and examples appropriate to the audience's knowledge of the topic.
    - **WHST.11-12.2.c:** Use varied transitions and sentence structures to link the major sections of the text, create cohesion, and clarify the relationships among complex ideas and concepts.
    - **WHST.11-12.2.d:** Use precise language, domain-specific vocabulary and techniques such as metaphor, simile, and analogy to manage the complexity of the topic; convey a knowledgeable stance in a style that responds to the discipline and context as well as to the expertise of likely readers.
    - **WHST.11-12.2.e:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the information or explanation provided (e.g., articulating implications or the significance of the topic).
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Classroom application

Run a multi-source synthesis as the year's anchor task. Pose a scientific question that lives in current research, for example a public-health question or an emerging-technology question with a data set students can actually examine. Give students a mix of sources: textbook background, a recent research summary, a primary data set, and a popular-press article. Require an argument that synthesizes at least three of them. This is `RST.11-12.9` and `WHST.11-12.1` in one project.

Score corroboration as a separate criterion. `RST.11-12.8` asks students to verify data when possible and corroborate or challenge conclusions with other sources. Most student arguments at this level fail when they take one source as authoritative without checking it. A rubric line for corroboration makes the move visible.

Teach uncertainty as the mark of expertise. At every analysis checkpoint, ask students to name one important issue that remains unresolved in the source (`RST.11-12.6`). Naming what science does not yet know is what distinguishes a serious high school analysis from a competent one.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How does this map to AP science courses?">
    Closely. The AP Biology, AP Chemistry, AP Physics, and AP Environmental Science exam tasks (free-response questions that ask students to analyze data, evaluate a claim, or design an experiment) are scored against rubrics that mirror `RST.11-12.1`, `RST.11-12.8`, and `WHST.11-12.1`. The AP science practices (analyzing data, justifying claims, experimental design) are essentially the same skills the literacy standards name.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What about science research courses and senior capstones?">
    Capstone research courses (independent science research, engineering capstones, dual-enrollment lab courses) are where these standards land most directly. A student-designed research project, with a literature review, a methods section, results, and discussion, exercises every standard in `RST.11-12` and most of `WHST.11-12`. The literacy standards do not require a capstone, but they describe the work it should look like.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
