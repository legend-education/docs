# What are the Common Core Literacy in Science and Technical Subjects standards for Grades 9-10?

<div class="answer-box">
Grades 9-10 push science reading toward primary scientific writing: research summaries, journal-style articles, and technical procedures. Students cite evidence with attention to the science (`RST.9-10.1`), evaluate the reasoning behind a hypothesis or conclusion (`RST.9-10.8`), and compare findings across sources. Writing in science adds the counterclaim and the structured argument from evidence.

## What it covers

- Reading in science and technical subjects (`RST.9-10`): citing evidence with attention to the science; tracing central ideas through a source; following a complex multistep procedure; advanced scientific vocabulary, symbols, and notation; structural relationships; the author's purpose in a definition or experimental design; integration of text and visual data; assessing the reasoning behind a hypothesis or conclusion; and comparing findings across sources.
- Writing in history, science, and technical subjects (`WHST.9-10`): argument with claim and counterclaim, informative writing on discipline-specific topics, short or sustained research from authoritative sources, and citation.
- Scope: applies to high school science courses (biology, chemistry, earth science, physics) and to technical-subject courses (pre-engineering, computer science, CTE).

## What changes this band

The reading set widens to include real scientific writing. A research summary in a science journal article, a procedure from a lab manual, and a section of a technical manual are all fair game at this band.

Vocabulary takes a real step up. `RST.9-10.4` adds symbols and notation alongside terminology: the chemical formula, the equation, the diagram label. Reading a science source now means reading both the words and the symbols.

The reasoning bar moves with it. `RST.9-10.8` asks students to assess the reasoning behind a hypothesis or conclusion. The reading question shifts from "is the evidence relevant" to "does the conclusion actually follow from the evidence."

Writing in science adds the counterclaim. `WHST.9-10.1` mirrors the grades 9-10 ELA argument standard. In science class, this often looks like writing up a lab argument that acknowledges and refutes an alternate explanation of the data.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="WHST.9-10.1 sub-points: discipline-specific argument writing">
    - **WHST.9-10.1.a:** Introduce precise claim(s), distinguish the claim(s) from alternate or opposing claims, and create an organization that establishes clear relationships among the claim(s), counterclaims, reasons, and evidence.
    - **WHST.9-10.1.b:** Develop claim(s) and counterclaims fairly, supplying data and evidence for each while pointing out the strengths and limitations of both claim(s) and counterclaims in a discipline-appropriate form and in a manner that anticipates the audience's knowledge level and concerns.
    - **WHST.9-10.1.c:** Use words, phrases, and clauses to link the major sections of the text, create cohesion, and clarify the relationships between claim(s) and reasons, between reasons and evidence, and between claim(s) and counterclaims.
    - **WHST.9-10.1.d:** Establish and maintain a formal style and objective tone while attending to the norms and conventions of the discipline in which they are writing.
    - **WHST.9-10.1.e:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from or supports the argument presented.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="WHST.9-10.2 sub-points: informative or explanatory writing">
    - **WHST.9-10.2.a:** Introduce a topic and organize ideas, concepts, and information to make important connections and distinctions; include formatting (e.g., headings), graphics (e.g., figures, tables), and multimedia when useful to aiding comprehension.
    - **WHST.9-10.2.b:** Develop the topic with well-chosen, relevant, and sufficient facts, extended definitions, concrete details, quotations, or other information and examples appropriate to the audience's knowledge of the topic.
    - **WHST.9-10.2.c:** Use varied transitions and sentence structures to link the major sections of the text, create cohesion, and clarify the relationships among ideas and concepts.
    - **WHST.9-10.2.d:** Use precise language and domain-specific vocabulary to manage the complexity of the topic and convey a style appropriate to the discipline and context as well as to the expertise of likely readers.
    - **WHST.9-10.2.e:** Establish and maintain a formal style and objective tone while attending to the norms and conventions of the discipline in which they are writing.
    - **WHST.9-10.2.f:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the information or explanation presented (e.g., articulating implications or the significance of the topic).

## Classroom application

Use real scientific writing, not just textbook prose. A short research summary, the abstract of a journal article, or a section of a published lab procedure gives students the kind of text `RST.9-10.1` and `RST.9-10.4` were written for. A textbook chapter rewrites the science; primary writing makes them read the science.

Replace the formulaic lab report with a structured lab argument. A claim-evidence-reasoning (CER) writeup at the end of an investigation is the natural form of `WHST.9-10.1` in a science class. Students make a claim about what the data show, cite the data as evidence, and explain the scientific reasoning that ties one to the other.

Compare a finding with another source on purpose. `RST.9-10.9` asks students to compare a finding with their own experiments or with other sources, noting when findings agree or contradict. A short follow-up read after a lab makes this concrete.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How do these line up with NGSS at the high school level?">
    The NGSS "Engaging in argument from evidence" practice and the high-school argument writing standard at `WHST.9-10.1` are essentially the same skill. The NGSS practice "Obtaining, evaluating, and communicating information" maps directly to `RST.9-10`. A school that teaches NGSS already covers most of the reading and writing work; the literacy standards mostly make the writing structure and source citation explicit.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How is this different from grades 6-8?">
    The texts shift from textbook chapters to real scientific writing, and vocabulary now includes symbols and notation alongside terms. The reasoning bar also moves from "follow the procedure" to "assess whether the conclusion is supported." Writing adds the counterclaim, the same way it does on the grades 9-10 ELA side.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
