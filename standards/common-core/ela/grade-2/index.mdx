## What it covers

- Reading Foundational Skills (`RF.2`): grade-level phonics for two-syllable words, prefixes and suffixes, irregular spellings, and reading fluency. Print concepts and phonological awareness drop off (mastered).
- Reading Literature (`RL.2`) and Reading Informational Text (`RI.2`): central message and lesson, multi-paragraph topic structure, comparing two versions of a story, and using text features.
- Writing (`W.2`): opinion, informative, and narrative pieces with linking words and concluding statements.
- Speaking and Listening (`SL.2`): linking comments to others, recounting a text read aloud, and producing complete sentences.
- Language (`L.2`): collective nouns, irregular plurals and verbs, formal vs. informal English, and prefix and root-word strategies for word meaning.

## What changes this year

Grade 2 is a consolidation year. Phonics narrows to the harder cases (two-syllable long-vowel words, affixes, irregular spellings), and fluency becomes the daily target.

Comprehension widens. Students recount fables and folktales for a lesson or moral (`RL.2.2`), describe how characters respond to challenges (`RL.2.3`), and identify the main topic of a multi-paragraph text plus the focus of specific paragraphs (`RI.2.2`).

Writing picks up linking words and a real conclusion (`W.2.1`, `W.2.2`, `W.2.3`). Language adds the first register standard: `L.2.3.a` asks students to compare formal and informal uses of English.

By June, students should read and understand texts in the grades 2 to 3 complexity band proficiently (`RL.2.10`, `RI.2.10`).

## Standards

### Reading: Foundational Skills (`RF.2`)

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="RF.2.3 sub-points: phonics and word recognition">
    - **RF.2.3.a:** Distinguish long and short vowels when reading regularly spelled one-syllable words.
    - **RF.2.3.b:** Know spelling-sound correspondences for additional common vowel teams.
    - **RF.2.3.c:** Decode regularly spelled two-syllable words with long vowels.
    - **RF.2.3.d:** Decode words with common prefixes and suffixes.
    - **RF.2.3.e:** Identify words with inconsistent but common spelling-sound correspondences.
    - **RF.2.3.f:** Recognize and read grade-appropriate irregularly spelled words.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="RF.2.4 sub-points: fluency">
    - **RF.2.4.a:** Read on-level text with purpose and understanding.
    - **RF.2.4.b:** Read on-level text orally with accuracy, appropriate rate, and expression on successive readings.
    - **RF.2.4.c:** Use context to confirm or self-correct word recognition and understanding, rereading as necessary.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### Language (`L.2`)

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="L.2.1 sub-points: grammar and usage">
    - **L.2.1.a:** Use collective nouns (e.g., `group`).
    - **L.2.1.b:** Form and use frequently occurring irregular plural nouns (e.g., `feet`, `children`, `teeth`, `mice`, `fish`).
    - **L.2.1.c:** Use reflexive pronouns (e.g., `myself`, `ourselves`).
    - **L.2.1.d:** Form and use the past tense of frequently occurring irregular verbs (e.g., `sat`, `hid`, `told`).
    - **L.2.1.e:** Use adjectives and adverbs, and choose between them depending on what is to be modified.
    - **L.2.1.f:** Produce, expand, and rearrange complete simple and compound sentences (e.g., `The boy watched the movie`; `The little boy watched the movie`; `The action movie was watched by the little boy`).
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.2.2 sub-points: capitalization, punctuation, and spelling">
    - **L.2.2.a:** Capitalize holidays, product names, and geographic names.
    - **L.2.2.b:** Use commas in greetings and closings of letters.
    - **L.2.2.c:** Use an apostrophe to form contractions and frequently occurring possessives.
    - **L.2.2.d:** Generalize learned spelling patterns when writing words (e.g., `cage` to `badge`; `boy` to `boil`).
    - **L.2.2.e:** Consult reference materials, including beginning dictionaries, as needed to check and correct spellings.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.2.3 sub-points: language for effect">
    - **L.2.3.a:** Compare formal and informal uses of English.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.2.4 sub-points: word meaning">
    - **L.2.4.a:** Use sentence-level context as a clue to the meaning of a word or phrase.
    - **L.2.4.b:** Determine the meaning of the new word formed when a known prefix is added to a known word (e.g., `happy/unhappy`, `tell/retell`).
    - **L.2.4.c:** Use a known root word as a clue to the meaning of an unknown word with the same root (e.g., `addition`, `additional`).
    - **L.2.4.d:** Use knowledge of the meaning of individual words to predict the meaning of compound words (e.g., `birdhouse`, `lighthouse`, `housefly`; `bookshelf`, `notebook`, `bookmark`).
    - **L.2.4.e:** Use glossaries and beginning dictionaries, both print and digital, to determine or clarify the meaning of words and phrases.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.2.5 sub-points: word relationships">
    - **L.2.5.a:** Identify real-life connections between words and their use (e.g., describe foods that are spicy or juicy).
    - **L.2.5.b:** Distinguish shades of meaning among closely related verbs (e.g., `toss`, `throw`, `hurl`) and closely related adjectives (e.g., `thin`, `slender`, `skinny`, `scrawny`).
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Classroom application

By grade 2, most students are reading independently for parts of the day. Daily fluency practice (`RF.2.4.b`) sits next to a comprehension block built around shared texts. The teacher asks students to recount the lesson of a folktale (`RL.2.2`) or to point to the bold subheading that names the focus of a paragraph (`RI.2.2`, `RI.2.5`).

The grades 2 to 3 text complexity band is wide on purpose. A class might read a "Frog and Toad" story one day and a science article about pond habitats the next, with the same comprehension targets in both lessons.

Writing in grade 2 starts to use real structure. A `W.2.1` opinion piece looks like an introduction, two reasons each connected with a linking word, and a one-sentence closing. A `W.2.2` informative piece uses facts and definitions instead of just facts.

Conferring around `W.2.5` (focus and revision) is the highest-leverage move you can make this year. Ask "what is your topic?" and "which sentence is off-topic?" and you have a revision target the student can act on.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Why do RF.2.1 and RF.2.2 not appear at this grade?">
    Print concepts (`RF.K.1`, `RF.1.1`) and phonological awareness (`RF.K.2`, `RF.1.2`) are assumed mastered by grade 2. The standards document deliberately drops them at this grade so that instruction focuses on phonics (`RF.2.3`) and fluency (`RF.2.4`). If a grade 2 student has gaps in those earlier skills, the standards point you back to the K and grade 1 versions for intervention.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="What does the grades 2 to 3 text complexity band mean for my classroom library?">
    The band is the same Lexile or Guided Reading range your school likely uses to organize the bookshelves. Grade 2 students should be reading proficiently across the full range by June (`RL.2.10`, `RI.2.10`), with scaffolding at the top end. Most state assessments use a similar range, which is why most state ELA tests start at grade 3 with passages that overlap the top of this band.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
