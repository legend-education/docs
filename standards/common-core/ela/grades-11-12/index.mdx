# What are the Common Core ELA standards for Grades 11-12?

<div class="answer-box">
Grades 11-12 are the last two-year band. The argument essay is the K-12 endpoint: students develop both their claim and the counterclaim thoroughly, anticipating the audience's knowledge, values, and possible biases (`W.11-12.1.b`). Reading adds foundational U.S. documents and Supreme Court reasoning. Most schools split the band into English III and English IV.

## What it covers

- Reading literature (`RL.11-12`) and informational text (`RI.11-12`): strong textual evidence (including where the text leaves matters uncertain), interacting themes, satire and irony, multiple interpretations of dramatic works, foundational American literature, foundational U.S. documents, and Supreme Court reasoning.
- Writing (`W.11-12`): argument that anticipates the audience's knowledge, concerns, values, and possible biases; informative writing where each element builds on the last; narrative with tone and outcome; sustained research from authoritative sources.
- Speaking and listening (`SL.11-12`): civil discussions; evaluating a speaker's stance, premises, and tone; presentations that address opposing perspectives.
- Language (`L.11-12`): conventions as evolving usage, hyphenation, syntactic variation for effect, college- and career-ready vocabulary, and figures of speech like hyperbole and paradox.
- Content-area literacy: `RH.11-12`, `RST.11-12`, and `WHST.11-12` complete the K-12 literacy progression for history, science, and technical subjects.

## What changes this year

This is the College and Career Readiness destination. The band describes what students should be able to do by the end of grade 12.

Reading gets more honest about uncertainty. `RL.11-12.1` and `RI.11-12.1` now ask students to name where the text leaves matters open. In analysis, that looks like calling out an ambiguous ending, an unreliable narrator, or a place where the evidence is partial.

Argument writing goes one level deeper on audience. At `W.11-12.1.b`, students develop both claim and counterclaim fairly and thoroughly, and anticipate the audience's knowledge, concerns, values, and possible biases.

Informational reading adds constitutional and legal reasoning. `RI.11-12.8` brings in Supreme Court majority opinions and dissents. `RI.11-12.9` widens the set of foundational U.S. documents to include texts like the Declaration of Independence, the Preamble to the Constitution, the Bill of Rights, and Lincoln's Second Inaugural Address.

## Classroom application

Plan around two long-form arguments. One literary analysis and one issues-based research argument with cited sources (`W.11-12.8`). Both are scored against the same `W.11-12.1` rubric, with the audience-awareness piece of `W.11-12.1.b` as the highest bar in the document.

Build a "where the text leaves matters uncertain" move into analysis routines. Have students end every analytical paragraph with one sentence that names what the text refuses to settle. This is the small habit that satisfies `RL.11-12.1` and `RI.11-12.1` and signals college-level reading.

Anchor the year on foundational U.S. texts plus a literary partner. `RI.11-12.8` brings in Supreme Court majority opinions and dissents. `RI.11-12.9` widens the document set: Declaration of Independence, Preamble to the Constitution, Bill of Rights, Lincoln's Second Inaugural. `RL.11-12.7` requires at least one Shakespeare play and one play by an American dramatist. Most AP English Language and AP English Literature reading lists already cover this ground.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="What does 'where the text leaves matters uncertain' look like in student writing?">
    The standard (`RL.11-12.1`, `RI.11-12.1`) asks students to name what a text deliberately does not resolve. In literary analysis, that might be an ambiguous ending, an unreliable narrator, or a question the author refuses to answer. In informational reading, it might be a place where the evidence is partial, the source is biased, or two reasonable interpretations remain. In a response, this usually appears as a sentence like, "The text does not say whether ___, which leaves the question of ___ open." Acknowledging uncertainty is a marker of college-level reading.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How do AP English courses map to the grades 11-12 band?">
    AP English Language is typically offered in grade 11 and AP English Literature in grade 12, and both align well with the grades 11-12 band. AP English Language emphasizes rhetorical analysis (`RI.11-12.6`), argument writing with a deep understanding of audience (`W.11-12.1.b`), and seminal U.S. texts (`RI.11-12.9`). AP English Literature emphasizes complex character development (`RL.11-12.3`), the impact of structural choices (`RL.11-12.5`), and analysis of multiple interpretations (`RL.11-12.7`). The standards do not require an AP course; they describe what students should be able to do by the end of grade 12.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

The Common Core ELA standards for Grades 11-12 are the final two-year band and describe the College and Career Readiness endpoint of the K-12 progression. Students cite strong and thorough textual evidence and name where the text leaves matters uncertain. They determine two or more themes or central ideas and analyze how those interact. Argument writing anticipates the audience's knowledge, concerns, values, and possible biases. Reading includes foundational American literature across three centuries, Shakespeare, foundational U.S. documents, and Supreme Court reasoning.
