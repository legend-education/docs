# What are the Common Core ELA standards for Grades 11-12?

<div class="answer-box">
  Grades 11-12 are the last two-year band. The argument essay is the K-12 endpoint: students develop both their claim and the counterclaim thoroughly, anticipating the audience's knowledge, values, and possible biases (`W.11-12.1.b`). Reading adds foundational U.S. documents and Supreme Court reasoning. Most schools split the band into English III and English IV.

## What it covers

- Reading literature (`RL.11-12`) and informational text (`RI.11-12`): strong textual evidence (including where the text leaves matters uncertain), interacting themes, satire and irony, multiple interpretations of dramatic works, foundational American literature, foundational U.S. documents, and Supreme Court reasoning.
- Writing (`W.11-12`): argument that anticipates the audience's knowledge, concerns, values, and possible biases; informative writing where each element builds on the last; narrative with tone and outcome; sustained research from authoritative sources.
- Speaking and listening (`SL.11-12`): civil discussions; evaluating a speaker's stance, premises, and tone; presentations that address opposing perspectives.
- Language (`L.11-12`): conventions as evolving usage, hyphenation, syntactic variation for effect, college- and career-ready vocabulary, and figures of speech like hyperbole and paradox.
- Content-area literacy: `RH.11-12`, `RST.11-12`, and `WHST.11-12` complete the K-12 literacy progression for history, science, and technical subjects.

## What changes this year

This is the College and Career Readiness destination. The band describes what students should be able to do by the end of grade 12.

Reading gets more honest about uncertainty. `RL.11-12.1` and `RI.11-12.1` now ask students to name where the text leaves matters open. In analysis, that looks like calling out an ambiguous ending, an unreliable narrator, or a place where the evidence is partial.

Argument writing goes one level deeper on audience. At `W.11-12.1.b`, students develop both claim and counterclaim fairly and thoroughly, and anticipate the audience's knowledge, concerns, values, and possible biases.

Informational reading adds constitutional and legal reasoning. `RI.11-12.8` brings in Supreme Court majority opinions and dissents. `RI.11-12.9` widens the set of foundational U.S. documents to include texts like the Declaration of Independence, the Preamble to the Constitution, the Bill of Rights, and Lincoln's Second Inaugural Address.

### Writing (`W.11-12`)

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="W.11-12.1 sub-points: argument writing">
    - **W.11-12.1.a:** Introduce precise, knowledgeable claim(s), establish the significance of the claim(s), distinguish the claim(s) from alternate or opposing claims, and create an organization that logically sequences claim(s), counterclaims, reasons, and evidence.
    - **W.11-12.1.b:** Develop claim(s) and counterclaims fairly and thoroughly, supplying the most relevant evidence for each while pointing out the strengths and limitations of both in a manner that anticipates the audience's knowledge level, concerns, values, and possible biases.
    - **W.11-12.1.c:** Use words, phrases, and clauses as well as varied syntax to link the major sections of the text, create cohesion, and clarify the relationships between claim(s) and reasons, between reasons and evidence, and between claim(s) and counterclaims.
    - **W.11-12.1.d:** Establish and maintain a formal style and objective tone while attending to the norms and conventions of the discipline in which they are writing.
    - **W.11-12.1.e:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the argument presented.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="W.11-12.2 sub-points: informative or explanatory writing">
    - **W.11-12.2.a:** Introduce a topic; organize complex ideas, concepts, and information so that each new element builds on that which precedes it to create a unified whole; include formatting (e.g., headings), graphics (e.g., figures, tables), and multimedia when useful to aiding comprehension.
    - **W.11-12.2.b:** Develop the topic thoroughly by selecting the most significant and relevant facts, extended definitions, concrete details, quotations, or other information and examples appropriate to the audience's knowledge of the topic.
    - **W.11-12.2.c:** Use appropriate and varied transitions and syntax to link the major sections of the text, create cohesion, and clarify the relationships among complex ideas and concepts.
    - **W.11-12.2.d:** Use precise language, domain-specific vocabulary, and techniques such as metaphor, simile, and analogy to manage the complexity of the topic.
    - **W.11-12.2.e:** Establish and maintain a formal style and objective tone while attending to the norms and conventions of the discipline in which they are writing.
    - **W.11-12.2.f:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the information or explanation presented (e.g., articulating implications or the significance of the topic).
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="W.11-12.3 sub-points: narrative writing">
    - **W.11-12.3.a:** Engage and orient the reader by setting out a problem, situation, or observation and its significance, establishing one or multiple point(s) of view, and introducing a narrator and/or characters; create a smooth progression of experiences or events.
    - **W.11-12.3.b:** Use narrative techniques, such as dialogue, pacing, description, reflection, and multiple plot lines, to develop experiences, events, and/or characters.
    - **W.11-12.3.c:** Use a variety of techniques to sequence events so that they build on one another to create a coherent whole and build toward a particular tone and outcome (e.g., a sense of mystery, suspense, growth, or resolution).
    - **W.11-12.3.d:** Use precise words and phrases, telling details, and sensory language to convey a vivid picture of the experiences, events, setting, and/or characters.
    - **W.11-12.3.e:** Provide a conclusion that follows from and reflects on what is experienced, observed, or resolved over the course of the narrative.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="W.11-12.9 sub-points: using textual evidence in writing">
    - **W.11-12.9.a:** Apply grades 11-12 Reading standards to literature.
    - **W.11-12.9.b:** Apply grades 11-12 Reading standards to literary nonfiction.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### Speaking and Listening (`SL.11-12`)

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="SL.11-12.1 sub-points: collaborative discussion">
    - **SL.11-12.1.a:** Come to discussions prepared, having read and researched material under study; explicitly draw on that preparation by referring to evidence from texts and other research on the topic or issue to stimulate a thoughtful, well-reasoned exchange of ideas.
    - **SL.11-12.1.b:** Work with peers to promote civil, democratic discussions and decision-making, set clear goals and deadlines, and establish individual roles as needed.
    - **SL.11-12.1.c:** Propel conversations by posing and responding to questions that probe reasoning and evidence; ensure a hearing for a full range of positions on a topic or issue; clarify, verify, or challenge ideas and conclusions; and promote divergent and creative perspectives.
    - **SL.11-12.1.d:** Respond thoughtfully to diverse perspectives; synthesize comments, claims, and evidence made on all sides of an issue; resolve contradictions when possible; and determine what additional information or research is required to deepen the investigation or complete the task.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### Language (`L.11-12`)

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="L.11-12.1 sub-points: grammar and usage">
    - **L.11-12.1.a:** Apply the understanding that usage is a matter of convention, can change over time, and is sometimes contested.
    - **L.11-12.1.b:** Resolve issues of complex or contested usage, consulting references (e.g., Merriam-Webster's Dictionary of English Usage, Garner's Modern American Usage) as needed.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.11-12.2 sub-points: capitalization, punctuation, and spelling">
    - **L.11-12.2.a:** Observe hyphenation conventions.
    - **L.11-12.2.b:** Spell correctly.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.11-12.3 sub-points: language for effect">
    - **L.11-12.3.a:** Vary syntax for effect, consulting references (e.g., Tufte's Artful Sentences) for guidance as needed; apply an understanding of syntax to the study of complex texts when reading.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.11-12.4 sub-points: word meaning">
    - **L.11-12.4.a:** Use context (e.g., the overall meaning of a sentence, paragraph, or text; a word's position or function in a sentence) as a clue to the meaning of a word or phrase.
    - **L.11-12.4.b:** Identify and correctly use patterns of word changes that indicate different meanings or parts of speech (e.g., `conceive`, `conception`, `conceivable`).
    - **L.11-12.4.c:** Consult general and specialized reference materials (e.g., dictionaries, glossaries, thesauruses), both print and digital, to find the pronunciation of a word or determine or clarify its precise meaning, its part of speech, its etymology, or its standard usage.
    - **L.11-12.4.d:** Verify the preliminary determination of the meaning of a word or phrase (e.g., by checking the inferred meaning in context or in a dictionary).
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.11-12.5 sub-points: word relationships">
    - **L.11-12.5.a:** Interpret figures of speech (e.g., hyperbole, paradox) in context and analyze their role in the text.
    - **L.11-12.5.b:** Analyze nuances in the meaning of words with similar denotations.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Classroom application

Plan around two long-form arguments. One literary analysis and one issues-based research argument with cited sources (`W.11-12.8`). Both are scored against the same `W.11-12.1` rubric, with the audience-awareness piece of `W.11-12.1.b` as the highest bar in the document.

Build a "where the text leaves matters uncertain" move into analysis routines. Have students end every analytical paragraph with one sentence that names what the text refuses to settle. This is the small habit that satisfies `RL.11-12.1` and `RI.11-12.1` and signals college-level reading.

Anchor the year on foundational U.S. texts plus a literary partner. `RI.11-12.8` brings in Supreme Court majority opinions and dissents. `RI.11-12.9` widens the document set: Declaration of Independence, Preamble to the Constitution, Bill of Rights, Lincoln's Second Inaugural. `RL.11-12.7` requires at least one Shakespeare play and one play by an American dramatist. Most AP English Language and AP English Literature reading lists already cover this ground.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="What does 'where the text leaves matters uncertain' look like in student writing?">
    The standard (`RL.11-12.1`, `RI.11-12.1`) asks students to name what a text deliberately does not resolve. In literary analysis, that might be an ambiguous ending, an unreliable narrator, or a question the author refuses to answer. In informational reading, it might be a place where the evidence is partial, the source is biased, or two reasonable interpretations remain. In a response, this usually appears as a sentence like, "The text does not say whether \_\_\_, which leaves the question of \_\_\_ open." Acknowledging uncertainty is a marker of college-level reading.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="How do AP English courses map to the grades 11-12 band?">
    AP English Language is typically offered in grade 11 and AP English Literature in grade 12, and both align well with the grades 11-12 band. AP English Language emphasizes rhetorical analysis (`RI.11-12.6`), argument writing with a deep understanding of audience (`W.11-12.1.b`), and seminal U.S. texts (`RI.11-12.9`). AP English Literature emphasizes complex character development (`RL.11-12.3`), the impact of structural choices (`RL.11-12.5`), and analysis of multiple interpretations (`RL.11-12.7`). The standards do not require an AP course; they describe what students should be able to do by the end of grade 12.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
