# What are the Common Core Reading anchor standards?

<div class="answer-box">
The ten Reading anchor standards (CCRA.R.1-10) name what every K-12 student should be able to do with a text: cite evidence, find central ideas, track how characters and ideas develop, interpret word choice, analyze structure, assess point of view, integrate media, evaluate arguments, compare texts, and read complex material independently. Every grade-level Reading: Literature (RL) and Reading: Informational Text (RI) standard, kindergarten through grade 12, ladders up to one of these ten anchors.

## What it covers

- CCRA.R.1: Cite textual evidence.
- CCRA.R.2: Identify central ideas and themes.
- CCRA.R.3: Analyze how characters, events, and ideas develop.
- CCRA.R.4: Interpret word choice in context.
- CCRA.R.5: Analyze text structure.
- CCRA.R.6: Assess point of view and purpose.
- CCRA.R.7: Integrate information across media.
- CCRA.R.8: Evaluate arguments and claims.
- CCRA.R.9: Compare two or more texts on a shared theme.
- CCRA.R.10: Read complex literary and informational text independently.

## Classroom application

Teachers use the Reading anchors to plan instruction that builds the same ten skills from kindergarten through grade 12. The anchor sets the year-end target. The grade-level RL and RI standards underneath tell you what that target looks like at your grade. When you write a reading rubric, each criterion can map back to one anchor, which makes vertical alignment with the grade below and the grade above easier to defend. Many state assessments report reading scores by anchor (citing evidence, central idea, structure, and so on), so anchor-aligned feedback also lines up with the data families and principals already see.
