# What are the Common Core ELA standards for Grade 8?

<div class="answer-box">
Grade 8 students cite the textual evidence that most strongly supports their analysis (`RL.8.1`, `RI.8.1`). Argument writing now acknowledges and distinguishes the writer's claim from opposing claims (`W.8.1.a`). `RI.8.8` adds recognizing irrelevant evidence. `RI.8.9` asks students to analyze cases where two texts disagree on fact or interpretation.

## What it covers

- Reading Literature (`RL.8`) and Reading Informational Text (`RI.8`): citing the evidence that most strongly supports analysis, analyzing how lines of dialogue or incidents propel action, comparing structural choices across texts, and analyzing conflicting information across sources.
- Writing (`W.8`): argument that distinguishes claims from opposing claims, informative writing with well-chosen evidence, narrative with reflection, and self-generated research questions.
- Speaking and Listening (`SL.8`): discussions that qualify views in light of evidence, evaluating motives behind a presentation, and presenting with valid reasoning.
- Language (`L.8`): verbals (gerunds, participles, infinitives); active and passive voice; indicative, imperative, interrogative, conditional, and subjunctive moods; ellipses; and verbal irony.
- Content-area literacy: `RH.6-8`, `RST.6-8`, and `WHST.6-8` continue at this grade band and end with grade 8.

## What changes this year

Grade 8 is the on-ramp to high school.

The evidence standard tightens from "several pieces" (`RL.7.1`, `RI.7.1`) to "the textual evidence that most strongly" supports the analysis. The phrase most strongly asks students to discriminate among evidence and pick the best one, not stockpile examples.

Argument writing matches that move. `W.8.1.a` asks students to distinguish their claim from opposing claims, one step closer to the high school move of developing both sides fairly. `RI.8.8` and `SL.8.3` add the work of recognizing when irrelevant evidence shows up. `RI.8.9` asks students to analyze cases where two texts disagree on fact or interpretation.

Language is the most grammatically demanding it has been in the K-8 progression. Verbals (`L.8.1.a`), active and passive voice (`L.8.1.b`), and the five moods (`L.8.1.c`) all land in the same year.

  <Accordion title="W.8.1 sub-points: argument writing">
    - **W.8.1.a:** Introduce claim(s), acknowledge and distinguish the claim(s) from alternate or opposing claims, and organize the reasons and evidence logically.
    - **W.8.1.b:** Support claim(s) with logical reasoning and relevant evidence, using accurate, credible sources and demonstrating an understanding of the topic or text.
    - **W.8.1.c:** Use words, phrases, and clauses to create cohesion and clarify the relationships among claim(s), counterclaims, reasons, and evidence.
    - **W.8.1.d:** Establish and maintain a formal style.
    - **W.8.1.e:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the argument presented.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="W.8.2 sub-points: informative or explanatory writing">
    - **W.8.2.a:** Introduce a topic clearly, previewing what is to follow; organize ideas, concepts, and information into broader categories; include formatting (e.g., headings), graphics (e.g., charts, tables), and multimedia when useful to aiding comprehension.
    - **W.8.2.b:** Develop the topic with relevant, well-chosen facts, definitions, concrete details, quotations, or other information and examples.
    - **W.8.2.c:** Use appropriate and varied transitions to create cohesion and clarify the relationships among ideas and concepts.
    - **W.8.2.d:** Use precise language and domain-specific vocabulary to inform about or explain the topic.
    - **W.8.2.e:** Establish and maintain a formal style.
    - **W.8.2.f:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the information or explanation presented.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="W.8.3 sub-points: narrative writing">

    - **W.8.3.c:** Use a variety of transition words, phrases, and clauses to convey sequence, signal shifts from one time frame or setting to another, and show the relationships among experiences and events.
    - **W.8.3.d:** Use precise words and phrases, relevant descriptive details, and sensory language to capture the action and convey experiences and events.
    - **W.8.3.e:** Provide a conclusion that follows from and reflects on the narrated experiences or events.

## Classroom application

The hardest grade 8 habit to teach, and the most useful one, is the "most strongly" move. After reading a passage, ask students to find three pieces of evidence for a claim, then pick the strongest one and defend the choice. That mini-lesson lines up with `RL.8.1`, `RI.8.1`, and `RI.8.8` (recognizing irrelevant evidence) and prepares students for the grade 9 to 10 standard, which asks them to cite strong and thorough textual evidence. Argument essays should now have a real introduction that acknowledges and distinguishes the writer's claim from the opposing claim (`W.8.1.a`), which usually takes two to three sentences of setup.

Grammar at grade 8 is denser than at any earlier grade. Voice and mood (`L.8.1.b-d` and `L.8.3.a`) reward direct instruction with student writing in hand. A useful five-minute lesson is to take a passive-voice sentence from a recent piece of student writing and rewrite it in active voice on the board, naming who is doing the action. Subjunctive mood (`If I were ___, I would ___`) shows up rarely in student writing, which is exactly why it benefits from a few targeted examples each marking period.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How does grade 8 argument writing prepare students for high school?">
    The grade 8 standard (`W.8.1.a`) asks students to acknowledge and distinguish their claims from opposing claims. The grades 9 to 10 standard (`W.9-10.1.a`) raises that to developing claims and counterclaims fairly, supplying evidence for both, and pointing out the strengths and limitations of each. The grades 11 to 12 standard adds that students must anticipate the audience's knowledge level, concerns, values, and possible biases. The grade 8 move sets up that whole progression.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="When do students need to identify irrelevant evidence?">
    Grade 8 is the first year the standards name this directly. `RI.8.8` and `SL.8.3` both ask students to recognize when irrelevant evidence is introduced. In practice, this means students can read or hear an argument, point to a piece of evidence, and explain why it does not support the claim. This is the same skill they will use on state assessments at grade 8 and on high school argument analysis later.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
