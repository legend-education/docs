# What are the Common Core ELA standards for Grades 9-10?

<div class="answer-box">

Reading adds seminal U.S. documents and rhetorical analysis. Most schools split the band into English I and English II.

## What it covers

- Reading literature (`RL.9-10`) and informational text (`RI.9-10`): strong textual evidence, complex character development, cumulative impact of word choice, structural choices, rhetorical analysis, fallacious reasoning, and seminal U.S. documents.
- Writing (`W.9-10`): argument that develops claims and counterclaims fairly, informative writing with extended definitions, narrative with multiple plot lines, and sustained research from authoritative sources.
- Speaking and listening (`SL.9-10`): initiating discussions, pushing conversations toward broader themes, evaluating speakers for fallacious reasoning, and presenting with digital media.
- Language (`L.9-10`): parallel structure, varied phrases and clauses, semicolons and colons, MLA or another discipline-appropriate style, and figures of speech like euphemism and oxymoron.
- Content-area literacy: `RH.9-10`, `RST.9-10`, and `WHST.9-10` appear for history and science classrooms.

## What changes this year

The standards run on a two-year band. They describe what students should be able to do by the end of grade 10. Most schools split the band into English I and English II.

Evidence becomes strong and thorough. This is the high school version of the grade 8 "most strongly" standard. Expect students to anchor analysis in several specific quotes or details.

Argument writing now takes the counterclaim seriously. At `W.9-10.1.b`, students develop both the claim and the counterclaim fairly with evidence on each side, including the strengths and limitations of both.

Reading adds seminal U.S. documents (`RI.9-10.9`) and rhetorical analysis (`RI.9-10.6`). This is why most state high school assessments pull from texts like Washington's Farewell Address, the Gettysburg Address, or King's "Letter from Birmingham Jail."

### Writing (`W.9-10`)

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="W.9-10.1 sub-points: argument writing">
    - **W.9-10.1.a:** Introduce precise claim(s), distinguish the claim(s) from alternate or opposing claims, and create an organization that establishes clear relationships among claim(s), counterclaims, reasons, and evidence.
    - **W.9-10.1.b:** Develop claim(s) and counterclaims fairly, supplying evidence for each while pointing out the strengths and limitations of both in a manner that anticipates the audience's knowledge level and concerns.
    - **W.9-10.1.c:** Use words, phrases, and clauses to link the major sections of the text, create cohesion, and clarify the relationships between claim(s) and reasons, between reasons and evidence, and between claim(s) and counterclaims.
    - **W.9-10.1.d:** Establish and maintain a formal style and objective tone while attending to the norms and conventions of the discipline in which they are writing.
    - **W.9-10.1.e:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the argument presented.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="W.9-10.2 sub-points: informative or explanatory writing">
    - **W.9-10.2.a:** Introduce a topic; organize complex ideas, concepts, and information to make important connections and distinctions; include formatting (e.g., headings), graphics (e.g., figures, tables), and multimedia when useful to aiding comprehension.
    - **W.9-10.2.b:** Develop the topic with well-chosen, relevant, and sufficient facts, extended definitions, concrete details, quotations, or other information and examples appropriate to the audience's knowledge of the topic.
    - **W.9-10.2.c:** Use appropriate and varied transitions to link the major sections of the text, create cohesion, and clarify the relationships among complex ideas and concepts.
    - **W.9-10.2.d:** Use precise language and domain-specific vocabulary to manage the complexity of the topic.
    - **W.9-10.2.e:** Establish and maintain a formal style and objective tone while attending to the norms and conventions of the discipline in which they are writing.
    - **W.9-10.2.f:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the information or explanation presented (e.g., articulating implications or the significance of the topic).
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="W.9-10.3 sub-points: narrative writing">
    - **W.9-10.3.a:** Engage and orient the reader by setting out a problem, situation, or observation, establishing one or multiple point(s) of view, and introducing a narrator and/or characters; create a smooth progression of experiences or events.
    - **W.9-10.3.b:** Use narrative techniques, such as dialogue, pacing, description, reflection, and multiple plot lines, to develop experiences, events, and/or characters.
    - **W.9-10.3.c:** Use a variety of techniques to sequence events so that they build on one another to create a coherent whole.
    - **W.9-10.3.d:** Use precise words and phrases, telling details, and sensory language to convey a vivid picture of the experiences, events, setting, and/or characters.
    - **W.9-10.3.e:** Provide a conclusion that follows from and reflects on what is experienced, observed, or resolved over the course of the narrative.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="W.9-10.9 sub-points: using textual evidence in writing">
    - **W.9-10.9.a:** Apply grades 9-10 Reading standards to literature.
    - **W.9-10.9.b:** Apply grades 9-10 Reading standards to literary nonfiction.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### Speaking and Listening (`SL.9-10`)

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="SL.9-10.1 sub-points: collaborative discussion">
    - **SL.9-10.1.a:** Come to discussions prepared, having read and researched material under study; explicitly draw on that preparation by referring to evidence from texts and other research on the topic or issue to stimulate a thoughtful, well-reasoned exchange of ideas.
    - **SL.9-10.1.b:** Work with peers to set rules for collegial discussions and decision-making (e.g., informal consensus, taking votes on key issues, presentation of alternate views), clear goals and deadlines, and individual roles as needed.
    - **SL.9-10.1.c:** Propel conversations by posing and responding to questions that relate the current discussion to broader themes or larger ideas; actively incorporate others into the discussion; and clarify, verify, or challenge ideas and conclusions.
    - **SL.9-10.1.d:** Respond thoughtfully to diverse perspectives, summarize points of agreement and disagreement, and, when warranted, qualify or justify their own views and understanding and make new connections in light of the evidence and reasoning presented.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### Language (`L.9-10`)

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="L.9-10.1 sub-points: grammar and usage">
    - **L.9-10.1.a:** Use parallel structure.
    - **L.9-10.1.b:** Use various types of phrases (noun, verb, adjectival, adverbial, participial, prepositional, absolute) and clauses (independent, dependent; noun, relative, adverbial) to convey specific meanings and add variety and interest to writing or presentations.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.9-10.2 sub-points: capitalization, punctuation, and spelling">
    - **L.9-10.2.a:** Use a semicolon (and perhaps a conjunctive adverb) to link two or more closely related independent clauses.
    - **L.9-10.2.b:** Use a colon to introduce a list or quotation.
    - **L.9-10.2.c:** Spell correctly.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.9-10.3 sub-points: language for effect">
    - **L.9-10.3.a:** Write and edit work so that it conforms to the guidelines in a style manual (e.g., MLA Handbook, Turabian's Manual for Writers) appropriate for the discipline and writing type.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.9-10.4 sub-points: word meaning">
    - **L.9-10.4.a:** Use context (e.g., the overall meaning of a sentence, paragraph, or text; a word's position or function in a sentence) as a clue to the meaning of a word or phrase.
    - **L.9-10.4.b:** Identify and correctly use patterns of word changes that indicate different meanings or parts of speech (e.g., `analyze`, `analysis`, `analytical`; `advocate`, `advocacy`).
    - **L.9-10.4.c:** Consult general and specialized reference materials (e.g., dictionaries, glossaries, thesauruses), both print and digital, to find the pronunciation of a word or determine or clarify its precise meaning, its part of speech, or its etymology.
    - **L.9-10.4.d:** Verify the preliminary determination of the meaning of a word or phrase (e.g., by checking the inferred meaning in context or in a dictionary).
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="L.9-10.5 sub-points: word relationships">
    - **L.9-10.5.a:** Interpret figures of speech (e.g., euphemism, oxymoron) in context and analyze their role in the text.
    - **L.9-10.5.b:** Analyze nuances in the meaning of words with similar denotations.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Classroom application

Make the argument essay the spine of the year. Grade 8 introduced the counterclaim; at grades 9-10, the counterclaim is a graded part of the rubric. A common structure: introduce the claim, give two body paragraphs of evidence, devote one body paragraph to the strongest counterclaim and its rebuttal, and end with a conclusion that re-grounds the position. `W.9-10.1.d` adds the discipline-conventions piece, which is the bridge to college and career writing.

Pair a seminal U.S. document with a literary text. Build a unit around the Gettysburg Address, the "Letter from Birmingham Jail," or Roosevelt's Four Freedoms speech, then read a literary text that lives on the same theme. The rhetorical analysis standard at `RI.9-10.6` is the natural connector: students analyze how each author uses rhetoric to advance a point of view.

Teach fallacious reasoning by name. `RI.9-10.8` and `SL.9-10.3` ask students to identify false statements and fallacious reasoning in arguments and in speakers. A short unit on the most common fallacies (straw man, ad hominem, false dilemma) pays off across the year.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How does the two-year band work for grading and assessment?">
    The standards describe what students should be able to do by the end of grade 10. Most schools assess against the full band on summative work and use the grade 9 reading-level expectation (`RL.9-10.10`, `RI.9-10.10`) for benchmark setting in grade 9. State assessments at grade 10 or grade 11 are usually written against the full grades 9-10 band. The band is not a license to delay instruction; the document is clear that students should be proficient with scaffolding by the end of grade 9 and independent at the top of the band by the end of grade 10.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="What counts as a seminal U.S. document?">
    The standard names Washington's Farewell Address, the Gettysburg Address, Roosevelt's Four Freedoms speech, and King's "Letter from Birmingham Jail" as examples. State exams pull from a similar list, often including the Declaration of Independence, the U.S. Constitution and the Bill of Rights, Federalist Papers, key Supreme Court opinions, and major presidential addresses. The standard expects students to analyze how these documents address related themes and concepts, not just to summarize them.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
