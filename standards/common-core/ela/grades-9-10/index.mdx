# What are the Common Core ELA standards for Grades 9-10?

<div class="answer-box">
Grades 9-10 sit together as one two-year band. The biggest shift is argument writing: students now develop both their claim and the strongest counterclaim, with evidence on each side (`W.9-10.1.b`). Reading adds seminal U.S. documents and rhetorical analysis. Most schools split the band into English I and English II.

## What it covers

- Reading literature (`RL.9-10`) and informational text (`RI.9-10`): strong textual evidence, complex character development, cumulative impact of word choice, structural choices, rhetorical analysis, fallacious reasoning, and seminal U.S. documents.
- Writing (`W.9-10`): argument that develops claims and counterclaims fairly, informative writing with extended definitions, narrative with multiple plot lines, and sustained research from authoritative sources.
- Speaking and listening (`SL.9-10`): initiating discussions, pushing conversations toward broader themes, evaluating speakers for fallacious reasoning, and presenting with digital media.
- Language (`L.9-10`): parallel structure, varied phrases and clauses, semicolons and colons, MLA or another discipline-appropriate style, and figures of speech like euphemism and oxymoron.
- Content-area literacy: `RH.9-10`, `RST.9-10`, and `WHST.9-10` appear for history and science classrooms.

## What changes this year

The standards run on a two-year band. They describe what students should be able to do by the end of grade 10. Most schools split the band into English I and English II.

Evidence becomes strong and thorough. This is the high school version of the grade 8 "most strongly" standard. Expect students to anchor analysis in several specific quotes or details.

Argument writing now takes the counterclaim seriously. At `W.9-10.1.b`, students develop both the claim and the counterclaim fairly with evidence on each side, including the strengths and limitations of both.

Reading adds seminal U.S. documents (`RI.9-10.9`) and rhetorical analysis (`RI.9-10.6`). This is why most state high school assessments pull from texts like Washington's Farewell Address, the Gettysburg Address, or King's "Letter from Birmingham Jail."

## Classroom application

Make the argument essay the spine of the year. Grade 8 introduced the counterclaim; at grades 9-10, the counterclaim is a graded part of the rubric. A common structure: introduce the claim, give two body paragraphs of evidence, devote one body paragraph to the strongest counterclaim and its rebuttal, and end with a conclusion that re-grounds the position. `W.9-10.1.d` adds the discipline-conventions piece, which is the bridge to college and career writing.

Pair a seminal U.S. document with a literary text. Build a unit around the Gettysburg Address, the "Letter from Birmingham Jail," or Roosevelt's Four Freedoms speech, then read a literary text that lives on the same theme. The rhetorical analysis standard at `RI.9-10.6` is the natural connector: students analyze how each author uses rhetoric to advance a point of view.

Teach fallacious reasoning by name. `RI.9-10.8` and `SL.9-10.3` ask students to identify false statements and fallacious reasoning in arguments and in speakers. A short unit on the most common fallacies (straw man, ad hominem, false dilemma) pays off across the year.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How does the two-year band work for grading and assessment?">
    The standards describe what students should be able to do by the end of grade 10. Most schools assess against the full band on summative work and use the grade 9 reading-level expectation (`RL.9-10.10`, `RI.9-10.10`) for benchmark setting in grade 9. State assessments at grade 10 or grade 11 are usually written against the full grades 9-10 band. The band is not a license to delay instruction; the document is clear that students should be proficient with scaffolding by the end of grade 9 and independent at the top of the band by the end of grade 10.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What counts as a seminal U.S. document?">
    The standard names Washington's Farewell Address, the Gettysburg Address, Roosevelt's Four Freedoms speech, and King's "Letter from Birmingham Jail" as examples. State exams pull from a similar list, often including the Declaration of Independence, the U.S. Constitution and the Bill of Rights, Federalist Papers, key Supreme Court opinions, and major presidential addresses. The standard expects students to analyze how these documents address related themes and concepts, not just to summarize them.
