# What are the Common Core Literacy in History/Social Studies standards for Grades 9-10?

<div class="answer-box">
Grades 9-10 raise the reading bar in history class. Students now cite textual evidence with attention to dates and origin (`RH.9-10.1`), assess an author's claims by checking the reasoning and evidence (`RH.9-10.8`), and compare authors' treatment of the same events (`RH.9-10.9`). Writing in history adds the counterclaim. This band is shared with English I and English II ELA courses.

## What it covers

- Reading in history and social studies (`RH.9-10`): citing textual evidence with attention to date and origin, central ideas across a source, vocabulary in historical context, structure of an account, point of view, the role of visual sources, evaluation of an author's claims and evidence, and comparison across sources.
- Writing in history, science, and technical subjects (`WHST.9-10`): argument with claims and counterclaims, informative writing with disciplinary content, short or sustained research projects, and integrating sources with citation.
- Shared territory: `WHST.9-10` is the writing set for history, science, and technical subjects.

## What changes this band

The reading bar moves from "what does the source say" to "how reliable is the source." `RH.9-10.1` adds attention to date and origin. Students note when the source was written and who wrote it, then use that information when they weigh the evidence.

Source comparison becomes part of the work. `RH.9-10.9` asks students to compare the way different authors treat the same historical event. A Kennedy speech and a Khrushchev memoir on the Cuban Missile Crisis read like two different events. That gap is the lesson.

Cause and sequence get tighter. `RH.9-10.3` asks students to analyze a series of events and decide whether earlier events caused later ones or simply preceded them. This is the "post hoc" test, and it lives at the heart of historical reasoning.

Writing in history adds the counterclaim. `WHST.9-10.1` mirrors the grades 9-10 ELA argument standard. Students develop both their claim and a competing claim with evidence for each. This is the bridge from middle school source-based writing to college history papers.

  <Accordion title="WHST.9-10.1 sub-points: argument writing">
    - **WHST.9-10.1.a:** Introduce precise claim(s), distinguish the claim(s) from alternate or opposing claims, and create an organization that establishes clear relationships among the claim(s), counterclaims, reasons, and evidence.
    - **WHST.9-10.1.b:** Develop claim(s) and counterclaims fairly, supplying data and evidence for each while pointing out the strengths and limitations of both claim(s) and counterclaims in a discipline-appropriate form and in a manner that anticipates the audience's knowledge level and concerns.
    - **WHST.9-10.1.c:** Use words, phrases, and clauses to link the major sections of the text, create cohesion, and clarify the relationships between claim(s) and reasons, between reasons and evidence, and between claim(s) and counterclaims.
    - **WHST.9-10.1.d:** Establish and maintain a formal style and objective tone while attending to the norms and conventions of the discipline in which they are writing.
    - **WHST.9-10.1.e:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from or supports the argument presented.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="WHST.9-10.2 sub-points: informative or explanatory writing">

    - **WHST.9-10.2.b:** Develop the topic with well-chosen, relevant, and sufficient facts, extended definitions, concrete details, quotations, or other information and examples appropriate to the audience's knowledge of the topic.
    - **WHST.9-10.2.c:** Use varied transitions and sentence structures to link the major sections of the text, create cohesion, and clarify the relationships among ideas and concepts.
    - **WHST.9-10.2.d:** Use precise language and domain-specific vocabulary to manage the complexity of the topic and convey a style appropriate to the discipline and context as well as to the expertise of likely readers.
    - **WHST.9-10.2.e:** Establish and maintain a formal style and objective tone while attending to the norms and conventions of the discipline in which they are writing.
    - **WHST.9-10.2.f:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the information or explanation presented (e.g., articulating implications or the significance of the topic).

## Classroom application

Start every primary source with a sourcing line. Before reading, students record three facts: who wrote it, when, and for what audience. By June, this is a habit. It is `RH.9-10.1` in action and the simplest way to push reading past summary.

Build one document-based question per quarter. Pose a focused historical question and give students three to five sources. The deliverable is an argument with a counterclaim, scored against `WHST.9-10.1`. This is the same shape as a college history paper, just shorter.

Compare accounts on purpose, not as filler. When you teach the same event from two sources, name the disagreement out loud. `RH.9-10.6` and `RH.9-10.9` are the work, and they only show up when students see the gap between two accounts.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How do these standards line up with AP U.S. History or AP World History?">
    Closely. The DBQ (document-based question) and LEQ (long essay question) on AP history exams are scored against rubrics that mirror `RH.9-10.1`, `RH.9-10.8`, `RH.9-10.9`, and `WHST.9-10.1`. A grade 9 or 10 student who can source a document, assess an author's claims, and write a source-based argument with a counterclaim is doing the same intellectual work the AP rubric asks for, at a shorter length.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Where does ELA stop and history start?">
    The two strands overlap on purpose. Argument writing at `WHST.9-10.1` and at `W.9-10.1` is the same skill scored in a different setting. The history teacher scores it on a U.S. history prompt with primary sources. The ELA teacher scores it on a literary or rhetorical prompt. Most schools coordinate the two so students see one rubric across classes, with discipline-specific evidence requirements.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How do these standards line up with the SAT or ACT reading sections?">
    The reading work is recognizable. Both the SAT and ACT lean heavily on `RH.9-10.1` (cite specific textual evidence), `RH.9-10.6` (analyze point of view), and `RH.9-10.8` (assess an author's reasoning and evidence). The SAT's "Founding Documents" passage and the ACT's social science passage both reward the sourcing and corroboration habits these standards describe. A student who can source a primary document in history class will recognize the same moves on the test.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What if my school does not have a separate history class until grade 10?">
    `RH.9-10` and `WHST.9-10` are written as a two-year band, so they describe what students should be able to do by the end of grade 10. If your students only see a history course in grade 10, you have one year to cover the band. In practice this means front-loading sourcing (`RH.9-10.1`) and source comparison (`RH.9-10.9`) early in the fall and building toward a document-based argument by midyear. The grade 9 ELA team usually picks up some of the argument-writing scaffolding (`W.9-10.1`) so students arrive ready to write from sources.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
