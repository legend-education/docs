# What are the Common Core Literacy in History/Social Studies standards for Grades 11-12?

<div class="answer-box">
Grades 11-12 are the K-12 endpoint for reading and writing in history. Students evaluate authors' claims against other sources (`RH.11-12.8`), integrate information from several sources into a coherent account (`RH.11-12.9`), and write source-based arguments that anticipate the audience's values and possible biases (`WHST.11-12.1`). This is the bar that DBQs and college history papers are scored against.

## What it covers

- Reading in history and social studies (`RH.11-12`): citing textual evidence and acknowledging what the source leaves uncertain, central ideas across a complex source, complex analysis of events and ideas, advanced vocabulary in historical context, structural analysis, comparing authors' points of view on the same event, evaluating authors' claims against other sources, and integrating information from several sources.
- Writing in history, science, and technical subjects (`WHST.11-12`): argument that anticipates the audience's knowledge, values, and possible biases; informative writing where each element builds on the last; sustained research from authoritative sources; integrating sources without overreliance.
- Shared territory: `WHST.11-12` is the writing set for history, science, and technical subjects.

## What changes this band

Reading sources now means weighing what they leave open. `RH.11-12.1` adds attention to gaps and discrepancies in a source. A treaty might not say what the parties privately wanted. A speech might leave out the political context that made it possible. Students name those gaps in their analysis.

Integration becomes the work. `RH.11-12.9` asks students to integrate information from several sources into a coherent understanding of a problem or question. This is the move from comparing sources side by side to building one argument out of several. `RH.11-12.7` adds the quantitative dimension. Students fold in charts and research data alongside the text.

Corroboration moves to the center of evaluation. `RH.11-12.8` asks students to evaluate an author's premises and claims by corroborating or challenging them with other information. A speech read on its own is rarely enough. A student is expected to check it against a second source before accepting the argument.

Writing in history reaches its endpoint. `WHST.11-12.1` matches the grades 11-12 ELA argument standard. Students develop both claims and counterclaims fairly and thoroughly, and they anticipate the audience's knowledge, concerns, values, and possible biases. This is what AP DBQs and college history papers are scored against.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="WHST.11-12.1 sub-points: argument writing">
    - **WHST.11-12.1.a:** Introduce precise, knowledgeable claim(s), establish the significance of the claim(s), distinguish the claim(s) from alternate or opposing claims, and create an organization that logically sequences the claim(s), counterclaims, reasons, and evidence.
    - **WHST.11-12.1.b:** Develop claim(s) and counterclaims fairly and thoroughly, supplying the most relevant data and evidence for each while pointing out the strengths and limitations of both claim(s) and counterclaims in a discipline-appropriate form that anticipates the audience's knowledge level, concerns, values, and possible biases.
    - **WHST.11-12.1.c:** Use words, phrases, and clauses as well as varied syntax to link the major sections of the text, create cohesion, and clarify the relationships between claim(s) and reasons, between reasons and evidence, and between claim(s) and counterclaims.
    - **WHST.11-12.1.d:** Establish and maintain a formal style and objective tone while attending to the norms and conventions of the discipline in which they are writing.
    - **WHST.11-12.1.e:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from or supports the argument presented.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="WHST.11-12.2 sub-points: informative or explanatory writing">
    - **WHST.11-12.2.a:** Introduce a topic and organize complex ideas, concepts, and information so that each new element builds on that which precedes it to create a unified whole; include formatting (e.g., headings), graphics (e.g., figures, tables), and multimedia when useful to aiding comprehension.
    - **WHST.11-12.2.b:** Develop the topic thoroughly by selecting the most significant and relevant facts, extended definitions, concrete details, quotations, or other information and examples appropriate to the audience's knowledge of the topic.
    - **WHST.11-12.2.c:** Use varied transitions and sentence structures to link the major sections of the text, create cohesion, and clarify the relationships among complex ideas and concepts.
    - **WHST.11-12.2.d:** Use precise language, domain-specific vocabulary, and techniques such as metaphor, simile, and analogy to manage the complexity of the topic and convey a style appropriate to the discipline and context as well as to the expertise of likely readers.
    - **WHST.11-12.2.e:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the information or explanation provided (e.g., articulating implications or the significance of the topic).
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Classroom application

Run a multi-source research argument as the year's anchor task. Pose a focused historical or civics question. Give students six to ten sources (primary and secondary). Require an argument that uses at least four of them and addresses a counterclaim. This is `RH.11-12.9` and `WHST.11-12.1` together, and it is the same shape as a college history paper.

Score corroboration explicitly. Add a rubric line for `RH.11-12.8`: did the student check the source's claims against another source? Most student arguments at this level fail not for lack of evidence but for taking one source at face value.

Teach uncertainty as a skill. At every analysis checkpoint, ask students to name one thing the source does not settle. This is `RH.11-12.1` in daily practice and the move that separates a good high school argument from a competent college one.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How does this map to the AP history exams?">
    The AP U.S. History, AP European History, and AP World History rubrics for the DBQ and LEQ map directly onto these standards. Sourcing, contextualization, corroboration, and a complex argument are scored against the same skills named in `RH.11-12.1`, `RH.11-12.6`, `RH.11-12.8`, and `WHST.11-12.1`. The AP rubric is a shorter, more specific version of these standards.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What kinds of sources should students read at this band?">
    A mix. Foundational U.S. documents (Declaration of Independence, Federalist papers, Bill of Rights, Lincoln's Second Inaugural) appear in both the ELA standards and the history standards. State frameworks usually pair these with primary sources from the period being studied (slave narratives, Reconstruction-era newspapers, mid-twentieth-century policy memos) and secondary sources from historians. The standards do not name a reading list. The state framework usually does.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="How do I score a DBQ against WHST.11-12.1 and the AP rubric at the same time?">
    Build one rubric. The AP DBQ rubric and `WHST.11-12.1` ask for the same moves under different names. AP "thesis" matches `WHST.11-12.1.a`. AP "contextualization" and "evidence beyond the documents" map to `RH.11-12.1` and `RH.11-12.9`. AP "sourcing" maps to `RH.11-12.6` and `RH.11-12.8`. AP "complexity" matches `WHST.11-12.1.b` (counterclaim handled fairly and thoroughly with attention to audience). When you list both labels on the same rubric line, students stop treating AP prep and standards-based work as two different jobs.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What counts as an 'authoritative source' at this band?">
    `WHST.11-12.8` calls for "authoritative" sources without listing them. In practice the bar lands in a few places:

    - Primary sources from the period under study, such as treaties, speeches, letters, and government reports.
    - Peer-reviewed secondary sources by historians, such as academic articles and university press books.
    - Reference works that cite their own sources, such as encyclopedias of American history.

    General-purpose news sites and unsourced blog posts do not clear the bar at this level. A student who can name why a source is authoritative is already doing the work `RH.11-12.8` describes.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
