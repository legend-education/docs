# What are the Common Core Literacy in History/Social Studies standards for Grades 6-8?

<div class="answer-box">
These standards live inside the ELA document but are written for middle school history and social studies teachers. Reading (`RH.6-8`) covers working with primary and secondary sources: citing specific evidence, identifying central ideas, and judging an author's purpose. Writing (`WHST.6-8`) covers argument and informative writing in history class, plus short research with sources. ELA teachers do not own these standards alone.
</div>

## What it covers

- Reading in history and social studies (`RH.6-8`): citing specific textual evidence, central ideas, vocabulary and word choice in historical context, text structure, point of view and purpose, and using visual sources alongside text.
- Writing in history, science, and technical subjects (`WHST.6-8`): argument and informative writing with discipline-specific content, short research projects, and gathering information from multiple sources.
- Shared territory: `WHST` is written once and applies across history, science, and technical subjects. The H/SS reading standards (`RH`) and the science reading standards (`RST`) split into separate sets.

## What changes this band

Middle school is where students start reading like historians. Until now, "reading" usually meant a textbook chapter or a literary text. `RH.6-8` brings in primary sources: a letter from a soldier, a paragraph from a treaty, a political cartoon.

The reading work is small at this band but new. Students cite specific textual evidence (`RH.6-8.1`) and identify the central idea of a source on its own terms, separate from prior knowledge (`RH.6-8.2`). They start to notice the author's purpose and loaded language (`RH.6-8.6`), and they use visual sources like maps and photographs as evidence (`RH.6-8.7`).

Writing in history class becomes its own genre. `WHST.6-8.1` is the history argument: a claim with reasons and evidence from sources. `WHST.6-8.2` is the informative piece, where students explain a topic with quotations and facts from the sources they cite. Both expect source citations, even in middle school.

Comparison shows up at the easy end. `RH.6-8.9` asks students to look at one primary source next to one secondary source on the same topic and explain how they relate. This is the warm-up for the multi-source work that arrives in grades 9-10.

  <Accordion title="WHST.6-8.1 sub-points: argument writing">
    - **WHST.6-8.1.a:** Introduce claim(s) about a topic or issue, acknowledge and distinguish the claim(s) from alternate or opposing claims, and organize the reasons and evidence logically.
    - **WHST.6-8.1.b:** Support claim(s) with logical reasoning and relevant, accurate data and evidence that demonstrate an understanding of the topic or text, using credible sources.
    - **WHST.6-8.1.c:** Use words, phrases, and clauses to create cohesion and clarify the relationships among claim(s), counterclaims, reasons, and evidence.
    - **WHST.6-8.1.d:** Establish and maintain a formal style.
    - **WHST.6-8.1.e:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the argument presented.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="WHST.6-8.2 sub-points: informative or explanatory writing">
    - **WHST.6-8.2.a:** Introduce a topic clearly, previewing what is to follow; organize ideas, concepts, and information into broader categories as appropriate to achieving purpose; include formatting (e.g., headings), graphics (e.g., charts, tables), and multimedia when useful to aiding comprehension.
    - **WHST.6-8.2.b:** Develop the topic with relevant, well-chosen facts, definitions, concrete details, quotations, or other information and examples.
    - **WHST.6-8.2.c:** Use appropriate and varied transitions to create cohesion and clarify the relationships among ideas and concepts.
    - **WHST.6-8.2.d:** Use precise language and domain-specific vocabulary to inform about or explain the topic.
    - **WHST.6-8.2.e:** Establish and maintain a formal style and objective tone.
    - **WHST.6-8.2.f:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the information or explanation presented.
  </Accordion>

<Note>
`WHST` does not include a narrative writing standard (`CCRA.W.3`). Narrative work belongs to the ELA standards. In history and science, students write argument and informative pieces, not stories.
</Note>

## Classroom application

Teach citation as a routine, not a unit. Every time students reference a source, ask for the title or the line. By eighth grade, "according to the Declaration of Independence" or "in paragraph 3 of the source" should sound normal. This is `RH.6-8.1` in daily practice.

Use one short source at a time, not a packet. A single political cartoon or a paragraph from a treaty gives students enough to read closely. Long packets push them back into skim-and-summarize, which is what `RH.6-8.2` and `RH.6-8.6` are trying to break.

Build one source-based argument per quarter. Pose a focused question ("Was the New Deal a success?") and give students three or four short sources to work with. `WHST.6-8.1` is the target genre, and `WHST.6-8.8` is the source-handling skill. Score both with the same rubric the ELA team uses for opinion or argument writing.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Who owns these standards, the ELA teacher or the social studies teacher?">
    The social studies teacher. The standards live inside the ELA document because the authors wanted reading and writing to keep growing after grade 5, when ELA stops being a separate subject for many students. But `RH.6-8` and `WHST.6-8` describe reading and writing inside a history classroom, not inside ELA. Most schools share responsibility. The ELA team teaches argument structure and citation conventions. The social studies team teaches how to read a primary source and write a source-based argument.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Do these standards replace the state's social studies standards?">
    No. State social studies standards still describe what content students should learn: U.S. history through Reconstruction, world geography, civics. `RH.6-8` and `WHST.6-8` describe how students should read and write inside that content. The two work together. State content standards set the topic, and the literacy standards set the reading and writing bar.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Where do these standards leave room for elementary social studies?">
    They do not cover it. Common Core has no content-area literacy standards for K-5. Below grade 6, reading and writing in social studies fall under the main ELA standards (`RI` for informational reading, `W` for writing). Elementary teachers still teach communities, geography, and U.S. history, but the reading and writing expectations come from the K-5 ELA standards. For the content side at the elementary level, most states draw on the C3 Framework or their own state social studies framework.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What does a source-based argument look like for an eighth grader?">
    Shorter and more scaffolded than a high school version, but the structure is the same. A solid eighth-grade argument runs three to five paragraphs, opens with a one-sentence claim, and uses two or three short sources you provided. Each body paragraph names one source ("In the source, the author writes...") and quotes one specific line. The conclusion restates the claim and acknowledges one limitation, such as what a source leaves out. That is `WHST.6-8.1` and `WHST.6-8.8` at grade-level: a claim, supporting evidence, and honest source handling.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
