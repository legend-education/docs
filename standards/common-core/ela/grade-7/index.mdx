## What it covers

- Reading Literature (`RL.7`) and Reading Informational Text (`RI.7`): citing several pieces of evidence, tracing a theme or central idea across a whole text, analyzing how elements interact, and comparing fictional portrayals with historical accounts.
- Writing (`W.7`): argument with acknowledged alternate or opposing claims, informative writing that previews structure, narrative with point of view, and research with follow-up questions and standard citation.
- Speaking and Listening (`SL.7`): collegial discussions that track progress, evaluating soundness of reasoning, and presentations that emphasize salient points.
- Language (`L.7`): phrases and clauses, the four sentence types, modifier placement, coordinate-adjective commas, eliminating wordiness, and allusions.
- Content-area literacy: `RH.6-8`, `RST.6-8`, and `WHST.6-8` continue at this grade band.

## What changes this year

Grade 7 raises the evidence bar from one piece of textual evidence to several. A grade 7 response should hang on two to four specific quotes or details.

Argument writing adds the counterclaim. `W.7.1.a` now asks students to acknowledge alternate or opposing claims. This is the move high school writing builds on.

Argument evaluation also gets sharper. At `RI.7.8` and `SL.7.3`, students judge whether the reasoning is sound and the evidence is relevant and sufficient.

Research grows up. Students use their own search terms (`W.7.8`), generate follow-up questions (`W.7.7`), and follow a standard citation format. Language adds the four sentence types at `L.7.1.b`, the grammar machinery for the longer sentences grade 8 and high school will ask for.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="W.7.1 sub-points: argument writing">
    - **W.7.1.a:** Introduce claim(s), acknowledge alternate or opposing claims, and organize the reasons and evidence logically.
    - **W.7.1.b:** Support claim(s) with logical reasoning and relevant evidence, using accurate, credible sources and demonstrating an understanding of the topic or text.
    - **W.7.1.c:** Use words, phrases, and clauses to create cohesion and clarify the relationships among claim(s), reasons, and evidence.
    - **W.7.1.d:** Establish and maintain a formal style.
    - **W.7.1.e:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the argument presented.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="W.7.2 sub-points: informative or explanatory writing">
    - **W.7.2.a:** Introduce a topic clearly, previewing what is to follow; organize ideas, concepts, and information, using strategies such as definition, classification, comparison/contrast, and cause/effect; include formatting (e.g., headings), graphics (e.g., charts, tables), and multimedia when useful to aiding comprehension.
    - **W.7.2.b:** Develop the topic with relevant facts, definitions, concrete details, quotations, or other information and examples.
    - **W.7.2.c:** Use appropriate transitions to create cohesion and clarify the relationships among ideas and concepts.
    - **W.7.2.d:** Use precise language and domain-specific vocabulary to inform about or explain the topic.
    - **W.7.2.e:** Establish and maintain a formal style.
    - **W.7.2.f:** Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the information or explanation presented.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="W.7.3 sub-points: narrative writing">
    - **W.7.3.a:** Engage and orient the reader by establishing a context and point of view and introducing a narrator and/or characters; organize an event sequence that unfolds naturally and logically.
    - **W.7.3.b:** Use narrative techniques, such as dialogue, pacing, and description, to develop experiences, events, and/or characters.
    - **W.7.3.c:** Use a variety of transition words, phrases, and clauses to convey sequence and signal shifts from one time frame or setting to another.
    - **W.7.3.d:** Use precise words and phrases, relevant descriptive details, and sensory language to capture the action and convey experiences and events.
    - **W.7.3.e:** Provide a conclusion that follows from and reflects on the narrated experiences or events.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="W.7.9 sub-points: using textual evidence in writing">
    - **W.7.9.a:** Apply grade 7 Reading standards to literature (e.g., "Compare and contrast a fictional portrayal of a time, place, or character and a historical account of the same period as a means of understanding how authors of fiction use or alter history").
    - **W.7.9.b:** Apply grade 7 Reading standards to literary nonfiction (e.g., "Trace and evaluate the argument and specific claims in a text, assessing whether the reasoning is sound and the evidence is relevant and sufficient to support the claims").
  </Accordion>

## Classroom application

Two grade 7 standards move the most student work: `RL.7.2` and `RI.7.2` (theme or central idea analyzed across the whole text) and `W.7.1.a` (acknowledging alternate or opposing claims).

For theme, students annotate a novel chapter by chapter for an emerging theme, then write a single response that traces how it develops. That is the through-line of grade 7 literary analysis.

For argument, the counterclaim is what separates a grade 7 essay from a grade 6 essay. A typical grade 7 argument structure: claim, two reasons with evidence, one acknowledged counterclaim with a short rebuttal, and a conclusion that follows from the argument.

Research is more rigorous than at grade 6. Students use their own search terms (`W.7.8`), assess credibility without a provided source list, and follow a standard citation format. Most schools adopt MLA in middle school for this, but the standard does not require a specific style.

One smaller change worth teaching directly: `L.7.3.a` asks students to recognize and cut wordiness. This is the year to start teaching deliberate concision.

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="How is grade 7 argument writing different from grade 6 argument writing?">
    The grade 6 standard (`W.6.1.a`) asks students to introduce a claim and organize reasons and evidence. The grade 7 standard (`W.7.1.a`) adds the requirement to acknowledge alternate or opposing claims. That is the formal introduction of counterargument to the K-12 progression. By grade 8, students will be expected to distinguish their claims from opposing claims, and by grades 9-10 to develop both their claims and opposing claims fairly.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What does 'several pieces of textual evidence' mean in practice?">
    The grade 6 standard asks for textual evidence; grade 7 asks for "several pieces"; grade 8 asks for the evidence that "most strongly" supports the analysis. In practice, "several" usually means two to four specific pieces of evidence per response. Most state assessment rubrics for grade 7 score extended responses higher when they include two or more well-chosen quotations or details rather than a single source.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
