# What are the Common Core ELA standards for Kindergarten?

<div class="answer-box">
Kindergarten is the year the alphabet starts behaving like a system. Students learn the letters and the sounds they make, then blend them into short words. By June, kindergartners read a small bank of sight words and use a mix of drawing, dictation, and early writing to put a thought on the page. Most reading standards run "with prompting and support," because the teacher still does much of the lifting.

## What it covers

- Reading Foundational Skills (`RF.K`): print concepts, phonological awareness, phonics, and early fluency.
- Reading Literature (`RL.K`) and Reading Informational Text (`RI.K`): key details, characters and topics, and the work an illustrator does alongside an author.
- Writing (`W.K`): opinion, informative, and narrative pieces built from drawing, dictation, and early writing.
- Speaking and Listening (`SL.K`): turn-taking, retelling, and asking for help.
- Language (`L.K`): naming letters, forming simple sentences, and learning how new words work.

## What changes this year

Kindergarten is where the printed page stops being a picture and starts behaving like a system. Students learn that letters stand for sounds and that words move left to right on the page.

The year covers two big moves. Students go from recognizing letters to producing the sounds those letters make. And they go from labeling a drawing to writing a short opinion about it, often with the teacher's hand near the pencil.

Almost every kindergarten reading and listening standard carries the phrase "with prompting and support." That phrase is the standards' way of saying the teacher does much of the modeling and scaffolding for most of the year.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="RF.K.1 sub-points: print concepts">
    - **RF.K.1.a:** Follow words from left to right, top to bottom, and page by page.
    - **RF.K.1.b:** Recognize that spoken words are represented in written language by specific sequences of letters.
    - **RF.K.1.c:** Understand that words are separated by spaces in print.
    - **RF.K.1.d:** Recognize and name all upper- and lowercase letters of the alphabet.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="RF.K.2 sub-points: phonological awareness">
    - **RF.K.2.a:** Recognize and produce rhyming words.
    - **RF.K.2.b:** Count, pronounce, blend, and segment syllables in spoken words.
    - **RF.K.2.c:** Blend and segment onsets and rimes of single-syllable spoken words.
    - **RF.K.2.d:** Isolate and pronounce the initial, medial vowel, and final sounds (phonemes) in three-phoneme (consonant-vowel-consonant, or CVC) words. (Excludes CVCs ending with /l/, /r/, or /x/.)
    - **RF.K.2.e:** Add or substitute individual sounds (phonemes) in simple, one-syllable words to make new words.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="RF.K.3 sub-points: phonics and word recognition">
    - **RF.K.3.a:** Demonstrate basic knowledge of one-to-one letter-sound correspondences by producing the primary or many of the most frequent sounds for each consonant.
    - **RF.K.3.b:** Associate the long and short sounds with the common spellings (graphemes) for the five major vowels.
    - **RF.K.3.c:** Read common high-frequency words by sight (e.g., `the`, `of`, `to`, `you`, `she`, `my`, `is`, `are`, `do`, `does`).
    - **RF.K.3.d:** Distinguish between similarly spelled words by identifying the sounds of the letters that differ.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="L.K.1 sub-points: grammar and usage">
    - **L.K.1.a:** Print many upper- and lowercase letters.
    - **L.K.1.b:** Use frequently occurring nouns and verbs.
    - **L.K.1.c:** Form regular plural nouns orally by adding /s/ or /es/ (e.g., `dog`, `dogs`; `wish`, `wishes`).
    - **L.K.1.d:** Understand and use question words (interrogatives) (e.g., `who`, `what`, `where`, `when`, `why`, `how`).
    - **L.K.1.e:** Use the most frequently occurring prepositions (e.g., `to`, `from`, `in`, `out`, `on`, `off`, `for`, `of`, `by`, `with`).
    - **L.K.1.f:** Produce and expand complete sentences in shared language activities.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="L.K.2 sub-points: capitalization, punctuation, and spelling">
    - **L.K.2.a:** Capitalize the first word in a sentence and the pronoun `I`.
    - **L.K.2.b:** Recognize and name end punctuation.
    - **L.K.2.c:** Write a letter or letters for most consonant and short-vowel sounds (phonemes).
    - **L.K.2.d:** Spell simple words phonetically, drawing on knowledge of sound-letter relationships.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="L.K.4 sub-points: word meaning">
    - **L.K.4.a:** Identify new meanings for familiar words and apply them accurately (e.g., knowing `duck` is a bird and learning the verb `to duck`).
    - **L.K.4.b:** Use the most frequently occurring inflections and affixes (e.g., `-ed`, `-s`, `re-`, `un-`, `pre-`, `-ful`, `-less`) as a clue to the meaning of an unknown word.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="L.K.5 sub-points: word relationships">
    - **L.K.5.a:** Sort common objects into categories (e.g., shapes, foods) to gain a sense of the concepts the categories represent.
    - **L.K.5.b:** Demonstrate understanding of frequently occurring verbs and adjectives by relating them to their opposites (antonyms).
    - **L.K.5.c:** Identify real-life connections between words and their use (e.g., note places at school that are colorful).
    - **L.K.5.d:** Distinguish shades of meaning among verbs describing the same general action (e.g., `walk`, `march`, `strut`, `prance`) by acting out the meanings.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Classroom application

Most kindergarten reading lessons run as read-alouds. Students are not decoding on their own yet, so the teacher carries the lift: reading aloud, pointing to words, asking who-and-what questions, and writing the class's retelling on chart paper.

A daily phonics block sits next to reading. Ten or fifteen minutes on letter sounds, rhyming, blending, and segmenting, with `RF.K.1`-`RF.K.3` as the spine. By spring, most students are reading a small set of decodable and high-frequency words, what `RF.K.4` calls "emergent-reader texts."

Writing in kindergarten lives on the same page as drawing. A `W.K.1` opinion piece is often a picture of a favorite book with a sentence dictated to the teacher and a few words the child wrote alone. Kindergarten rubrics are short. Did the child name the topic? Did they say something about it? Did they put their thinking on the page in some mix of drawing, dictation, and writing?

Speaking and Listening shows up in morning meeting and partner talk. The daily targets are `SL.K.1.a` (taking turns) and `SL.K.1.b` (keeping a conversation going).

## Common teacher questions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Do kindergartners need to read on their own by the end of the year?">
    Yes, but only short, decodable texts. `RF.K.4` asks students to "read emergent-reader texts with purpose and understanding." That is the floor, not full independent reading of trade books. Most of the year's reading still happens through read-alouds and shared reading, where the teacher reads and the students follow along.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What does 'with prompting and support' mean for my lesson planning?">
    It means the standard is met when a student does the thinking with the teacher's scaffolding. You can ask the question, point to the picture, model the sentence stem, or write the response on the board, and the student still meets the standard by participating in the thinking. By grade 2 and grade 3, that phrase falls off most standards and students do the work on their own.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
